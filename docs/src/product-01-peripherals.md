# Metapedal Peripherals

This document covers the peripheral ecosystem that plugs into Metapedal modules through the M8 Smart Digital ports and other peripheral interfaces. Peripherals include the contact mic breakout, the passive footswitch breakout, the tile-based modular control surface, the rotational foot control, the pin header escape hatch for hacker modifications, external displays over USB-C, and USB MIDI controllers for synthesis use cases.

The audience is people designing peripheral hardware for the platform.

For the motherboard connector specifications that peripherals plug into, see the core technical document.

## The Contact Mic Breakout Peripheral

A natural v0 peripheral worth specifying explicitly is a contact mic breakout: a small board hosting an array of piezo transducer inputs that turn any physical surface into a percussion controller. This complements the on-module IMU tap detection by extending the platform's percussion capabilities beyond what the module's own case can do, while sharing the routing-graph integration. The IMU lets users tap on the module; the contact mic breakout lets users hit anything they want to make percussive.

The use case is what we have started calling rogue drumming: attaching contact mics to whatever happens to be around (park benches, dumpsters, construction site materials, found objects, building HVAC systems, anything that produces a percussive sound when struck) and using the resulting hits as musical input. The breakout captures the audio from each contact mic, processes it for trigger detection, and routes the resulting events through the chain. This is the experimental percussion tradition (Stomp, the various found-object percussion ensembles, Tom Waits's later albums) made portable and platform-integrated.

The breakout's electronics: six to eight TS quarter-inch input jacks, each with a high-impedance buffer stage appropriate for piezo elements. A multi-channel audio codec chip (similar to but smaller than what the B variants use, perhaps something like the Cirrus Logic CS5343 family or the TI PCM1802 for cost reduction) sampling at 48 kHz and 16 to 24 bits per channel. A small microcontroller (an STM32G or similar low-end part, costing $2-3) handling per-channel threshold detection, envelope following, and the routing-graph protocol over the M8 Smart Digital connection back to the parent module. LED indicators per channel showing when each contact mic is registering hits. Optional trimpots per channel for hardware-level threshold and gain adjustment, though firmware-level adjustment through the parent module's UI is the primary control mechanism.

The trigger detection produces two kinds of output that the routing graph can consume independently. Each channel generates timestamped trigger events with velocity information (the impact magnitude derived from the peak amplitude of the hit), which flow as typed events through the routing graph the same way IMU taps and footswitch presses do. The trigger events can drive sample playback, gate modulation, or any other event-consuming parameter in the chain. In parallel, each channel also produces continuous audio that can flow through the routing graph as a typed audio stream, enabling effects processing of the percussion sounds themselves (reverb on the kick, EQ on the snare, distortion on found-object surfaces).

A few thoughtful features extend the basic capability. A "learn mode" where the user hits each surface a few times and the system auto-calibrates the threshold and gain for that channel based on the natural hit dynamics. Cross-trigger rejection so a hit on one channel does not falsely trigger adjacent channels through mechanical coupling, which is a well-understood problem in commercial drum trigger design with mature solutions. Configurable trigger characteristics per channel (sharp transient detection for snare-like surfaces versus slower envelope detection for cymbal-like surfaces).

The BOM cost is modest: eight TS jacks ($0.50 each, $4 total), the multi-channel codec ($3-5), the microcontroller ($2-3), the M8 connector ($2-3), the indicator LEDs ($0.50), the small enclosure ($3-5), the PCB ($2-3), passives and small parts ($2-3). Total perhaps $20-30 in components, retailing at $50-80 once assembled and packaged. This is competitive with commercial drum trigger interfaces of comparable channel counts and substantially cheaper than dedicated drum modules with comparable capabilities.

The peripheral is a v0 product because the engineering is well-understood, the use case is clear, and it demonstrates the platform's broader vision in a compelling way. A percussionist with a Metapedal A module and the contact mic breakout has a portable percussion rig that fits in a small bag and that produces music from whatever surfaces happen to be available. The platform's flexibility shows up immediately: the percussionist can also use their A module's IMU as additional tap inputs, can route the contact mic signals through DSP effects in any B variant they also own, can record the percussion performance to microSD for later use, and can integrate the percussion rig with the rest of their gear through the inter-module bus. The breakout is one piece of a broader system that scales with the user's other Metapedal investments.

## The Passive Footswitch Breakout

Another natural v0 peripheral worth specifying explicitly is a passive footswitch breakout: a small metal enclosure with four (or eight, in a larger variant) momentary footswitches connected through the M8 Smart Digital port to a parent module. The breakout serves users who want more footswitch real estate than their module's single onboard footswitch provides, without paying for additional full modules.

The architectural elegance of this peripheral is that the parent module's M8 Smart Digital port and the routing graph do all the heavy lifting; the breakout itself is essentially passive. Each footswitch on the breakout is a momentary mechanical switch wired to a GPIO line. The parent module reads the GPIO states and routes each switch's press events through the routing graph to whatever parameter or event the user has assigned it to. The user gets four independently mappable footswitches that behave identically to having four module-onboard footswitches, configured through the same routing-graph mechanism that maps any other event source.

The implementation has one small refinement worth noting. A purely passive breakout would consume four GPIO pins on the M8 connector for four footswitches, plus power and ground, which is six of the M8 Smart Digital port's eight pins. Adding optional indicator LEDs (one per switch, to show current state of whatever parameter the switch controls) would consume another four pins for a total of ten, exceeding the M8 budget. The solution is a small I2C GPIO expander chip (TI PCA9554 or Microchip MCP23008, $0.50-1) on the breakout that provides 8 GPIOs over a 2-wire I2C interface, with the M8 port carrying I2C plus power. The breakout supports four switches and four LEDs (or up to eight of either) without consuming all the M8 pins, and the I2C interface leaves headroom for additional signaling if needed. This adds one chip and a couple of passives to the breakout, bumping the BOM up by maybe two dollars, but vastly increases the signaling flexibility.

So strictly speaking the breakout is not purely passive; it has one tiny IC for GPIO expansion. From the user's perspective it remains a passive peripheral: no firmware to flash, no setup to do, no microcontroller to keep updated, no software configuration of the breakout itself. The breakout plugs in, the parent module recognizes it as a footswitch breakout through I2C device-class detection, and the user configures the routing graph for each switch through the parent module's UI. The breakout has no state of its own.

The BOM cost is excellent. Four quality momentary footswitches (3PDT or DPDT mechanical parts comparable to what commercial pedals use, $2-3 each, $8-12 total). Four optional indicator LEDs ($0.50). The I2C GPIO expander ($0.50-1). The M8 connector ($2-3). A small metal enclosure ($5-10). The PCB ($1-2). Cabling between the enclosure and the M8 connector ($1-2). Passives and small parts ($1-2). Total perhaps $15-25 in components, retailing at $40-70 once assembled and packaged. This is genuinely cheap for a four-footswitch external controller, competing well against dedicated MIDI footswitch boxes that traditionally serve this market at $100-200 price points.

The footswitches are momentary rather than latching because the routing graph owns all state. Latching mechanical switches have physical state that gets out of sync with the routing graph's logical state in confusing ways (the switch is physically up but the parameter is logically engaged, or vice versa, depending on how preset changes affect the routing graph state). Momentary switches send press events; the routing graph decides what each event means and tracks the resulting state. The user gets consistent behavior regardless of how the switches relate to the underlying parameters.

A pair of breakout variants serves different scale needs: a four-switch breakout in a small enclosure for users who need a few extra controls, and an eight-switch breakout in a slightly larger enclosure for users who need more. Both use the same internal architecture (I2C GPIO expander handling the signal routing); the difference is just the number of switches and the enclosure size.

The same passive-breakout pattern extends naturally to other simple input peripherals worth considering for v1 or shortly after: a knob breakout with four rotary controls connected to the GPIO expander's analog inputs (using a part with built-in ADC capability or a separate small ADC), providing continuous-parameter control. A combo breakout with two switches and two knobs for users who want mixed input types. A jack breakout with four standard quarter-inch jacks for connecting third-party expression pedals or footswitches, which converts standard pedal industry electrical conventions into the smart-digital protocol. Each of these uses essentially the same architectural pattern (passive peripheral with a tiny I2C expander chip, no firmware, no microcontroller) and would have similar cost structures.

The passive footswitch breakout is genuinely the kind of v0 product that demonstrates the platform's flexibility at the entry-level price point. A user with a single Metapedal module who wants more footswitches drops in a passive breakout for $40-70 and gets exactly that. The breakout is cheap, simple, reliable (no electronics to fail beyond the switches themselves), and makes the platform meaningfully more useful for users who want pedalboard-style control without committing to additional full modules.

## The Tile System: Modular Control Surface Specification

The passive breakout pattern (fixed-layout enclosures with a small set of controls) serves users who want a standardized form factor. A more ambitious version of the same idea is a tile system: a modular control surface specification that lets users build custom physical layouts from interchangeable tiles. The pattern is borrowed from Gridfinity (the modular storage system designed by Zack Freedman in 2022), with the key insight being that defining a small physical and electrical standard enables an ecosystem of compatible accessories to emerge.

The tile system has three architectural elements: the baseplate (the structural and electrical foundation), the tile specification (what makes a piece compatible), and the standard tile catalog (the set of officially specified tile types). Each element needs careful design because the specification becomes a long-term commitment that the ecosystem builds against.

### The Baseplate

The baseplate is a flat metal panel with a grid of mounting points and an embedded set of M8 Smart Digital ports along one edge for connection back to the parent Metapedal module. The baseplate provides:

The physical mounting structure for tiles. Each tile position has standardized fastening points (threaded inserts for permanent installations, magnets for fast-swap configurations, or both) at standardized spacing across the grid. Tiles snap into place mechanically and stay there reliably even when footswitches are stomped.

The electrical distribution path. A PCB embedded in or behind the baseplate carries power and the I2C bus from the M8 connection at one edge to each tile position. Each tile position has standardized electrical contacts (pogo pins or board-to-board connectors at the bottom of the tile) that mate with the baseplate's PCB when the tile is installed.

The connection to the parent module. The M8 Smart Digital port carries the I2C bus, power, and ground. The baseplate can be connected to any parent module's M8 port using standard M8 cabling. Multiple baseplates can be connected to a single parent module through a peripheral hub if the user has more tiles than one baseplate can hold.

The baseplate comes in several standard sizes: 2 by 2 (4 tile positions), 2 by 4 (8 positions), 4 by 4 (16 positions), and 2 by 8 (16 positions in a wider format). The aspect ratios serve different pedalboard layouts; 2 by 8 is good for users with long narrow boards, while 4 by 4 is good for users with squarer layouts. Different sizes share the same grid dimension and tile specification, so tiles work interchangeably across baseplate sizes.

The baseplate is made of metal (steel or aluminum, painted or anodized) for durability and to ground tile shells through their mounting points. The metal also provides RF shielding for the I2C signals that travel along the baseplate's internal PCB.

### The Grid Dimension

The grid spacing is a key specification commitment that affects every tile design forever. The right size is a tradeoff: too small and tiles cannot hold substantial controls (especially footswitches); too large and the tile system is wasteful for small controls like indicator LEDs.

A grid dimension of 50 millimeters by 50 millimeters is a reasonable starting point. This is large enough that a single tile can hold a substantial footswitch (commercial pedal footswitches typically need 30-40 millimeters of clearance around them for reliable foot operation) with the tile's own edge framing the switch. Small controls like LEDs or potentiometers fit comfortably on a 50 millimeter tile with room around them, though several small controls could also share a single tile in a multi-control configuration.

An alternative is 60 millimeters by 60 millimeters, which gives more clearance around footswitches at the cost of more tile real estate per control. The choice depends on physical-stomp ergonomics testing that should happen during v1 prototyping. 50 millimeters is the smaller end of what works for footswitch tiles; 60 millimeters is the more comfortable end. The specification commits to one of these or to whatever number falls out of ergonomic testing.

Tiles can also span multiple grid positions. A "double-wide" tile that occupies two adjacent grid positions can hold larger controls (a stereo expression pedal jack pair, a larger OLED, a substantial knob with more usable rotation range). The specification supports multi-grid tiles by defining how the tile's mounting and electrical connections relate to the underlying grid.

### The Tile Electrical Specification

Each tile gets standardized electrical resources from its baseplate connection: power (3.3 volts at small current limits, perhaps 50 milliamps maximum per tile), ground, and access to the I2C bus that the baseplate distributes to all tile positions. The baseplate's I2C bus has a master controller (a small microcontroller on the baseplate, perhaps an STM32G or similar low-end part costing $2-3) that handles the protocol with the parent module on one side and the individual tiles on the other.

Each tile has its own small I2C GPIO expander (PCA9554 or MCP23008, $0.50-1) that handles the tile's specific controls. The expander provides 8 GPIOs accessible over I2C; a footswitch tile uses one input GPIO and one output GPIO (for an indicator LED); a knob tile uses one analog input through a tiny ADC; an OLED tile uses I2C directly to address the OLED chip. The tile's GPIO expander has a unique I2C address (set by jumpers or by a small EEPROM with a unique ID), so multiple tiles on the same bus can be addressed independently.

The baseplate's master controller scans the I2C bus for tile presence at startup and reports the discovered tiles to the parent module. The parent module sees each tile as a distinct event source or control source in its routing graph; the user configures the routing graph to map each tile to whatever parameter or event they want. The tile system is transparent from the parent module's perspective; each tile just shows up as another addressable element in the routing graph.

### The Standard Tile Catalog

A set of officially specified tile types covers the most common control needs. v1 should include the following baseline tiles:

Footswitch tile: One quality momentary footswitch (3PDT or DPDT mechanical, $2-3) plus one optional indicator LED. The most common tile type, expected to be used in groups of 2-8 on most baseplates. BOM around $4-6, retail $8-12.

Rotary knob tile: One continuous-rotation potentiometer ($1-2) connected to a tiny ADC ($0.50), with a tactile knob ($1-2). Provides one continuous-parameter input. BOM around $3-5, retail $6-10.

Indicator LED tile: One or two indicator LEDs in a small tile, useful for showing routing-graph state when the user wants a dedicated visual indicator without a footswitch. BOM around $1, retail $3-5.

OLED tile: A small 0.96 inch monochrome OLED ($2-3) on a single tile, useful for showing preset names, parameter values, or chain status. BOM around $4-6, retail $10-15.

Expression pedal jack tile: One quarter-inch TRS jack ($1) with the appropriate signal conditioning circuitry to connect a third-party expression pedal. The tile converts standard pedal industry electrical conventions into the smart-digital protocol that the routing graph consumes. BOM around $2-3, retail $5-8.

Tap detection tile: A flat metal panel that the user can tap or hit with a drumstick, with a piezo transducer underneath that detects taps. Useful as a percussion input integrated into the control surface. BOM around $3-5, retail $8-12.

Capacitive proximity sensor tile: A capacitive touch sensor exposed on the tile surface, useful for gestural inputs (sliding a finger across, hovering, tapping with no mechanical force). BOM around $2-3, retail $6-10.

The v1 catalog covers the most common cases. v2 and beyond expand the catalog with specialty tiles based on user demand and community contribution.

### The Open Specification

The tile specification is open from day one, which means third-party manufacturers can produce conforming tiles without licensing fees or special parts access, and hobbyists can produce one-off tiles using their own 3D printers and basic electronics skills. The specification documents:

The physical dimensions: grid spacing, tile height, mounting point positions, electrical contact positions.

The electrical interface: I2C bus specification, power and current limits, addressing scheme, device-class identification for the master controller to recognize tile types.

The mechanical mounting: how tiles attach to the baseplate (magnets, threaded inserts, or both, with specific positions and force requirements), how tiles resist accidental dislodging during stomping.

The tile manifest format: how a tile identifies itself to the baseplate's master controller, what capabilities it advertises, what routing-graph integration it requests.

Reference implementations: example tile designs that demonstrate the specification, with schematic, PCB layout, and bill of materials for each official tile type. These serve as starting points for third-party tile designs.

The specification is licensed under terms that allow commercial use without royalties (something like CERN-OHL-W or similar permissive open hardware license), so manufacturers can produce conforming tiles for commercial sale without legal complications. The Metapedal project itself produces and sells the official tiles; third parties produce and sell whatever variations they want; users can produce their own for personal use. The ecosystem grows organically through the contributions of all three groups.

The tile system is a v1 commitment because the specification needs to ship with the platform; adding tile support later would require retrofitting baseplate detection into the parent module firmware in ways that might break compatibility with v1 modules. By committing in v1 the platform has a richer initial product offering and demonstrates the open-ecosystem philosophy in concrete form.

## The Rotational Foot Control: Position-Based Continuous Input

A genuinely new kind of foot-operated control worth specifying explicitly is a rotational foot pedal that uses angular position rather than rocker-style force to produce continuous control values. This addresses a real ergonomic limitation of traditional expression pedals that has not been well-served by existing commercial gear, and it opens up musical expression possibilities that traditional pedals struggle with.

### The Problem with Traditional Expression Pedals

Traditional expression pedals are foot-operated rockers that rotate around an axis at the heel, with the user pressing with their toe to rock the pedal forward and back. A potentiometer or optical sensor measures the rocker angle, producing the control value. This is functional but has a real ergonomic limitation: the gesture is force-controlled rather than position-controlled. The user's foot has to provide both the force to overcome the pedal's spring tension and the precision of positioning at the same time. Fine control is hard because human leg muscles are not designed for high-precision force modulation, especially when the user is simultaneously playing an instrument that demands attention.

The keyboard world has been navigating this tradeoff for decades. Pitch bend wheels are spring-loaded and return to center, providing momentary deviation that snaps back; modulation wheels are not spring-loaded and stay where the user leaves them, providing persistent settings. Both types of control are position-based rather than force-based; the user's hand sets the wheel to a specific angle and the wheel stays there (or returns) without requiring continuous force. This produces much finer control than a force-controlled equivalent would, which is why keyboards have wheels rather than pressure-sensitive plates.

### The Rotational Foot Control

The rotational foot pedal applies the wheel paradigm to foot control. A flat or slightly curved foot platform rotates around a vertical axis at the center, with the user pivoting their foot left or right to change the rotation angle. The foot rests on the platform; the ankle's natural articulation provides the gesture. The user does not have to overcome continuous spring tension to maintain a position; they just rest their foot at the angle they want, and the platform stays there or returns based on whether springs are installed.

Two variants serve different musical purposes, mirroring the keyboard pitch-bend versus modulation-wheel distinction:

The spring-loaded variant returns to center when the foot lifts, providing momentary deviation that snaps back. Good for pitch bends, vibrato, wah-style modulation, or any effect where the user wants temporary deviation from a neutral state. Spring tension can be tunable for users who want different feels (lighter for fast gestures, heavier for stability).

The non-spring variant stays at whatever position the user leaves it, providing persistent settings the user can establish and walk away from. Good for volume swells, filter sweeps, parameter snapshots that hold until changed. The user kicks the surface to a position and the parameter stays there until kicked elsewhere.

The two variants use the same underlying mechanism (the rotating foot platform) with springs as an optional element. The platform's mechanical design supports both configurations through user-installable spring kits, similar to how some commercial pedals offer tunable spring tension. A single physical product can be either variant based on how it is configured.

### The Mechanical Design

The rotation axis is vertical at the center of the foot platform. The platform pivots in place rather than translating across the pedalboard, so the user's foot stays in roughly the same location while the angle changes. The rotation range is plus or minus 30-45 degrees from center, which is comfortable for ankle articulation without straining the user's leg. The exact range benefits from prototyping with real users.

The foot platform is 10-12 centimeters across, large enough for a foot to rest comfortably without awkward overhang. The surface is slightly textured or rubberized to prevent the foot from slipping during the pivot motion. A slight concave shape can cup the foot for stability; a slight convex shape allows the foot to rock as well as rotate, providing additional expressive dimension. The final shape benefits from ergonomic testing with diverse foot sizes and playing styles.

The rotation mechanism uses a thrust bearing at the rotation axis to handle the downward load of the user's foot weight (including the impact of a stomp from above) while providing smooth rotation. A standard ball-bearing thrust assembly handles the load, and the rotation has minimal stiction so small foot movements translate cleanly into rotation changes. The assembly is substantial enough to handle pedal-grade abuse.

For the spring-loaded variant, torsion springs at the rotation axis provide the centering force. The spring tension is set to require maybe 1-3 newtons of foot pressure to displace from center, which is light enough for fine control but firm enough to feel stable. Springs are accessible through the bottom of the assembly for users who want to swap them for different tension preferences, or to remove them entirely for the non-spring variant.

### The Angular Position Sensor

The angle measurement uses a magnetic absolute position encoder. Parts like the AMS AS5048 or AS5600 ($5-10 in production quantities) read the rotation of a small magnet attached to the rotating shaft, providing 12 to 14 bits of absolute position over a 360 degree range. The encoder chip mounts to the stationary part of the assembly, the magnet attaches to the rotating part, and the assembly has no electrical contacts to wear out. The absolute position aspect is important because the system always knows the current angle without needing to home or calibrate, even after the rig has been powered off and on or after the user's foot has moved the platform during transport.

12-bit resolution gives 4096 distinct positions across the full 360-degree encoder range. Mapping this to the useful plus or minus 45-degree range gives approximately 1000 distinct positions across that range, which is more than enough for musical control. The user gets effectively continuous control with no audible quantization steps.

### The Form Factor Options

The rotational foot control can be packaged in several forms:

As a tile in the tile system, occupying two adjacent grid positions to give the foot enough room for the platform. The tile has the rotating platform on top, the encoder and supporting electronics underneath, and connects through the standard tile electrical interface. Total BOM around $25-35, retail $50-80. The tile form lets users mix rotational controls with footswitches and other tile types in a single integrated baseplate.

As a standalone peripheral that connects through the M8 Smart Digital port, similar to the contact mic breakout and the passive footswitch breakout. The peripheral has its own enclosure, the rotating platform, and the supporting electronics. Total BOM around $30-45, retail $60-120. The standalone form is good for users who want a fixed dedicated pedal that does this one thing.

As a tile-system baseplate occupying multiple grid positions integrated with the baseplate, for users who want an entire baseplate organized around rotational input plus a few supporting footswitches. This is a more specialized form factor for users who really want to lean into rotational control.

The tile form is probably the right primary v1 product because it integrates with the broader tile system the platform already commits to. The standalone form might come as a secondary product for users who do not buy the tile system.

### Musical Use Cases

The rotational foot control enables musical expression that traditional pedals struggle with. A guitarist assigns the spring-loaded variant to wah frequency, getting wah-pedal-like behavior but with substantially finer control than a traditional rocker-style wah. The same control assigned to pitch shift produces whammy-bar-style pitch modulation with very precise control over the bend amount. Assigned to a filter cutoff with the non-spring variant, the control sets a filter setting that persists; the user pivots their foot to change the filter, takes their foot off, and the filter stays at the new setting until they pivot again.

A particularly interesting application is two rotational controls used together for X-Y parameter spaces. The user has two rotational pedals, one for each foot, with one controlling filter cutoff and the other controlling filter resonance. The user's foot positions traverse a 2D space of filter settings, with fine control in both dimensions. This kind of expressive control is essentially impossible with traditional pedal arrangements but becomes natural with rotational input.

The position-based control model is also compatible with the platform's routing graph, where the rotational input is just another typed event source that maps to whatever parameter the user wants. The user can assign one rotational pedal to drive multiple parameters simultaneously (filter cutoff plus reverb size plus delay feedback, all moving together as the foot rotates), or split the rotation range into segments that drive different parameters (the first 30 degrees of rotation drives filter cutoff, the next 30 drives resonance), or do any number of other routing tricks that the routing graph supports.

The rotational foot control is a genuinely new kind of input that the platform supports, distinct from the rocker-style expression pedal that has dominated this corner of the pedal market for decades. The keyboard world has long understood that position-based wheels give finer control than force-based alternatives; bringing the same insight to pedalboard control is overdue. The platform's modular architecture makes this kind of new control surface a natural fit; the routing graph treats it as just another continuous-parameter input, and the user can use it wherever continuous control is desired.

## The Pin Header Escape Hatch

Inside the metal box, beyond the user-facing connectors, the standard motherboard exposes a pad array of some twenty to forty pads at 0.1-inch pitch. This is the GPIO escape hatch for hackers, similar in spirit to the Raspberry Pi GPIO header. It provides direct access to the microcontroller's general purpose input/output pins, to its I2C and SPI buses, to three-point-three-volt power rails, and to other useful signals.

The pinout is specified tightly. A consistent pinout across all motherboards, across all manufacturers, across all generations, is what enables an ecosystem of add-on boards and breakout modules. The pinout uses a single logic voltage of three-point-three volts to avoid the damage-causing voltage-mixing problems that plagued early Raspberry Pi accessories. Power rails are exposed with defined current limits so that add-on boards know what they can draw without designing in their own regulators.

The pad array is unpopulated by default, with no header installed at the factory. Users who want to use the escape hatch install whatever connector or wiring they prefer. This default is the right choice for several reasons. The first is electromagnetic compatibility: a populated pin header is an array of small unterminated metal stubs sticking up from the board, each connected to a signal trace and each acting as a small antenna at the frequencies the H7 operates at (the SAI bus runs at a few MHz, the SDMMC at tens of MHz, USB at 12 or 480 MHz). These stubs both radiate from the board (potentially failing EMC testing) and pick up environmental noise that gets onto the signals. The unpopulated pad array does not have this problem because the pads are flush with the board.

The second reason is short-circuit risk inside the enclosure. The 0.1-inch pin spacing is wide compared to modern surface-mount work but still close enough that conductive debris, a dropped screwdriver, a stray strand of wire, or a curious finger can bridge two pins easily. Inside a closed enclosure with the rest of the electronics, an accidental short on a populated header could damage the H7's GPIOs or the power rails. Flush pads are much less likely to cause this kind of problem because they are not protruding.

The third reason is that users who want to use the escape hatch get more flexibility with bare pads than with a pre-installed header. They can solder wires directly to the pads for compact permanent connections, route the wires however their project requires. They can install through-hole components directly into the pad holes for inline expansion (a temperature sensor's leads going through the holes, soldered in place). They can install whatever connector style fits their project: standard 0.1-inch headers, JST connectors, Molex KK series, 1.27 mm IDC headers, or specialized parts. They can populate only the specific pins they need, leaving the rest as bare pads. This matches the kind of user who would actually be using the escape hatch: someone with soldering skill and a specific modification in mind, who values flexibility over convenience.

The cost savings are minor (a 0.1-inch through-hole header is cents per part, and the assembly avoids one through-hole soldering step on an otherwise surface-mount-only board), but they accumulate at scale and flow through to a slightly lower module price for users who do not use the escape hatch.

There is an accessibility trade-off worth being explicit about: users who lack soldering skill cannot use the escape hatch on the bare board. They need either to install a header themselves (which requires the same soldering skill), to buy a separately-sold header kit from the project or from third parties (which the user would install with help from a friend or local repair shop), or to use the M8 Smart Digital port for capabilities that the escape hatch would otherwise provide. The platform deliberately keeps the escape hatch accessible only to users who have the skill to use it safely, which is the same target audience the escape hatch is meant to serve.

The pad array is inside the enclosure rather than externally accessible, for protection during normal use. Add-on boards plug in during initial setup and stay connected, not hot-swapped during operation. This is similar to how Raspberry Pi hats work and is the right tradeoff for the use case. Even if a user installs a header on the pad array, that header is only accessible when the enclosure is open.

The pad array is the place where someone can build a Frankenstein pedal with sixteen buttons on it, or a custom sensor array, or whatever else they imagine. They remove the motherboard from its standard case, mount it on a custom panel, and connect their custom hardware to the escape hatch. The standard module's identity is lost but the standard module's capabilities are preserved, because the motherboard and its firmware still work normally.

The Smart Digital M8 port, exposed externally, serves a related but different purpose. The escape hatch is for permanent additions and breakouts that become part of a module. The Smart Digital port is for user-friendly peripherals that plug in and out as the user reconfigures their gear. Both have their place. The escape hatch is for the hacker who wants to extend a module's capabilities. The Smart Digital port is for the user who wants to plug in an expression pedal or a Bluetooth dongle.

## External Displays Over USB-C

The platform supports external displays through two distinct paths depending on the use case: a data-channel path for interactive displays where the client device renders the UI, and a server-rendered path for passive displays that show video output from the rig.

The data-channel path is the architecturally clean one and handles the most important use case (the touchscreen mixer described in the vision document). The rig sends parameter values, level data, and chain state as small numeric messages over a USB 2.0 data channel, and the touch device runs a Metapedal companion application that renders the mixer UI client-side from this data. The bandwidth is tiny: a few kilobytes per second for the entire stream of meter updates and parameter changes. The touch device does all rendering work, leveraging whatever GPU capability it has. The rig is not in the video path at all. This path uses USB 2.0 high-speed comfortably, requires no DisplayPort source silicon on the rig, and produces a richer user experience than rendered-video would because the touch device can do smooth 60 Hz animations that a server-rendered approach could not match.

The bandwidth math for the data-channel path is striking. Mixer state updates are small: six channels of level data at 100 samples per second per channel is 600 floats per second, perhaps 2.4 kilobytes per second. Parameter changes are even smaller and event-driven (a fader moves, a single message goes; otherwise nothing). The full state-of-the-rig stream including levels, parameters, scene status, and recording status fits well under 10 kilobytes per second. This is the data flow of a status terminal, not a video stream.

The server-rendered path is for use cases where the receiving device cannot run client-side rendering: a passive monitor mounted on the side of a venue showing chain status, a display in a band's rehearsal space showing preset names and tuning information, an audience-facing visualization showing the rig's audio activity. For these cases, the rig has to produce video, but the bandwidth and processor requirements depend on what the video contains.

Uncompressed full HD at 60 Hz is 1920 × 1080 × 60 × 24 bits = roughly 3 gigabits per second of raw pixel data. This is far beyond what USB 2.0 high-speed can carry. Driving uncompressed FHD video over USB requires USB 3 (5 gigabits per second SuperSpeed or higher), which the H7 does not natively support. Adding USB 3 to the platform for this use case would require either a different microcontroller family with USB 3 peripherals or an external USB 3 controller chip, both of which are substantial architectural changes. So the platform deliberately does not support uncompressed FHD video output from the rig.

What the platform can support, given the H7's capabilities, is more modest server-rendered video. The H7's LTDC (LCD-TFT Display Controller) peripheral produces parallel RGB output suitable for driving an LCD panel directly or through a parallel-to-DisplayPort bridge chip. The bridge chip handles the protocol conversion to standard DisplayPort signaling, while the H7 produces the RGB output it already produces for its own OLED. This path supports modest resolutions (perhaps up to 1280×720 at 60 Hz depending on the H7's LTDC configuration and the bridge chip's capabilities) and is appropriate for status displays and simple visualizations. The cost is the bridge chip ($5-15 depending on capability) plus the additional USB-C mux for routing the DisplayPort signaling onto the right pins of the USB-C connector.

A compressed video path could in principle support FHD 60 Hz over USB 2.0 high-speed by encoding the video with H.264 or H.265 before transmission. Modern codecs can compress FHD 60 Hz to 5-50 megabits per second depending on quality, comfortably within the 480 megabit per second USB 2.0 high-speed bandwidth. The challenge is that compression requires substantial compute: a real-time H.264 encoder for FHD 60 Hz needs hardware acceleration in practice, which the H7 does not have. Software encoding on the H7 would consume most of the chip's compute budget for the encoding alone, leaving little for the audio processing the platform actually exists to do. So the compressed video path is not a good fit for the H7-based architecture.

The practical conclusion is that the platform supports data-channel displays for interactive use (the primary use case, well-served by USB 2.0 high-speed and the client-side rendering approach) and modest server-rendered displays for passive status output (driven through the H7's LTDC and a parallel-to-DisplayPort bridge chip). Use cases that require uncompressed high-resolution video from the rig (audience visualizations, video production output) are deliberately not in scope; users who want these capabilities can plug a laptop into one of the rig's USB-C ports, receive the data-channel stream, and run their preferred visualization or video production software on the laptop. The laptop drives the display. The rig provides the data. This is the same architectural pattern as the touchscreen mixer, generalized to whatever video output the user wants to produce.

A future peripheral worth flagging here even though it is not v1 scope: a graphics-accelerator breakout that connects through the M8 Smart Digital port and drives a high-bandwidth external display from the data-channel stream. The breakout combines a graphics accelerator chip (Bridgetek BT815 or similar, $10-15) with a small microcontroller that translates the data-channel stream into rendering commands. The breakout becomes a Metapedal-specific "video card" without affecting the rig's architecture, because the rig is still only providing the data flow it already provides; the breakout consumes that flow and renders it as video. This pattern is the right way to add high-bandwidth video output to the platform later without bloating the standard module's BOM with capabilities most users do not need. The v1 commitment that supports this future peripheral is that the data-channel stream is well-specified and complete enough to drive any reasonable consumer, not just the touchscreen mixer UI: per-channel audio levels, per-channel spectrum data, current preset names and parameter values, chain configuration, recording status, and any other chain-wide state that an external presentation layer might need.

USB role negotiation matters for the display use case. The same USB-C port on a module needs to act as a USB device when plugged into a host computer (for the audio interface use case), as a USB host or DisplayPort source when a display is plugged in, and as the inter-module bus when another module is plugged in. USB-C supports role swapping through the USB Power Delivery protocol, but the implementation is non-trivial. The module needs to detect what is on the other end of the cable, negotiate the appropriate role, and adjust its USB stack accordingly. The role negotiation logic is shared across use cases; the same code handles "what is this device" detection regardless of whether the answer is a computer, a display, or another module.

Which module hosts the display when multiple modules are present is a clean question with a clean answer. Any module can host the display, but only one at a time, and the user picks which by plugging the display into the module they want to be the host. The hosting module fetches the chain-wide state from the inter-module bus, sends the data-channel stream out the USB-C port to the client device (or renders the status display via LTDC for a passive monitor), and accepts user input to send configuration changes back to the other modules. The other modules are unaware that the display exists; they just see configuration commands coming through the bus like any other configuration changes.

## USB MIDI Controllers and the Synthesizer Use Case

The USB host capability that enables external displays also enables an entirely different use case: plugging a USB MIDI controller into the module and running a software-defined synthesizer locally. A user plugs an inexpensive USB MIDI keyboard into one of the module's USB-C ports, the module enumerates it as a USB MIDI device, and the keyboard's MIDI events flow into the routing graph as control events of the same type the dedicated MIDI peripheral produces. The routing graph cannot tell whether MIDI events arrived through USB or through the TRRS MIDI jack; they are just MIDI events flowing into the chain.

The synthesizer itself is a DSP algorithm running on the module's H7 microcontroller. In the typed-port model, this is a module with a MIDI input port and an audio output port. The DSP runs continuously, generating audio in response to MIDI events as they arrive. This is fundamentally the same architecture as any other DSP module in the platform, just with a different input type. The routing graph handles the wiring naturally, and the audio output goes wherever the user has configured it to go: through the quarter-inch jack on a B variant, through the headphone jack for monitoring, or through the inter-module bus to other modules for further processing.

The specific case of DX7 emulation is feasible on the H7 and worth being explicit about because it is a compelling product proposition. The Yamaha DX7 is one of the most iconic synthesizers ever made, with sounds that defined an entire era of pop music. Its six-operator FM synthesis and thirty-two algorithms produce a wide range of sounds that no other synthesis technique replicates exactly. The Dexed project is the canonical open-source DX7 emulation, GPL-licensed and actively maintained, and it has been successfully ported to embedded microcontrollers in the MicroDexed and MiniDexed projects.

The performance numbers from the existing ports give a clear feasibility picture. On the Teensy 4.0, a Cortex-M7 running at 600 megahertz, MicroDexed runs 16 voices of DX7 emulation with all effects active at approximately 50 percent CPU usage. The STM32H7 at 480 megahertz is somewhat slower but in the same general performance class. Running full 16-voice DX7 emulation on a Metapedal B variant is well within the H7's capability, with headroom remaining for additional effects in the chain.

This makes the controller-keyboard-plus-synthesizer use case a real product configuration. The user plugs a cheap USB MIDI keyboard into the Metapedal module, the module runs the DX7 emulation locally, and the synthesized audio comes out through whatever destination is configured. No additional synthesizer hardware is needed. The Metapedal module becomes a complete DX7 in a small box, with the user supplying the keyboard.

The economics for users are favorable. A cheap entry-level USB MIDI controller like an Akai MPK Mini or a Korg microKEY runs perhaps fifty to a hundred dollars new and much less used. These typically have 25 or 32 keys with velocity sensitivity, a few knobs and pads, and USB MIDI output. They are designed precisely for the use case of being an input device that plugs into something else that generates sound. The combination of one of these controllers and a Metapedal B variant module gives the user a complete DX7 emulation for substantially less than the cost of a used original DX7, with better physical form factor and full Metapedal integration.

The DX7 patch library is enormous and easily accessible. The original Yamaha factory patches define a specific era of sounds. Thousands of third-party and community patches have accumulated over decades. All of these use the standard Yamaha SysEx voice dump format, which Dexed already handles. So the Metapedal synthesizer module can load patches in this standard format, giving users access to the full ecosystem of existing DX7 patches without any custom format conversion.

Detailed patch editing is the natural use case for the external configuration interfaces. The DX7 has dozens of parameters per voice, far too many to edit comfortably on a small OLED with three buttons. The phone app over Bluetooth, or a USB-C touchscreen plugged into the module, gives a much better interface for the kind of deep menu diving that patch design requires. Live performance uses the controller's existing physical knobs, mod wheel, and pitch bend mapped through the routing graph to specific synth parameters, giving expressive control without diving into menus.

The MIDI controller's continuous controllers, like the modulation wheel and any assignable knobs the controller provides, become control sources in the routing graph. The mod wheel typically controls vibrato depth or filter cutoff depending on the patch. The pitch bend wheel controls pitch. Aftertouch, where the controller supports it, can modulate any parameter the user wants. The routing graph handles all of this through the same mappings infrastructure that handles every other control signal in the platform.

The DX7 is not the only synthesizer that can run on the platform. Any synthesis algorithm that takes MIDI events as input and produces audio as output works. Subtractive synthesis with virtual analog oscillators and filters, wavetable synthesis, physical modeling for specific instruments, granular synthesis, additive synthesis, all are feasible on the H7 with appropriate algorithms. The DX7 is a specific choice that fits the H7's capabilities well and has cultural significance, but the platform supports any synthesis algorithm that fits the available compute budget. Users can switch between synthesizer types the same way they switch between effects, by loading different presets.

A particularly elegant example of the platform's decoupling story is Hammond B3 emulation with Leslie speaker simulation. The Hammond B3 is fundamentally an additive synthesizer with nine sine-wave oscillators per note mixed at adjustable levels via the famous drawbars. The combination of drawbar settings determines the timbre, with classic notations like "888000000" describing a specific configuration. The CPU budget for the Hammond synthesis itself is modest: nine sine oscillators per note, sixteen voices of polyphony, plus percussion harmonics and key click, fits comfortably in a small fraction of the H7's available cycles.

The Leslie speaker emulation is where the more interesting DSP work lives. The original Leslie is a rotating speaker cabinet with two rotors whose movement creates Doppler effect on the sound, modulating both pitch and amplitude. Emulating this in DSP requires modeling the Doppler effect with delay-line modulation, modeling the amplitude modulation from rotating directional pickup patterns, modeling the frequency-dependent radiation patterns of the horn and drum, and modeling the physical inertia of the rotors during speed transitions. Well-established algorithms for all of this exist and fit comfortably in a few percent of the H7's capacity. The combined Hammond plus Leslie emulation is well within budget with substantial headroom for additional effects.

The drawbar interface is the most interesting decoupling story. On a real B3, the nine drawbars are physical sliders that take up significant panel space on the console. Replicating these directly on a small Metapedal module is impossible if you take the physical interface literally. But the platform's decoupling premise makes the question of where the drawbars live entirely separable from where the synthesis runs. The synthesis runs on the H7. The drawbars are nine continuous control inputs that can come from anywhere: nine sliders on a touchscreen connected through USB-C, the assignable knobs on a MIDI keyboard mapped through the routing graph, an external controller surface, or any other source the user prefers.

A dedicated Metapedal peripheral that is just nine sliders, designed to look and feel like the drawbars on a real B3, plugged into a host module through the inter-module bus, would be a complete drawbar controller surface. It does no audio processing; it just produces nine continuous control values that flow into the routing graph. This generalizes nicely to other classic instruments: a Wurlitzer with its tremolo speed knob, a Clavinet with its pickup selection switches, a Moog with its filter cutoff and resonance knobs. Each instrument has characteristic performance controls that the user wants direct access to during performance, and Metapedal can host dedicated controller surfaces for each one. The same Metapedal motherboard with different front panel layouts becomes a family of instrument-specific controller surfaces.

The Leslie speed control is similarly a decoupled control input. On the original Leslie, the speed switch toggles between slow chorale and fast tremolo modes, with the actual rotor speeds transitioning continuously because of physical inertia. In Metapedal, the speed control is just another control input. The traditional footswitch can be a footswitch on any Metapedal module. A continuous control comes from an expression pedal or a knob. Exotic mappings work too, like having the speed follow the keyboard's volume envelope so the rotor speeds up during louder passages and slows down during quieter ones automatically. The user picks whatever control interface fits their performance style and the routing graph wires it to the Leslie speed parameter.

The complete classic organ rig is a USB MIDI keyboard, a Metapedal B variant running Hammond plus Leslie emulation, and whatever drawbar interface the user prefers. Total cost is perhaps two hundred dollars including the keyboard. A real B3-with-Leslie rig costs thousands of dollars, weighs hundreds of pounds, and requires a truck to transport. The traditional rig has character that emulation does not fully capture, but for most performance contexts the convenience advantage of the platform-based approach is substantial.

This also opens the platform to a different user community than guitarists. Keyboardists have different gear, different performance conventions, and different aesthetic preferences than guitarists. The architectural decisions made for guitarists, like the footswitch on top of every module and the metal pedal aesthetic, are not perfect for keyboardists but work well enough that the platform is usable for both. A keyboardist's Metapedal setup might place modules on a tabletop next to the keyboard rather than on a pedalboard on the floor, with footswitches used less frequently than keyboard-attached controls. The platform supports both styles without compromise, broadening the addressable market beyond what a guitar-only platform could reach.

The synthesizer use case fits cleanly with the portable busker case discussed earlier. A musician singing into a microphone, playing a USB MIDI keyboard plugged into a Metapedal module, with the synthesized sound mixed with the vocal and sent to a speaker, is a complete one-person performance rig. Total equipment is the Metapedal module, the battery pack, a small MIDI controller, a microphone, and a speaker. Everything fits in a backpack.

There is a topology consideration worth flagging. The USB-C port being used for the MIDI controller is one of the inter-module ports. A user who wants to chain multiple modules and also use a USB MIDI controller needs at least two USB-C ports on at least one of the modules, with one dedicated to the controller and one for the chain connection. The standard module has two ports for chaining, so this works, but users building complex setups need to plan their topology to accommodate the USB host connections they need.
