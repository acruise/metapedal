# Metapedal Video Strategy

This document captures the strategic reasoning behind the platform's video marketing portfolio: five canonical video concepts for launch, the analysis of what makes each one work, the stylistic conventions that run across the portfolio, and the production considerations that anyone executing this work would need to understand.

The audience for this document is anyone involved in commissioning, producing, evaluating, or extending the platform's video marketing work. A marketing team evaluating which videos to lead with finds the strategic reasoning for each piece here. A director or producer commissioned to make the videos finds the analysis of why specific beats and choices were made. A founder explaining the platform's go-to-market plan finds evidence that the marketing thinking is concrete enough to execute.

The scripts themselves live in a companion document: video-01-scripts.md. That document is the production-ready collection of scripts that can be storyboarded directly. The two documents are designed to work together: this one explains why the scripts read the way they do; that one captures what to actually shoot. Production teams should read both: the scripts to know what to produce, the strategy to know why each beat exists and what each choice is meant to accomplish.

This document complements the platform's broader strategy document (strategy-00.md), which covers the platform's positioning, voice, branding, and commercial approach at the strategic level. The platform-strategy document establishes the platform's overall marketing identity; this video-strategy document captures how that identity translates into specific video work.

## How These Videos Fit Together

The five canonical videos in this document together address the platform's main audience categories and culminate in a finale that brings the entire cast together for a real creative project. The same casual register and narrative-arc approach runs across all of them.

**Video one (drawer to stage)** demonstrates the platform's standalone-capability story against the audio interface positioning. The individual musician banishes their laptop, sets up the BX2, goes from home to stage with the same hardware. Broadest audience reach. A musical phrase carries from inspiration through frustration through capture to performance, structurally tying the video together.

**Video two (Band In A Can)** demonstrates the platform's collaborative and accessory-ecosystem story with cultural-traction motif. The same user a month later with accessories getting kickass results, then collaborating with another musician for a band performance. Reaches musicians thinking about collaborative scenarios.

**Video three (hardware hacker with two-phase Theremin)** demonstrates the platform's hackability story plus its foundational architectural payoff in a vivid musical demonstration. A maker in a workshop builds a Theremin clone, plays classical Theremin sounds, then transforms the Theremin into an expression controller for a collaborator's Eurorack synth. Reaches the technical community whose contributions enrich the ecosystem.

**Video four (all-in-thrift-store with cameo)** demonstrates the platform's portability and community-continuity story. A budget-conscious musician finds a $10 thrift store MIDI controller, sets up the whole rig right there in the store with a power bank and cheap speakers, plays DX7 and Hammond emulators, and encounters another platform user (from a previous video) who joins them for an impromptu jam in the thrift store. Reaches the substantial audience that wants serious musical capability at minimal cost.

**Video five (the album)** brings the entire cast together for a basement recording session, simultaneously livestreamed, with six to eight stems captured cleanly through the platform's multi-track recording capability. The next morning, one cast member retrieves the laptop from the drawer (the deliberate inversion of video one's banishment), pulls the stems off the microSD card, and begins post-production on what becomes a real album. The video clarifies that the platform's position is not anti-computer but anti-computer-during-creative-work; the laptop has its proper role in post-production. The album exists in the world as cultural output that lives alongside the videos.

Across all five videos, the platform's marketing voice remains consistent: action over claims, narrative arcs over feature lists, casual register over polished marketing voice. Each video demonstrates the platform's capabilities through real users doing real things with real music rather than through abstract product claims. The videos form a coherent portfolio that builds across releases: individual discovery, community formation, collaborative creative work that produces tangible musical output.

## Video One: From Drawer to Stage

The first video establishes the platform's basic value proposition for individual users: standalone operation, real workflow, BX2 as the rig. The narrative arc moves from home setup to stage performance using the same hardware throughout.

### What the Video Communicates Without Saying

The pan up to reveal the audience is the visual masterstroke. The same person who was at home with a laptop in a drawer is now on stage with an audience watching. The implication is that the BX2 made the entire journey practical: from bedroom setup to live performance with no change of equipment, no reconfiguration, no software running on a laptop somewhere offstage, no audio interface plugged into anything. The BX2 is the whole rig. And critically, the sound they will play on stage is the exact sound they just dialed in at home: the customized preset they saved minutes ago is loaded and ready, with no chance of a different venue producing different results because the venue's gear is not in the signal path.

The song beginning as the ad ends is the right cutoff point. The viewer never sees the full performance; they just see the moment of beginning. This leaves the imagination filling in the rest, which is more powerful than showing the performance.

The video never claims any of these things directly, but the viewer absorbs all of them:

- The BX2 boots fast enough that boot time is not part of the user experience
- The BX2 does not need a laptop or any other computer to function
- The BX2 handles both guitar and microphone inputs simultaneously with appropriate processing on each
- The BX2 works with whatever monitoring the user happens to have
- The BX2 setup process is real (the user actually interacts with it) but quick (the whole sequence from boot to mic check fits in maybe a minute)
- The same BX2 that does home practice does live performance, without configuration changes
- The platform is built for actual musicians playing actual music, not for audiophile spec-watchers

Every claim is shown rather than told. Viewers leave the video having absorbed the messages without ever being lectured at. This is the kind of marketing that earns trust because it respects the viewer's intelligence.

### The Musical Phrase Through-Line

The single most important structural choice in the video is the musical phrase that the user is carrying from the opening shot through to the stage performance. The phrase is the spine of the video; everything else hangs from it.

The user is on a walk when the phrase arrives. Songwriters and improvising musicians know this moment intimately: musical ideas rarely arrive in the controlled studio setting where marketing usually depicts them. They arrive when the musician is walking the dog, taking a shower, driving somewhere, doing literally anything other than trying to write music. The inspiration comes from the ambient texture of life: a phrase someone said, a melody overheard from a passing car, birdsong against traffic noise.

The dog-walk setting is doing significant work. Walking the dog is one of those mandatory daily activities that takes the musician out of their studio space and into the broader world. The musician's mind is free: the dog is doing its thing, the walk has its own pace imposed by the dog's needs rather than by the musician's intention, the brain has space to wander. This is when ideas arrive. Showing the platform's user having an idea in this realistic way validates the platform's marketing as understanding how musicians actually work.

The traffic-and-birdsong as the source of the phrase makes the inspiration concrete. The phrase emerges from the audio texture of urban walking: cars passing at variable speeds and distances, birds singing in the trees, the ambient hum of the city. The musician's brain processes this audio material and finds musical patterns in it. This is genuinely how musicians work; they listen to the world musically, hearing what most people experience as background noise as raw material for music.

The dog-pooping moment is the perfect comedic and authentic detail. The musician is in the middle of mandatory dog-ownership work. The dog has chosen its spot and is doing its business. The musician is standing there with the leash, waiting, and they cannot help but start humming the phrase, working out the contours of the melodic idea while their dog poops. The juxtaposition of high art (musical inspiration) with low biological function (dog pooping) signals that the platform's marketing is willing to be honest about life rather than aspirational. The musician's creative life happens alongside the rest of their life, including the parts that are not glamorous.

The phrase develops during the walk home. The viewer experiences the idea growing through the user's vocalization: it gains notes, gains shape, finds its actual form as the user rushes back to their studio. This gives the opening narrative motion that a "user rushes in with the phrase already formed" version would lack. Musicians know that ideas develop during the time between inception and capture; the video showing this development demonstrates that the platform's marketing understands the actual creative process.

The dog becomes a character in the video's narrative arc rather than just being incidentally present on the floor during the home studio setup. The dog was on the walk; the dog witnessed the inspiration; the dog rushed home with the user; the dog is now on the floor during the entire creative session, eventually settling in the doorway as the user heads to the stage. The dog is part of the creative work even though the dog is just being a dog. The dog connects the inspiration moment to the home studio section and provides cross-video continuity if the same dog appears in video five's post-production scene.

The phrase persists through the entire video in different forms:

In the opening, it is fresh inspiration. The user hums or whistles it casually but constantly, trying to hold onto it.

During the computer-shit frustration, the phrase warps. The user's irritation colors the melodic line; the humming shifts into a minor key. The melody that started as inspiration becomes something darker, distorted by the user's emotional state. They cannot help but keep producing the phrase, but the phrase is being corrupted by the obstacle.

After the BX2 comes out, the phrase returns to its original key. The platform has not corrupted it; the obstacle is gone. The phrase is the one the user originally had.

When the platform is set up and recording, the user lets loose in full song for a second or two. The phrase that has been carrying through the video finally gets released: sung properly, fully, with the user committing to it now that the platform is capturing it. The musical idea has been captured cleanly.

At the stage reveal, the song begins, and the opening of the song is the same musical phrase. The phrase the user has been carrying since the inspiration moment is the song they are now performing. The musical idea has traveled from the user's head through capture and refinement to public performance, and the video has shown the whole journey.

### What the Musical Through-Line Does

The musical phrase transforms the video from "user banishes laptop and plays music" into "user has a musical idea and brings it from inspiration through capture to performance." The platform becomes the means by which the idea travels from the user's head to the world.

The phrase gives the video three reinforcing arcs that play simultaneously:

The visual arc: home/desk → home/floor → stage.

The emotional arc: frustrated → engaged → satisfied → public.

The musical arc: inspiration → distorted by frustration → captured cleanly → released as performance.

Each arc reinforces the others. The viewer experiences all three simultaneously without consciously analyzing them. The video has the kind of structural depth that distinguishes memorable marketing from forgettable marketing.

### The Minor-Key Distortion During Frustration

The detail of the phrase shifting into a minor key during the computer-shit frustration is doing serious work. It demonstrates a claim the platform's marketing wants to make but rarely gets to prove: that the platform serves musical creativity while competing approaches actively harm it.

This is not the kind of claim that marketing usually gets to make convincingly. Most marketing claims of "this product is better for creativity" are abstract. The video makes the claim concrete: watch the user's phrase get worse as the computer interferes; watch it recover when the platform takes over. The damage and recovery are audible.

The viewer absorbs the claim through their own ears. The minor-key version sounds worse than the original key version (the viewer experiences this directly without analysis). The restored version sounds right again. The platform's value for creativity becomes a sensory experience rather than an abstract claim.

A viewer with formal music theory training will recognize the minor-key shift explicitly. A viewer without that training will not be able to name what they are hearing but will still feel the difference. The phrase will sound "darker" or "sadder" or "off" during the frustration phase. The phrase will sound "right" again after the BX2 capture. The video does not require musical literacy to work; it just requires functioning ears.

### The Capture Moment as Climax

When the BX2 comes out and is recording and the user lets loose in full song for a second, this is the climactic moment of the video's emotional arc. The whole video has been building to this: the user has been carrying the phrase since the opening shot, fighting to keep it alive through the computer frustration, and now the platform lets them finally express it fully.

The "for a second" qualifier matters. The full song is not what the video is about; the platform is. The brief release into full song demonstrates that the user has the idea ready, that the platform captures it cleanly, that the idea is real and worth capturing. Then the user returns to the practical work of setting up the rig for the stage.

The release into full song is emotionally satisfying for the viewer. The frustration of the computer-shit opening has built tension; the capture moment releases that tension. The viewer experiences relief alongside the user when the phrase finally gets sung properly.

### The Phrase Becoming the Stage Performance

The phrase that opened the video as a hummed inspiration is the same phrase that opens the song the user performs on stage. This is the structural choice that ties the entire video together.

The viewer who has been carrying the phrase mentally throughout the video (because the user keeps repeating it in various forms) recognizes it immediately when the song begins. The recognition is the payoff. The phrase that seemed like a small idea at the opening turns out to have been the seed of an actual song. The platform made this possible.

The audience reveal at the end lands harder for this connection. The user is not just performing on stage; they are performing the song that started as a humming melody at the video's opening. The audience is hearing what the viewer has been hearing in the user's head for the whole video. The private musical idea has become public musical experience.

### Choosing the Phrase

The musical phrase becomes critical to the video's success. It needs to be:

Catchy enough that the viewer will remember it. A boring phrase the viewer cannot recall when the song starts would fail the payoff.

Short enough to be hummed naturally. A complex melodic idea the user has to think about reproducing would not feel like spontaneous inspiration.

Recognizable in different forms. The phrase needs to work as humming, as whistling, as under-breath singing, as the minor-key variation during frustration, as the full-throated release after capture, as the opening of the song. All these variations need to be obviously the same phrase.

The opening of an actual song. The phrase cannot be a fragment that does not lead anywhere musically; it needs to be the natural beginning of a song the user can plausibly perform.

The production team has real songwriting work to do here. The phrase is the spine of the video; getting it right matters. The production should probably commission an actual song for the video, with the opening phrase chosen specifically for the role it plays through the video's arc. The song does not need to be a hit; it just needs to be a song that exists, with an opening phrase memorable enough to carry the through-line.

The song selection also opens the possibility of releasing the song itself alongside the video. If the song is good, listeners who enjoy the video might want to hear the full track. The platform's marketing could include a Bandcamp or SoundCloud link to the full song; the song becomes a small piece of cultural content that lives alongside the video and extends the video's reach. The song could be credited to the fictional musician character or to a real musician who appears in the video; either way, the song has its own life beyond the marketing context.

### The Audio Design Implications

The musical phrase through-line affects the audio design throughout the video. Specifically:

The opening walk needs the ambient audio to be rich enough that the phrase plausibly emerges from it. The viewer hears traffic, birds, footsteps, the city's hum. The phrase the user hums incorporates audible elements from this source material: a particular interval that mimics a bird call, a rhythmic pattern that echoes the cadence of passing traffic. The viewer might not consciously notice these connections, but they reinforce that the phrase came from the world, not from nowhere. This is the kind of audio sophistication that rewards repeat viewing: first-time viewers absorb the phrase as the user's musical idea, repeat viewers start to hear the connection between the ambient opening sounds and the phrase's specific contours.

The phrase as it develops during the walk home needs to be clearly the same phrase, just gaining definition. The viewer learns the phrase through its development; by the time the user reaches the studio, the phrase is recognizable enough that they will remember it through its later variations.

The minor-key version during the computer-shit frustration needs to be clearly the same phrase, just colored differently. The audio mix should ensure this connection is audible. The frustration sounds (sighs, exasperated breaths) layer on top of the hummed phrase rather than replacing it; the viewer hears both the phrase and the user's irritation with the situation that is preventing them from capturing it.

The transition moment when the phrase returns to its original key after the BX2 plug-in should be subtle but clear. The viewer's ear catches the resolution; the sensation is "okay, the phrase is back to itself."

The capture moment release into full song needs to be the audio climax of the video. Up until this point, the phrase has been quiet and private; now it is loud and committed. The audio mix should support this shift: the user's voice gets foregrounded, the room ambience gets pushed down, the phrase fills the audio space.

The stage performance opening should connect seamlessly to the audio climax that preceded it. The viewer should recognize the phrase the moment the song begins; the audio mix should make this recognition immediate rather than requiring the viewer to work for it.

### The Rhythm Device: Continuous Pulse with Character Changes at Climactic Moments

The video's most ambitious structural choice is a rhythm that runs through the entire piece but changes character at climactic moments. The rhythm is always present; what changes is the pulse density and the instrumentation carrying it. This is more sophisticated than a "no rhythm then rhythm arrives" structure because it acknowledges that real life always has rhythm; what changes is the pulse's emotional energy.

The pulse density and the emotional energy are what do the structural work. The specific instruments carrying the pulse are at the service of the emotional energy. Quarter notes feel like relaxed energy regardless of what is carrying them; whether the arrangement uses literal quarter note hihats or the user's footsteps at quarter note pulse, the emotional effect is the same. Eighth notes feel like focused energy regardless of what is carrying them; closed hihats are a common choice but typing rhythms or keyboard taps can do the same work. Sixteenth notes feel like high energy regardless of what is carrying them; a full drum kit at sixteenth note hihat density is the canonical example but the same density can be carried by shaker, sequenced synth blips, or rapid hand percussion.

This frees the arrangement to use whatever percussion serves each moment best. The cadence stays consistent within each phase; the instrumentation varies as the narrative demands.

### The Cadence Accelerates Across Three Stages

The cadence accelerates across the video in three clean stages, each stage doubling the pulse density of the previous one.

**Quarter notes during the dog walk.** The pulse is comfortable, like a slow heartbeat. The user is walking, not rushing. The pace is the dog's pace. The musical idea is forming but the user is not yet urgent about capturing it. The pulse might be carried by the user's footsteps, the rhythm of breath, the rhythm of casual humming, or by sparse arrangement elements: occasional soft percussion at narratively significant moments rather than constant pulse-keeping. The pulse is felt rather than always heard explicitly.

**Eighth notes from the moment the laptop opens.** The pulse has doubled. Time is subdividing twice as fast. The user's attention has narrowed to the screen; the broader world has receded. This captures the change in interior energy when the user sits down at the laptop and commits to capturing the idea while the computer is starting to interfere. Closed hihats are the natural choice for this phase because they capture the tense waiting quality, but the typing rhythms, mouse clicks, and other diegetic sounds of the user's interaction with the computer are doing rhythmic work alongside the hihats. The computer's events (update prompts, login dialog, spinning loader, beachball, notification slide-in) land on or near the eighth note grid. The computer's friction is in time with the user's tension; both escalate together.

**Sixteenth notes from the moment the laptop falls.** The kick drum enters simultaneously with another doubling of pulse density. The pulse has quadrupled since the dog walk. Crucially, the emotional register of the dense pulse has shifted. The eighth notes during the computer-shit served tension because they subdivided the time the user was waiting. The sixteenth notes after the laptop falls serve flow because they subdivide the time the user is actively working. Both phases have high pulse density, but one is anxious and the other is focused.

The sixteenth note cadence after the laptop fall is high energy but pleasant. The user is happy, the work is going well, the music supports the activity. The full drum kit is now in play. The diegetic sounds of the user's setup actions (BX2 placement, cable plugs, button presses, menu navigation) integrate with the kit's pattern. This is the register of uptempo pop or breakbeat: busy and energetic but fundamentally good-natured.

### Sparse Percussion and Single Hits

### Transitions Marked by Drum Fills

The cadence acceleration alone is not enough to carry the video's transitions; the transitions need musical events that mark them as such. Drum fills are how real music marks transitions between sections. A fill is a short burst of percussion that breaks the established pattern, signals that something is changing, and lands at a new pattern. A couple of snare hits and a cymbal crash lasting maybe a second is the canonical fill structure. The viewer feels the transition through the fill before the new pattern establishes itself.

This is the difference between a song that mechanically shifts gears and a song that musically transitions between sections. The fill provides the musical glue that makes transitions feel like part of the song rather than like edit points. The video has three major transitions and they should all be marked by fills, with the fills calibrated to the magnitude of each transition.

The phrase-forming fill during the dog walk is the smallest. The user is still walking the dog; the world has not changed; only the user's interior state has shifted from contented-walking to focused-walking-toward-home. A subtle fill marks this: maybe a single roll across a small drum, or a brief shaker pattern landing on a beat. The fill is the size of the transition: small but real.

The getting-in-the-door fill is medium-sized. The user moves from outside to inside. The ambient texture changes completely: traffic and birds and wind give way to the interior sound of the apartment. A couple of percussion events mark the threshold: a tom hit, a hihat roll, maybe a small cymbal accent landing as the door closes. The transition is environmental and gets a proportional musical event.

The laptop-fall fill is the climactic fill of the video, and a specific reference makes the structural intent clear: the roughly 2.5 seconds covering the laptop falling into the drawer through to the BX2 hitting the desk should sound a bit like the famous tom fill from Phil Collins's "In The Air Tonight." A descending pattern across three to five toms, with appropriate impact and possibly the gated reverb treatment that defines the original. The fill marks the most consequential transition in the video and is correspondingly the most elaborate. The cadence acceleration, the time signature resolution, and the move from sparse percussion to full kit all happen at this fill.

The In The Air Tonight reference is doing specific structural work. The Phil Collins fill is famously the moment the full kit enters his song after three minutes of sparse atmospheric production; the fill is the threshold between the song-as-it-was-becoming and the song-as-it-is. The platform's video uses the fill the same way: before it, the video has had sparse percussion and the closed hihat pulse; after it, the video has the full drum kit at sixteenth notes. The fill marks the moment the kit introduces itself.

Each visual action in the laptop-fall sequence lands on a tom hit: the laptop hitting the drawer bottom (first tom), the drawer closing (second tom), the user grabbing the BX2 (third tom), the BX2 hitting the desk (fourth tom), the cable plugging in (cymbal accent that bridges into the full kit's establishment). The viewer experiences the visual sequence as a musical phrase. The fill does both rhythmic and synchronization work: it provides the climactic musical event AND locks the visual actions to the music.

Toms are the right instrument for this fill specifically. Snares would be too sharp and aggressive; cymbals would not have the body; kick drum hits would not have the melodic content. Toms combine impact with tonal descent. A descending tom fill (high to medium to low) creates the feeling of falling, of arriving at a deeper place, of settling into the song's foundation. The descending pitch matches the laptop's literal fall into the drawer.

The gated reverb treatment is optional but worth considering. In The Air Tonight's fill is famous specifically because of the gated reverb on the drums, the technique that defined 1980s drum production. Using gated reverb on the laptop-fall fill makes the reference unmistakable; the viewer who knows the reference feels it immediately. The production team can choose whether to use the exact treatment or a different reverb approach that captures the structural feel without trying to clone the exact sonic fingerprint. The reference is about the structural moment, not about copying the exact sound.

The cultural recognition factor matters here. The Phil Collins fill is one of the most parodied and referenced drum moments in popular music history. The viewer who has any cultural exposure to popular music recognizes the fill or at least recognizes its structural function. The platform's marketing is signaling that it operates in the same cultural space as the music that everyone knows. The video is not trying to be obscure or arty; it is using one of the most universally recognized musical structures because that structure works perfectly for the platform's narrative needs.

The exact rhythmic content of each fill is a real songwriting decision that benefits from a drummer's intuition about how fills actually feel. The platform's production team should consult with experienced drummers about the specific patterns that mark these transitions most effectively. A drummer's ear for what makes a fill land well is the kind of expertise that distinguishes great music from competent music.

### The Pullback at the Desk-to-Floor Transition

The video's most counter-intuitive rhythmic choice is the moment when the rhythm pulls back rather than pushes forward. After the laptop fall, the studio phase has been continuous high-energy flow. Sixteenth notes have been driving the rhythm; the full kit has been in play; the user has been doing focused creative work.

When the user picks up the BX2 and moves it from the desk to the floor, the rhythm pulls back. The full kit recedes; the sixteenth note hihats give way to sparse closed hihats; the busy energy of the studio phase gives way to quiet anticipation. This is structurally surprising. The video has been accelerating since the dog walk; pulling back at this moment violates the energy curve the video has been building.

But the desk-to-floor transition is a different kind of moment than the studio phase. The user is shifting from private creative work to public performance. They are physically picking up the BX2 and moving it from the work surface to the floor where it will be stomped. This is the threshold between two registers: creator and performer. The rhythm pulling back lets the threshold be felt; the viewer experiences the user gathering themselves before the public moment.

The sparse closed hihat that returns at this moment also creates a callback to the computer-shit phase. The closed hihat was the rhythm of waiting and tension; now it is the rhythm of preparation and anticipation. The same instrument and density that previously carried negative energy now carries positive anticipation. The viewer feels the difference even though the rhythmic content is similar.

This is how real performers experience the moment before going on stage. The energy of preparation is different from the energy of performance. The performer typically has a quiet moment of transition between them: a breath, a final adjustment, a mental shift from offstage to onstage. The video showing this quiet moment respects the actual texture of musical performance.

### The Visual Quality Shift at the Desk-to-Floor Transition

The video uses video quality and color grading shifts to mark the desk-to-floor transition as a magic teleportation from bedroom to stage. The visual register changes during the BX2's movement from desk to floor; the camera lingers on the pedal for a precise 200ms after it lands; the cause becomes clear when the stomp triggers the audience reveal.

Throughout the studio sequence, the video has been in a specific visual register: bright bedroom-studio lighting, clean color grading, balanced dynamic range, the texture of well-lit home video. The viewer sees the user's hands in good visible detail; the OLED screens are readable; the gear is clearly identifiable.

When the user picks up the BX2 from the desk and moves it toward the floor, the visual register starts to shift. The bedroom studio's clean lighting and balanced grading give way to something different: bright spots beginning to bloom, dark areas gaining visible noise, the dynamic range compressing, the color grading pushing in some direction. The shift happens during the BX2's downward motion.

The BX2 lands on the floor. The camera holds on the pedal for about 200 milliseconds. This is the suspended moment of mystery: the pedal sits there alone on a surface that the viewer now realizes is not the bedroom floor; the visual quality issues are fully visible; the cause is not yet clear. The user's foot is visible at the edge of frame; the venue's darkness and lights are visible in the background; everything is there for the viewer to read but the meaning is not yet assembled.

Then the stomp. The user's foot comes down on the footswitch. The massive 909 kick lands on the impact; the camera pans up; the audience appears in the same motion. The visual quality shift now makes sense as the natural texture of performance footage in a venue.

### Why 200 Milliseconds Specifically

200 milliseconds is the threshold of conscious perception. The viewer's brain registers that something has changed; the viewer cannot yet articulate what; the change is felt rather than analyzed. This is the duration that creates "wait, what?" without becoming "what is going on with this video?"

Shorter (under 100ms) would not register. The viewer's brain would not have time to notice the visual quality change before the stomp triggers the next phase. The mystery would be lost.

Longer (over 500ms) would start to feel like a problem. The viewer would have time to wonder if something is wrong with the video; they would shift from "noticing" to "interpreting"; they might form wrong conclusions about why the video looks strange.

200ms is the sweet spot where the viewer notices but does not yet analyze. The stomp arrives just before analysis would begin. The reveal resolves the noticing into understanding without going through wondering.

200 milliseconds is also roughly one sixteenth note at moderate tempo. The video has been at sixteenth-note cadence throughout the studio sequence; this 200ms hold lands as exactly one beat of the established subdivision. The lingering shot fits the song's rhythmic structure naturally. The hold is also the moment of the rhythm pullback's resolution: the closed hihat has been carrying the recessed pulse since the desk-to-floor motion began; the 200ms hold is the moment of suspended tension before the kick lands.

### The Pedal on the Floor as Focal Point

The 200ms lingering shot is on the pedal, not on the user. The user has just placed the BX2 down; the camera holds on the BX2 itself; the user's foot is visible at the edge of frame or just out of frame. The pedal is the protagonist of this brief moment.

This is consistent with the platform's broader visual identity. The over-the-shoulder POV throughout has positioned the gear as the visual focus rather than the performer's face. The 200ms lingering shot on the pedal continues this convention: the pedal is what the camera is interested in, not the user's expression.

The pedal sitting on a darker-than-expected floor with visible noise and bloom around it tells the viewer immediately that the location has changed. The pedal is the same; the floor is different. The viewer's brain does the comparison: pedal recognizable from the studio sequence, floor not from the bedroom.

### Why the Visual Shift Works

The visual quality shift is doing several pieces of structural work simultaneously.

It makes the magic teleportation visible. The desk-to-floor transition has been a structural device throughout: the user starts at the bedroom desk, the camera pans up after the stomp, the audience is revealed. The video has never explained how the user got from bedroom to stage; the video relies on the viewer accepting the leap. The visual quality shift acknowledges the leap rather than pretending the cut is invisible. The marketing respects the viewer's intelligence by making the structural device visible.

It creates a moment of mystery that engages the viewer. The brief confusion about why the video has changed is engagement; the viewer leans forward into the image trying to read it; the audience reveal lands on a viewer who is fully engaged. The mystery serves as a focusing device for the climactic moment.

It signals the venue's character through pure visual texture. Stage lights are aggressive light sources with high color temperatures and substantial intensity. Cameras shooting in venues struggle with the high dynamic range between the bright stage and the dark crowd; lower-end cameras (or cameras shooting in low-bit-depth modes) bloom on the bright areas and add noise to the dark areas. This visual character is canonically associated with concert footage; the platform's video using this texture for the closing moments positions the closing moments as recognizably "shot in a venue."

It fits the platform's anti-aspirational positioning. A sterile product video would shoot the stage moment with the same controlled lighting and clean grading as the bedroom studio. The platform's video specifically rejects this; the stage looks like a real stage in a real venue with the real visual character of performance footage, not a marketing approximation. The authenticity of the visual treatment matches the authenticity of the other production choices (the cheap-ass speakers, the dog at the feet, the masking tape price tags).

It pairs with the audio pullback to create combined audio-visual recession before the public moment. The rhythm pulls back from full kit to sparse closed hihat at the same moment the video quality starts to shift. Both registers are recessing before the climactic moment. The viewer feels the user gathering themselves through both audio and visual recession; the audience reveal lands on viewer who has been prepared through both senses.

### Choosing the Time Signature

The choice of tricky time signature matters. Some options:

5/4 is a relatively common odd meter, used by Dave Brubeck's "Take Five" and many others. The brain can lock into it but it never quite feels like home. Subtle wrongness rather than aggressive wrongness.

7/8 is clearly odd, harder to count, feels actively unbalanced. The brain keeps almost finding the downbeat and almost losing it. Captures real frustration well.

11/8 or 13/8 is heavily prog-rock territory, very challenging to feel. Probably too aggressive for the platform's casual register; would draw attention to itself rather than serving the narrative.

3/4 or 6/8 are technically not 4/4 but feel stable. Probably too stable to communicate the wrongness the computer-shit needs.

The right choice is probably 5/4 or 7/8. 5/4 captures "something is slightly off"; 7/8 captures "something is actively wrong." The composer can choose based on how aggressive they want the wrongness to feel.

## Video Three: The Hardware Hacker

The third video targets the hardware hacker community whose contributions to the platform's third-party ecosystem matter enormously for the platform's long-term health. The video shows a maker building a Theremin clone, integrating it with Metapedal, and finding an unexpected collaborator.

Phase one is the classical Theremin. The two antennas drive pitch and volume the canonical way. Phase two is the expression controller. The same Theremin, the same gestures, but the routing graph now sends the two antennas' signals to parameters on the Eurorack's audio rather than to a synth voice. Vertical to filter cutoff, horizontal to resonance. The Theremin transforms from a sound source into a dynamic effects controller.

This two-phase structure demonstrates several things at once:

- The platform's routing graph flexibility (same physical hardware serves two completely different musical functions in the same session)
- The platform's foundational architectural principle (controls are decoupled from audio processing)
- The richer collaboration possible with the platform (complementary musical roles enabled by routing capabilities)
- A capability that traditional gear cannot match (a real Theremin cannot easily become a controller for other gear; a modular's controls cannot easily be replaced by gestural input)

### Why the Filter-and-Resonance Mapping Specifically

Filter sweeps with resonance are core to a huge amount of contemporary electronic music. The video demonstrating expressive real-time filter control through gestural input shows the platform supporting serious electronic music production capability.

The visual gesture of the Theremin hacker's hand motion combined with the audible result of the filter sweeping is striking. The viewer can see the cause-and-effect relationship clearly: hand goes up, filter opens, sound gets brighter; hand goes down, filter closes, sound gets darker. The resonance on the horizontal axis gives the demonstration tonal variety. The same vertical hand motion produces different sounds depending on horizontal position. Hand at the left with low resonance gives smooth filter sweeps; hand at the right with high resonance gives squelchy acid-style movement. The two-dimensional control surface produces a wide range of sonic outcomes that the user can explore by exploring the physical space around the Theremin.

## Production Considerations

### The Over-the-Shoulder POV Convention

All five videos use a consistent visual convention: the camera positions the viewer over the user's shoulder rather than across from them. The viewer sees the user's hands, the gear, the screen, the instrument, the work being done, but does not see the user's face. The face appears only when the user is interacting with another character.

This convention has several substantial effects.

The hands become the protagonist. Without the face as emotional focal point, the hands carry the character through the video. Every action is visible: plugging in cables, navigating menus, playing instruments, tweaking parameters, building hardware, manipulating drawbars. Watching skilled hands do skilled work is intrinsically interesting in the way that watch repair videos, soldering tutorials, woodworking demonstrations, and cooking channels are interesting. The convention inherits the trust dynamic these established formats have built: the viewer trusts the work because they can see it being done.

The viewer inhabits the user's position rather than watching them from outside. The viewer sees what the user sees: the gear from the user's angle, the screen at the user's distance, the work surface as the user experiences it. The viewer's experience approximates being the user rather than watching the user. This is fundamentally different from standard product marketing, which positions the user as someone the viewer should aspire to be. Over-the-shoulder positioning makes the viewer the user; the platform's value becomes visceral because the viewer experiences it as if it were their own work.

The casting risk drops substantially. The earlier concerns about needing a performer who could mutter naturally, hum and whistle without seeming aware of the camera, and deliver authentic emotional reactions become less critical because the camera is not capturing the face. The performer just needs to be a real musician doing their actual work; the camera is positioned to capture the work, not the performance. The vocalizations carry the emotional content that the face would otherwise carry, making the audio narrative more important precisely because the visual narrative is focused on hands and gear.

The face-when-looking-at-another-character exception preserves the over-the-shoulder convention for solo work while allowing the face to appear when human connection matters. The face is reserved for moments of human-to-human interaction, which makes those moments more impactful when they happen. Specific examples across the portfolio:

In video three, the Theremin hacker's face appears when the Eurorack person walks over. The shrug and the "I don't know, give it a shot" line are accompanied by the hacker's expression.

In video four, the user's face appears when the cameo character zips over and says "oh, Metapedal. Sweet." The brief exchange between two strangers united by the platform is face-to-face.

In video five, faces appear during the recording session as the cast members react to each other's playing, share glances about musical decisions, communicate the energy of collaborative work. The basement session is fundamentally about human connection, so the faces appear throughout the session portion of the video.

In video one, the face might appear only at the "hello Cleveland" moment, or even there might be obscured to let the audience be the focal point of the reveal.

This face-reveal pattern creates a structural payoff across the portfolio. Viewers who have watched the previous four videos have seen these characters' hands, gear, and vocalizations but not their faces (mostly). The fifth video's basement session brings them all into the same room with faces visible during collaboration; the recognition is part of the climactic feeling that the community has finally come together.

The convention also reinforces the platform's broader anti-marketing-aesthetic stance. Most product marketing prominently features faces because faces are the most engaging visual element for human attention; marketing teams use this knowledge to make ads with smiling people. The platform's marketing rejecting this convention is itself a stance: the videos are not selling the user as an aspirational figure; they are selling the work as something the viewer can do themselves. Hiding the face removes the aspirational reading. The viewer cannot fantasize about looking like the user because they cannot see the user. They can only fantasize about doing what the user is doing, which is exactly what the platform wants them to fantasize about.

The same dog that walked with the user during the inspiration moment is now snoozing on the floor, occasionally raising its head at noises. **Video two (Band In A Can):** A cat watching from a windowsill while the user demonstrates the accelerometer and breath sensor. The cat is not interfering, just observing. At the stage venue, possibly a baby in the audience being held by a parent, briefly visible in the audience reveal.

**Video three (hardware hacker):** The Eurorack person who walks over to the Theremin hacker has a baby in a sling strapped to their chest. The baby is small enough to need close contact with the parent, probably under a year old, sleeping or quietly watching while the adults talk. This casting detail says something specific about the modular synth community: Eurorack people are often serious hobbyists with day jobs, families, lives. They are adults who fit their modular practice into their adult lives, which includes childcare.

The baby in the sling enriches the collaboration moment. The Eurorack person shows up with their modular AND their baby; they are casual about both because this is just how they live. The "can I plug in my MIDI" question gets asked with the baby right there on the parent's chest. When the jamming begins, the baby is inches from the music being made. The viewer sees this and absorbs that real life is exactly like this: people make music with their children right there with them, not stowed away in another room.

The makerspace can also have a resident cat that walks across workbenches and gets gently moved aside during precision work. The cat is comfortable with the smell of solder, sleeps on whatever surface is convenient, generally behaves as if they own the place.

**Video four (thrift store):** A cat lives at the thrift store. Many actual thrift stores have resident cats; the platform's video uses this convention. The cat is comfortable with strangers, accustomed to the musty smell of old merchandise, content to nap on whatever surface looks good.

When the user assembles their rig in the thrift store aisle and starts playing the cheap MIDI controller, the cat notices and walks over. At some point during the noodling, the cat strolls across the keyboard. Random keys depress under the cat's paws; the controller dutifully transmits the cat's playing to the Metapedal; the platform produces sounds the user did not intend.

This is the perfect comedic interruption. Cats walking on keyboards is one of the universal experiences of musicians who live with cats. The user's response is genuine: a mix of mild annoyance and amusement, the cat is in the way but the cat is also being a cat. The user gently moves the cat aside and continues. The cat finds another surface nearby and settles in to watch.

The cat-on-keys moment also subtly demonstrates platform robustness. The cat presses random keys; the platform handles the unexpected input gracefully (no crashes, no failures, just the cat's "notes" that disappear when the cat is removed). The platform survives the cat scenario, which is the kind of unscripted real-world test that distinguishes products that actually work from products that work only under controlled conditions.

**Video five (the album):** This is where the animals and children get most prominent. The basement session has the toddler from video three's baby (a year older now) in a playpen in the corner, looked after by whoever is not currently playing their instrument. A dog lies on the floor in a quiet spot during the recording. The keyboardist member of the band has their dog snoozing on a couch beside their keyboard rig.

The keyboardist's dog on the couch is doing specific work. The dog has claimed the couch; the keyboardist works around the dog because the dog has always been there. The dog sleeps through the recording session because the dog is used to the sounds. The musician's productivity does not require the dog to be elsewhere; the dog is welcome to sleep through the work. This is exactly how home musicians actually work with their dogs in real life. The viewer recognizes this immediately.

The next-morning post-production scene has the user with their dog at their feet again (callback to video one's home studio). During the latte beat specifically, the dog creates additional warmth: the user pets the dog absentmindedly while the album takes shape. The dog has been there since video one, witnessed the dog walk and the inspiration moment that started the whole arc, and is now there during the final post-production work that completes the album. The dog's presence across the videos is the small recurring detail that signals the user's life has continuity beyond the marketing moments.

### Animals and Children as Cross-Video Continuity

If the same dog appears in video one and video five, the dog becomes a recognizable recurring character. The dog was on the walk during the inspiration; the dog is on the floor during the home studio; the dog appears again in the next-morning post-production of video five. The dog has been there throughout the user's musical life. Viewers who pay attention notice the dog and feel rewarded by the continuity.

The baby in the sling in video three could become the toddler in the playpen in video five. The character has aged between videos; the Eurorack person's life has progressed; the platform has been a constant through their evolving family situation. This kind of temporal continuity reinforces that the platform fits into users' lives over time, not just for individual sessions.

The thrift store cat stays at the thrift store; it does not recur in other videos because thrift store cats stay at thrift stores. The cat is specific to video four; its appearance is a unique feature of that video rather than a recurring character.

The keyboardist's dog could appear in multiple videos if the keyboardist appears in multiple videos. A future studio video featuring the keyboardist would naturally include the dog on the couch beside them, the same dog from the basement session.

### Production Realities

Animals and children are unpredictable on set. Real productions handle this by working with experienced animal handlers and parents who understand what their child can do on camera. The platform's marketing should commit to working with real cast members' actual pets and children where possible, rather than hired animal actors.

This is consistent with the platform's broader anti-marketing-aesthetic stance. The dog should be the cast member's dog. The cat should be the makerspace's cat or a cast member's cat. The baby should be a cast member's baby. Authenticity beats polish.

The production schedule needs to accommodate these realities. Babies cannot work long shoots; pets get bored and may not perform; toddlers need breaks. The production should expect to spend more time on shots involving animals and children to get them right, and should have backup options if the animals or children are not cooperating on a given day.

The over-the-shoulder POV convention helps here. Because the animals and children are at the edge of the frame rather than the center, the shots do not require them to perform; they just need to be present in the space. A sleeping dog at the user's feet is a more reliable shot than a dog performing a specific action on cue.



Over-the-shoulder shots require careful framing. The user's shoulder and back of head are typically visible at the edge of the frame; the user's hands and the work they are doing fill the center of the frame. The camera position is typically slightly above and behind the user's head, angled down at the work surface.

For varied shot composition, the videos can use:

Close-up over-the-shoulder shots showing detail of specific work (a hand moving a drawbar, fingers playing a chord, hands soldering, hands plugging in cables, eyes-on-screen as the user navigates a menu, though the eyes might be cropped to keep the face out).

Medium over-the-shoulder shots showing the user's torso, hands, and the broader work area. The user becomes a presence in the frame without being its focal point.

Wide over-the-shoulder shots showing the user, their gear, and the surrounding space. The thrift store, the makerspace, the basement, the home studio all become visible while the user remains a shoulder-and-back-of-head in the foreground.

Each composition serves a different narrative purpose. The close-up shows the precision of the work; the medium shows the user-gear relationship; the wide shows the user in their environment. The variety prevents the over-the-shoulder convention from becoming visually monotonous.

Character recognition across videos still works through other visual elements: specific hands (a particular watch, calluses, rings, tattoos), specific gear (the sticker-covered A module, the Theremin clone, the cheap thrift-store controller), specific clothing (a particular jacket or shirt), specific vocalizations (humming phrases recognizable from earlier videos). Viewers learn to recognize the characters through these markers even without seeing their faces clearly.

### Budget

The five core videos at professional production quality probably cost $15,000-30,000 per video, putting the full launch portfolio at $75,000-150,000. The fifth video might cost more than the others because of the cast size (multiple performers in the basement session) and the additional complexity of the post-production sequence. The album that emerges from video five would be an additional production cost: studio time for finishing the album, mastering, distribution setup, perhaps $5,000-15,000 depending on the album's ambition.

This is modest by music industry marketing standards and reasonable for the marketing impact the videos and the album would have together. Lower-budget approaches are possible if some of the production work is done in-house or by community members willing to contribute time. The platform's hacker-friendly identity is consistent with leveraging community production capability rather than relying entirely on external professional production.

The over-the-shoulder convention may actually reduce production costs slightly. Faces require careful lighting, makeup consideration, and performance coaching; hiding faces removes some of these concerns. The cast members do not need to be photogenic in the conventional marketing sense; they need to have skilled hands and authentic working presence.

### Casting

Each video needs to be cast with real musicians who fit the casual register. A polished spokesperson would undermine the videos' authenticity. The musicians in the videos should be people who genuinely use this kind of gear and react to it authentically.

The over-the-shoulder convention substantially lowers the casting bar in some ways. The cast does not need to be conventionally attractive or have screen-ready faces; they need to be real musicians whose work is interesting to watch. Hands matter more than faces. Vocal expressiveness matters more than facial expressiveness.

The cameo structure across videos means the platform's marketing should think about its cast as a recurring ensemble rather than one-off appearances. The musician in video one returns in video four (as the cameo) and video five (as the album's post-production lead). The hardware hacker from video three returns in video five with their Theremin or expression controller. Casting decisions should consider the cumulative narrative the platform's marketing is building.

The fifth video's cast reunion creates a specific casting requirement: enough scheduling flexibility to bring multiple cast members together for the basement session shoot. This may be the most production-logistically demanding shoot in the portfolio.

### Distribution

The five videos work well across multiple platforms with different cuts and treatments:

- Video one (drawer to stage) works well as YouTube pre-roll, Instagram, the platform's product page hero video
- Video two (Band In A Can) works well on TikTok and YouTube Shorts plus the platform's social media feeds
- Video three (hardware hacker) works well on Hackaday, Adafruit's blog, r/electronics, plus the platform's GitHub presence
- Video four (thrift store) works well on the platform's social media plus communities focused on budget gear and vintage sounds
- Video five (the album) works well as the platform's marketing centerpiece, on the platform's website, in audio industry press coverage, alongside the album's own release promotion

The videos can be released in sequence over time to build narrative continuity, or released simultaneously at launch with the platform's website featuring all five with descriptions of which audience each targets. A hybrid approach might release the first four at launch and hold the fifth as a major event timed with the album release.

### The Casual Register Across All Five

A consistent thread across all five videos is the deliberately casual register. The users in the videos are not polished spokespeople; they are real musicians doing real things. They hum, they whistle, they swear when something surprises them, they react authentically, they use cheap gear when cheap gear is what they have.

The polished alternative (professional voiceover, premium gear in every shot, scripted enthusiasm, faces front-and-center delivering scripted lines) would be substantially worse. The casual register signals authenticity and matches the platform's broader voice strategy. The platform's marketing voice has been articulated through the strategy document; the videos are where that voice gets expressed visually.

Production teams commissioned to make these videos should understand this register and execute against it deliberately. The temptation to make the videos look more "produced" should be resisted; the videos work because they look like what they are showing actually happens in the real world, with real people, in real settings. The over-the-shoulder convention reinforces this register by removing the most production-aspirational visual element (the face) from most of the footage.
