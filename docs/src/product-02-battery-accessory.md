# Metapedal Battery Accessory

This document covers the 18650 battery backpack accessory specification. The audience is people designing the battery accessory specifically or designing similar lithium-ion battery accessories that integrate with Metapedal modules.

Topics: battery backpack implementation, hot-swappable cell behavior, the choice between protected and unprotected cells, and the cell identity and history tracking that supports long-term safe operation.

For the power requirements that the battery accessory must satisfy, see the core technical document.

## The Battery Backpack Implementation

The portable operation use case requires a battery accessory that provides USB-C power to a Metapedal module. The right battery solution is a separate accessory containing user-replaceable 18650 cells rather than a built-in pouch cell. The reasoning matters because it has long-term consequences for users and for the platform.

The 18650 cell is the cylindrical lithium-ion battery, eighteen millimeters in diameter and sixty-five millimeters long, that has become the de facto standard for high-energy portable applications. They are used in laptops, power tools, electric vehicles, flashlights, and most serious portable electronics. The market is enormous, the chemistry is well-understood, the supply chain is mature, and prices are low because volume is high. A quality 18650 cell holds roughly three thousand milliamp-hours of capacity at three point seven volts nominal, giving about eleven watt-hours of energy. At the hundred to two hundred milliwatts that a B variant Metapedal module typically draws, a single cell provides fifty to over a hundred hours of runtime.

The comparison with pouch cells is illuminating. Pouch cells are thin flat lithium-polymer batteries optimized for fitting into small product cavities. They give good energy density and form-factor flexibility, but they have real downsides. Pouch cells are not user-replaceable in any practical sense, so when they degrade the whole product becomes garbage. They degrade significantly over a few hundred charge cycles. They are vulnerable to physical damage and can swell or rupture if abused. When they fail, users cannot replace them without specialized tools.

18650 cells are the opposite design philosophy. The cells are rigid metal cans that protect the chemistry from physical abuse. They are standard parts with consistent dimensions, so holders designed for them work with cells from any manufacturer. They drop in and out of spring-loaded holders the same way AA batteries do. They last longer than pouch cells in cycle life, with quality cells supporting many hundreds of full cycles. They are cheaper per watt-hour than pouch cells at comparable quality levels. And when cells reach end of life, the user replaces them rather than throwing out the device they live in.

The accessory itself is essentially a battery pack with a USB-C output, similar in concept to the portable power banks that are now ubiquitous in consumer electronics. The pack contains one or more 18650 cells in spring-loaded holders, the battery management electronics that handle charging and discharging safely, a charging input port, a power output port, and some kind of mounting or carrying feature like a belt clip or strap loop. The user inserts cells through a battery door, similar to how flashlight battery compartments work.

The cell count is a product decision with tradeoffs. A single-cell pack at eleven watt-hours gives more than enough runtime for any single performance and is the smallest and lightest option. A two-cell pack at twenty-two watt-hours gives essentially unlimited practical runtime and provides a comfortable safety margin for users who do not want to think about battery life. A four-cell pack at forty-four watt-hours is appropriate for specific use cases like outdoor education programs or back-line use where many hours of continuous operation are expected. For version one, both single-cell and two-cell variants make sense as products, with the four-cell version being a possible later addition if demand warrants it.

The protection electronics on the cells deserve a note. Lithium-ion cells need protection against over-charge, over-discharge, over-current, and short circuits, all of which can cause dangerous failures. The protection is provided either by integrated circuits in the cell itself, called protected cells, or by separate protection chips on the battery management board. The right answer for the Metapedal accessory is to require protected cells, which adds a small cost premium but eliminates the need for the accessory to provide its own cell-level protection and ensures safer behavior if users substitute cells of varying quality.

The mechanical connection between the battery pack and the module uses a short USB-C cable rather than any specialized interface. The pack provides USB-C output, the module accepts USB-C input on any of its inter-module ports, and USB Power Delivery handles the power negotiation between them. The firmware does not need to know it is on battery power; the power management is all at the USB-PD protocol level. The pack also provides a 9V barrel output for the pedalboard-compatibility use case, with a small DC-DC converter producing 9V from the cell voltage. Users can power Metapedal modules through USB-C, traditional pedalboard gear through the 9V output, or both simultaneously, making the battery backpack a more versatile accessory than a USB-C-only solution would be.

## Hot-Swappable Cells and Safety

A feature that makes the battery backpack meaningfully more useful for performance is hot-swappable cells. A musician on stage with the pack powering their pedalboard cannot afford to lose power mid-set, so the ability to pull a depleted cell out, slot in a fresh one, and keep playing through the swap turns the pack from "battery you have to plan around" into "battery that just keeps working as long as you have fresh cells in your bag." This is genuinely better than the wall-wart-with-extension-cord approach that most pedalboards rely on for portable use. Several existing power banks, including some from ExpressLRS-adjacent vendors, already implement hot-swap capability, so this is not novel territory; it is a feature worth bringing into the Metapedal accessory.

The cleanest architecture for the hot-swap requirement is dual independent rails rather than series cells with balancing. Each cell is treated as its own self-contained system with its own BMS chip handling its own protection (over-voltage, under-voltage, over-current, over-temperature). Each cell feeds its own boost converter that produces a 9V intermediate rail. The two 9V intermediate rails get combined through an ideal-diode-OR stage to drive the actual output. Standard parts like the Linear Technology LTC4357 or LTC4416 implement the ideal-diode behavior with very low forward voltage drop and automatic reverse-current blocking. The two MOSFETs feed the same output node, and whichever rail has slightly higher voltage at any instant supplies the current. Both rails contribute roughly equally during normal operation because the boost converters have output impedance that naturally equalizes load sharing.

This eliminates the entire class of mismatched-series-cells problems by construction. The cells are never wired in series; they are independent power sources contributing to a shared output through ideal-diode OR. The user can install cells of different ages, different brands, different capacities, even different states of charge, and the system handles all combinations cleanly. There is no balancing to wait for, no mismatched-cell error state, no charge-before-use requirement. The pack just works with whatever cells the user puts in it, which matches the mental model that users actually have for replaceable batteries.

The single-cell-present case becomes trivial in this architecture. With only one cell installed, only one rail is active, and the ideal-diode OR just passes that rail through to the output. The boost converter on the active rail handles the full load, and the pack continues to operate normally at reduced runtime. No mode-switching logic, no special handling, no coordination between rails. The architecture is inherently single-cell-tolerant by construction.

The hot-swap process is also simpler than with a series architecture. The user pulls a depleted cell and inserts a fresh one. During the swap window, the pack runs from the remaining cell through its boost converter and ideal-diode OR. When the new cell is inserted, its BMS comes online, its boost converter starts producing 9V, and the ideal-diode OR brings the new rail into the parallel output configuration. No verification that cells are compatible for series operation is needed because they are never going to be wired in series. The mechanical design still benefits from sliding-contact arrangements with sequential pin engagement (ground first, then sense, then power) to handle the insertion without creating momentary shorts, but the firmware coordination is much simpler than in the series approach.

The cost is similar to or slightly higher than the series-with-balancing approach. The dual-independent architecture uses two BMS chips (a dollar or two each) and two boost converters (a dollar or two each) plus the ideal-diode OR stage (a dollar or two). The series approach uses one BMS chip with two-cell monitoring (two or three dollars) and one buck-boost converter (one or two dollars). Total cost is in a similar range, with the dual-independent approach perhaps two to four dollars more expensive but with substantially better architectural properties. The slight cost premium buys real simplification of the user model and elimination of mismatched-cell edge cases.

There is an efficiency trade-off worth being explicit about. Boost converters have efficiency around 85-92% depending on operating point. Running two boost converters in parallel means the conversion losses are paid twice. A single buck-boost in the series approach might achieve 90-94% efficiency by operating in buck mode for the dominant two-cell case. The energy difference is a few percent, meaningful but not decisive for a portable application. The simpler architecture and elimination of cell-balancing complexity is probably worth the slight efficiency cost for the Metapedal use case.

A key benefit of the dual-independent architecture is that cell capacity utilization is better. In the series approach, the pack is limited by the weaker cell because once that cell hits low-voltage cutoff, the whole pack stops. In the dual-independent approach, each cell can discharge to its own low-voltage cutoff independently, so the pack runs until both cells are depleted rather than stopping when the first one is. The total energy extracted is higher across the life of a charge cycle.

The per-cell visual indicator is the user-facing piece of this design. With user-replaceable cells, the user genuinely wants to know which cell is low so they know which one to swap. The minimum-viable indicator is a single RGB LED per cell with three states: green for healthy, yellow for getting low, red for nearly depleted. Two LEDs, one per cell, each driven by a microcontroller GPIO with PWM for the color mixing, costs perhaps fifty cents in components. The user sees both LEDs at a glance and knows which cell to replace when one goes red. Detailed cell information (exact voltage, exact remaining capacity, charging state) is available on the OLED display of any connected Metapedal module when the user navigates to a battery status screen, with the pack reporting per-cell data over the inter-module bus.

## Protected Versus Unprotected Cells

Lithium-ion cells come in two varieties relevant to user-replaceable battery applications: protected cells, which include a small integrated protection circuit inside the cell's positive terminal that handles over-charge, over-discharge, over-current, and short-circuit protection, and unprotected cells, which are the bare chemistry without integrated protection. The choice between them has real implications for the battery backpack design and for the user experience.

Protected cells are the consumer-friendly default. The integrated protection circuit handles edge cases that the pack's BMS might not catch quickly enough: dead shorts at the cell terminals, severe over-current events that exceed the pack's BMS reaction time, gross misuse like the user putting the cell in backwards. The protection adds a small cost premium (typically a dollar or two per cell) and slightly reduces the cell's effective capacity due to the parasitic losses from the protection circuit. Most quality consumer-grade 18650 cells sold in retail channels are protected cells.

Unprotected cells are used in applications where the user accepts the responsibility of managing cell safety through external means. The RC aviation community uses unprotected cells deliberately because the safety calculus is different than for consumer electronics. The cells are managed by the ESC and flight controller, which monitor cell voltages and react to them with sophisticated logic that depends on the flight state. Pilots accept the risk of cell damage because the alternative (a protection circuit shutting down power mid-flight) is worse. A multirotor with sudden power loss falls out of the sky; better to over-discharge a cell and have to throw it away than to crash a $2000 aircraft.

The Metapedal context is different in important ways. The user is a musician, not a trained battery operator. The pack will be handled by people who do not understand lithium chemistry. The cells will be sold separately from the pack and may come from any manufacturer. The use case is performance, not safety-critical flight. The consequences of a cell failure are property damage and possibly fire, not loss of an aircraft, with the user typically being in close proximity to the battery during use.

Given this, the right answer is for the pack to work with both protected and unprotected cells, with the pack's BMS providing the safety baseline regardless of which cells are used. Users who want the convenience and added safety of protected cells use them; users who want the slight performance benefits of unprotected cells use them; the pack works the same way in either case. This is similar to how flashlight manufacturers handle the same question: many quality flashlights work with either protected or unprotected 18650 cells, with the user making the choice based on their priorities.

The dual-BMS architecture actually makes this easier because each cell's BMS handles cell-level protection regardless of whether the cell itself has its own protection circuit. The pack provides robust safety for unprotected cells, and protected cells add a redundant layer of protection on top. The user can choose either type without any change to the pack itself. The pack's documentation should clearly explain the trade-offs and recommend protected cells for users who do not have specific reasons to prefer unprotected ones.

There is one capability inspired by the RC aviation context that is genuinely useful for live performance and worth adding as a configuration option. The default BMS behavior is to shut down at the cell's specified low-voltage cutoff (typically 3.0 volts for standard chemistry) to protect the cells from over-discharge. But for a performer in the middle of a song, this conservative behavior is exactly wrong: better to over-discharge a cell and throw it away than to lose power mid-performance. A "performance mode" configuration could be exposed through the parent module's interface that allows over-discharge to extend runtime, with clear warning that the affected cells may be damaged and need replacement. The default is the safe behavior; the user opts in to the aggressive behavior when the performance context warrants it. This matches the engineering philosophy that RC aviation uses: trust the operator to make context-aware decisions about whether to prioritize cell preservation or system uptime, with the system providing both options rather than only one.

The implementation is straightforward in the dual-BMS architecture because each cell's BMS chip can be configured for different low-voltage thresholds, and the parent module can communicate the desired mode over the inter-module bus. The user sets performance mode through a menu option or assigns it to a control source for runtime activation. The visual indicator on the pack can show whether performance mode is active, perhaps by adding a different color or flashing pattern to the cell-status LEDs. The user always knows whether their pack is in safe-default mode or in extended-runtime mode.

A small microcontroller (probably an STM32G0 in the dollar range) coordinates the high-level behavior, drives the LEDs, communicates with parent modules over USB-C, and manages the per-cell BMS chip configurations. The total bill of materials for the battery backpack electronics is approximately ten to twenty dollars (two BMS chips, two boost converters, ideal-diode OR stage, microcontroller, LEDs, cell holders with hot-swap contacts, USB-C and barrel jack output connectors, charging input, supporting passives) plus the cells themselves and the mechanical assembly. This is in line with what comparable consumer power banks cost to manufacture, with the hot-swap, per-cell indicator, and performance-mode features adding only a few dollars on top of the baseline.

## Cell Identity and History Tracking

A subtle but genuinely useful feature for non-expert users is per-cell identity tracking. The system identifies each cell through one of two methods, automatically choosing whichever is available: barcode scanning for cells that came with factory-printed 2D barcodes (most quality cells from responsible manufacturers do), and user-managed labeling with a Sharpie for cells without barcodes or with damaged wraps. The parent module's software prompts the user to identify which cell they have just inserted whenever the BMS detects an insertion event. From this, the system maintains a complete history per cell: when it was first registered, every insertion and removal event, the voltage and capacity at each event, the deepest discharge it has ever seen, the total cycles, and the date of the last charge. The user is identifying the cell (through scanning or labeling); the system is doing the tracking and analysis.

Most quality 18650 cells from Samsung, Sony/Murata, Panasonic, LG, Molicel, and other responsible manufacturers come with a Data Matrix 2D barcode printed on the cell wrap. The barcode encodes the manufacturer, the batch information, and a unique serial number for that specific cell. These barcodes exist for the manufacturers' own warranty and supply chain tracking, but they happen to be exactly what a cell-identification system needs. The user's phone, running the Metapedal companion app, can scan these barcodes through the phone camera using standard 2D barcode libraries (ZXing, ML Kit, libdmtx all handle Data Matrix decoding natively). When the user inserts new cells, the system prompts: "New cells detected. Scan them with your phone to identify." The user opens the app, points the camera at the cells, and the app reads both barcodes simultaneously and sends the identifications to the pack over Bluetooth or USB. The whole interaction takes a few seconds and requires no writing on cells or remembering which number is which.

For cells without barcodes (cheaper cells from less responsible manufacturers, or cells that have lost or damaged their wraps), the Sharpie approach is the fallback. The user writes a unique name or number on the cell with a permanent marker, the system prompts the user to identify which physical cell was just inserted at insertion time, and the rest of the tracking and analysis works the same way. The two identification methods coexist naturally in the system, with the user not needing to think about which method applies to which cell.

The barcode approach has an interesting side benefit beyond identification: counterfeit detection. The 18650 market has a real problem with counterfeit cells, and counterfeits are responsible for a significant fraction of lithium battery fires in consumer electronics. Authentic cells from responsible manufacturers can be validated against the manufacturer's database (or against a community database) using the serial numbers from their barcodes. The system can warn the user: "This cell does not match any known authentic cell from Samsung. It may be counterfeit. Counterfeit cells can fail catastrophically and are unsafe." The user makes an informed choice about whether to trust the cell, with information that most users have no other way to obtain. The counterfeit-detection feature requires an opt-in network query (the system needs to contact the manufacturer's API or a community database), so the default behavior is purely local identification with counterfeit detection available when the user wants it.

This pattern transfers the burden of remembering battery history from the user (who is unlikely to maintain that knowledge accurately) to the system (which is well-suited to maintain it), while keeping the user in the loop for the only action they can actually take: knowing which physical cell is which. For users with barcoded cells, the effort is essentially zero because the manufacturer has already done the labeling. For users with non-barcoded cells, the effort is small: writing a number with a Sharpie takes ten seconds and lasts the life of the cell.

The retirement recommendation is the key user-facing safety output. When the system decides a cell should be retired based on its history, it tells the user clearly: "Cell number 47 should be retired. It has been deep-discharged below 2.7 volts three times and its measured capacity has dropped to 60 percent of nominal. Please dispose of it safely at a battery recycling location." The user knows which physical cell to retire because the cell has a number written on it. The user does not need to understand why retirement is recommended; they just need to act on the recommendation. The system can also suggest specific disposal locations: IKEA, Home Depot, Lowe's, Best Buy, and many hardware stores accept lithium batteries for recycling, and the system can hint at these depending on the user's region. Some regions have specific battery recycling programs that the system can point to directly.

The system can also provide proactive warnings before retirement becomes necessary. If a cell is approaching the deep-discharge threshold that would trigger retirement, the system suggests swapping it earlier rather than letting it run to depletion: "Cell number 47 is at 3.0 volts and approaching deep discharge. Consider swapping to a fresh cell to extend its remaining life." This is the kind of advice that battery experts give themselves without thinking, but that non-experts cannot give themselves. The system supplies the expertise.

The integration with the performance-mode option is interesting. If the user has opted into performance mode (over-discharge allowed to extend runtime), the system can warn before pushing a specific cell into damage territory: "You are in performance mode. Continuing to draw from cell 47 will damage it. Press OK to acknowledge or pause to swap." The user makes the informed choice with full knowledge of consequences. The system trusts the user but gives them the information to be trustworthy.

For users with many cells across multiple battery packs, the system can present a cell-inventory dashboard showing which cells are charged, which are depleted, which are at risk of retirement, and which have unusual histories warranting attention. A serious touring musician might own ten to twenty cells across multiple packs, and managing this inventory becomes useful in the same way that managing guitar string inventory or cable inventory is useful. The dashboard is a tool for users who have invested in the platform seriously.

The system can also warn about cells that should not be installed together. While the dual-independent architecture handles mismatched states-of-charge cleanly, it does not magically handle cells with very different capacities or cells of different ages. The system can warn: "Cell 47 has 60 percent of nominal capacity and cell 23 has 90 percent of nominal capacity. The pack will work but cell 47 will deplete first and runtime will be shorter than with two matched cells." The user can choose to ignore the warning or to swap to better-matched cells.

The privacy and data-ownership angle matters and is worth being explicit about. The cell history is the user's data, stored locally on the user's device. The system does not phone home to a cloud service or share data with the manufacturer or with anyone else. The user can export their cell history if they want, or delete it, or transfer it to a different battery backpack if they buy a new one. The data belongs to the user, not to the platform.

The implementation requires storage for the cell history database, which is small (a few hundred bytes per cell, even for users with dozens of cells). The microSD card on the parent module is the natural storage location. The database can be synchronized across modules in a chain if multiple modules share battery backpack data, so the same cell history is available regardless of which module the backpack is connected to.

The cell labeling practice is not novel. The flashlight community has been doing this for years for exactly this kind of tracking purpose, and several power-bank products track cell history through user identification at insertion time. The Metapedal version brings this practice into the music-equipment context where users are even less likely to be battery experts and more likely to benefit from the system handling expertise for them.

An enhancement worth considering is using the cell's resistance signature as a fingerprint to cross-check the user's identification. Each cell has slightly different internal characteristics (internal resistance, time constants, voltage response curves) that can be measured at insertion time and used as an approximate identifier. The fingerprint is not reliable enough to identify a cell on its own, but it can be paired with user identification to detect mistakes. If the user says "this is cell 47" but the resistance signature does not match the stored signature for cell 47, the system can flag the discrepancy and ask the user to confirm. This catches cases where the user grabbed the wrong cell or mislabeled a cell. The cost is essentially zero because the BMS already has the measurement capability, and it adds a useful layer of robustness to the user-managed labeling system.

A wishlist note for the industry: the ideal solution to cell identification would be cell-side identification through a standardized communication protocol like 1-Wire or SMBus. Each protected 18650 would report its manufacturer, model number, serial number, manufacture date, lot number, and accumulated cycle history through a small communication interface integrated into the protection circuit under the button top. Laptop battery packs have had this capability since the late 1990s through the Smart Battery System specification, and power tool batteries from DeWalt, Milwaukee, and others all have similar internal interfaces. The technology is mature and the silicon is available; the obstacle is industry coordination. The 18650 form factor was standardized before smart-battery thinking emerged, and adding electrical identification capability would require changes to the cell, the holders, the chargers, and the devices that use them, breaking backward compatibility with the enormous installed base. This is a multi-year industry coordination effort that no single company can drive, and a music-equipment platform certainly cannot.

The barcode approach turns out to be a really nice intermediate solution because it captures most of the identification benefit (unique per-cell serial numbers from the manufacturer) without requiring any cell-side electronics or any breaking changes to the existing ecosystem. The information is already on the cell; it just needs to be read with a camera rather than through an electrical interface. The Metapedal platform leans into this opportunity by making barcode scanning the primary identification method, with the resistance-fingerprint enhancement providing cross-checks against user error, and the Sharpie approach as a fallback for cells without barcodes. If the industry ever does converge on electrical cell-side identification, the Metapedal platform can adopt it through a firmware update without changing any of the existing safety architecture; the barcode and Sharpie identification methods become redundant but would not need to be removed. The platform is forward-compatible with whatever the industry settles on, but does not depend on the industry changing anything to provide meaningful safety value today.

There is a question worth flagging about whether the battery pack should be a Metapedal-branded product or whether the project should simply recommend specific off-the-shelf USB-C power banks. The off-the-shelf approach has real advantages: lower cost for users, reduced manufacturing risk for the project, and choice from a competitive market. A Metapedal-branded version still has value for users who want the most polished portable solution with mounting hardware specifically designed for Metapedal modules, with the safety features (dual-BMS, hot-swap, cell tracking, performance mode) that generic power banks do not provide. Both approaches should probably coexist, with the project recommending generic power banks for users who want maximum flexibility and offering a branded version for users who want the integrated experience with full safety tracking.
