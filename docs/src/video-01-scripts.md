# Metapedal Video Scripts

This document is the production-ready script collection for the platform's five canonical launch videos. It contains only the scripts themselves, plus the minimal setting and framing necessary for a production team to begin storyboarding.

The strategic reasoning behind these scripts, the supporting analysis of why specific beats work, the stylistic conventions across the portfolio, and the production considerations are captured in a companion document: video-00-strategy.md. That document complements this one by providing the strategic context for the production decisions captured here. Production teams should read both: this document for what to shoot, the strategy document for why each beat exists and what each choice is meant to accomplish.

The five videos use consistent stylistic conventions documented in the strategy document. Briefly: over-the-shoulder POV throughout with face appearances reserved for character-to-character interaction; casual register; continuous portfolio-wide rhythm with character changes at climactic moments; non-language vocalization (humming, whistling, singing) carrying emotional content; dogs, cats, and babies present at the edges of the frame as life-in-the-shot texture.

A foundational production principle: the rhythm is in the driver's seat. The audio is composed first; the visual narrative gets adjusted to fit through speed ramps, slow-motion, and timing compression. Visual sequences may be sped up or slowed down to land on specific musical events. The editor's job is to lock the visual to the audio, not the other way around. The cast performs naturally during shooting and the editor handles the timing in post.

A recurring audio convention: plug-in moments across all five videos get a reversed cymbal swell that builds into the moment of contact. The "ssssSSIP" anticipation sound lands as the plug seats. This becomes the platform's audio signature for connections, marking each cable insertion as a small climactic event rather than an incidental action.

## Video One: From Drawer to Stage

The video opens on a walk. The user is walking their dog on a leash through their neighborhood. The audio is the texture of urban walking: traffic passing at variable speeds and distances, birds singing in trees, footsteps, the ambient hum of the city. The visual is whatever a walk looks like: sidewalk, houses or apartment buildings, trees, the dog ahead of the user pulling slightly on the leash.

Within the ambient audio, the user begins to notice a musical pattern. A bird's call combines with the rhythm of passing traffic to suggest a melodic phrase. The user's brain, always partly listening musically, finds the pattern and starts to repeat it under their breath. They hum a few notes, then a few more, working out the contours of the idea as they walk.

A small drum fill marks the moment the phrase first clicks into place. The fill is subtle: maybe a single roll across a small drum, or a brief shaker pattern landing on a beat. The transition is internal to the user; the world has not changed; the fill is correspondingly small. After this fill, the dog walk continues at quarter notes, but with slight urgency that was not present before.

The dog stops to do its business. The user stands there with the leash in one hand, the other hand free, working out the phrase out loud now. They hum it, try a variation, hum it again. A single snare hit lands at a specific moment, maybe when the dog finishes its business or when the user starts humming the phrase aloud for the first time. The hit is comedic without being broad; a small punctuation that says "this moment is the moment."

The user picks up after the dog. Then they start walking home, but faster now. The phrase has grown legs. They want to get home before it disappears. The dog matches their pace, picking up speed as the user does, possibly with the user encouraging them along ("come on, good dog").

The user humming or whistling continues as they walk, but the phrase is developing as they move. It is gaining notes, gaining shape, finding its actual form. By the time they reach their door, the phrase is substantially more developed than it was on the sidewalk. The viewer hears the idea growing through the user's vocalization.

They get inside their apartment. A medium-sized fill marks the threshold crossing: a couple of percussion events such as a tom hit or a hihat roll, with a small cymbal accent landing as the door closes behind them. The transition from outside to inside, ambient to enclosed, has been musically punctuated. The dog finds its usual spot. The user heads urgently to their bedroom studio, still repeating the phrase under their breath, trying to hold onto it while they get set up to record.

They reach the desk where their laptop is set up. They sit down and try to get to their music software so they can record the phrase. This is where the computer-shit begins.

The screen shows an update prompt that has appeared at the wrong moment: "DAWg Walk: Update available. Install now or restart later." The user dismisses it with growing frustration. They try to open their music software, but a login dialog appears: "Sign in to continue using DAWg Walk." The user has to type in their credentials to use software they paid for and use every day. A spinning loader follows the login. The cursor flips to a beachball briefly. Maybe a notification slides in to advertise some unrelated thing.

Throughout all of this, the user keeps repeating the musical phrase under their breath. But the phrase is changing: their irritation with the computer is warping it. The phrase shifts into a minor key and into a tricky time signature (probably 5/4 or 7/8). The melodic line that started as inspiration in 4/4 is becoming something darker AND meterically unstable, distorted in both pitch space and time space by the user's emotional state. They cannot help but keep producing the phrase, but the phrase is being corrupted by the obstacle between them and capturing it.

The cadence shifts at this moment. During the dog walk, the underlying pulse was at quarter notes: relaxed, comfortable, the pace of casual walking. The pulse was mostly carried by natural sounds (footsteps, the user's vocalization, the ambient city) with sparse percussion punctuating specific narrative moments: maybe a single snare hit when the dog finishes pooping, a soft ride tap when the phrase first comes together in the user's humming. When the user sits down and opens the laptop, the cadence doubles to eighth notes: tighter, more pressured, time subdividing faster as the user's attention narrows and tension builds. Closed or partial-open hihats are the natural pulse carrier for this phase because they capture the tense waiting quality; the typing rhythms, mouse clicks, and other diegetic sounds of the user's interaction with the computer do rhythmic work alongside the hihats. The hihats and the computer-shit keep counting time but the rest of the band has not arrived.

The user reaches their breaking point. They close the laptop. Then they reach down and pull open the desk drawer that sits directly below the work surface. With a casual sweep of their other hand, they slide the laptop sideways off the desk edge. The laptop falls into the open drawer below.

The laptop landing is the climactic fill of the video. The roughly 2.5 seconds covering the laptop falling into the drawer through to the BX2 hitting the desk should sound a bit like the famous tom fill from Phil Collins's "In The Air Tonight": a descending pattern across three to five toms with appropriate impact and possibly the gated reverb treatment that defines the original. Each visual action in the sequence lands on a tom hit: the laptop hitting the drawer bottom, the drawer closing, the user grabbing the BX2, the BX2 hitting the desk, the cable plugging in (with the final hit landing as a cymbal accent that bridges into the full kit's establishment). After the fill resolves, the new pattern is established: full drum kit, sixteenth note cadence, 4/4 time signature.

The pulse density has quadrupled since the dog walk and now serves flow rather than tension: the user has crossed into focused creative work, and the music's energy matches. The sixteenth notes are busy and energetic but fundamentally pleasant. The full drum kit stays in play through the rest of the studio sequence.

The kit is layered with diegetic sounds throughout. The drawer closing is the second beat. The BX2 being placed on the desk is the next beat. The USB-C power cable lifted is a beat. The cable plugging in is a beat. The 100ms boot happens within a single beat. The OLED ready-to-play appears on the next beat. The microphone plugged in on a beat. The guitar plugged in on a beat. The cheap-ass speakers plugged in on a beat. Every action is a beat, and each diegetic sound integrates with the kit's pattern.

The user picks up the BX2 and plugs it into the same USB-C power cord that was powering the laptop. The cord did not move; the BX2 takes over the laptop's spot in the user's setup.

The BX2 boots in approximately 100 milliseconds. A stopwatch graphic overlays the screen with the sweep hand at zero; the sweep hand never gets to tick once. Split screen: stopwatch at zero, OLED already showing ready-to-play.

The user picks up the BX2 and does a tiny bit of menu diving to bring up a mic-plus-guitar solo preset. As they navigate, they hum the phrase again, and now it is back in its original key. The platform has not corrupted it; the obstacle is gone.

They plug in a microphone, then a guitar, then cheap-ass powered speakers.

They hit record on the platform and let loose in full song for a second or two. The phrase that has been carrying through the video finally gets released: sung properly, fully, with the user committing to it now that they have the platform recording it.

The user hits a chord, mutters something about the sound, does another five seconds of menu diving to grab the right guitar and vocal effects. As they enter the effect selection menu, the video shifts into a brief stylized montage of input testing.

The montage is staccato: hard cuts between very similar visual sequences, choppy audio, compressed time. Short percussive bursts of guitar pluck (each lasting 150-200ms in edit duration), alternating with short bursts of vocal (also 150-200ms). The visual cuts on each subdivision: hand on guitar from one angle, cut, hand on guitar from a slightly different angle, cut, hand on mic, cut, hand back on guitar. The editor compresses the user's actual input testing into a music-video-style sequence that lasts a few seconds total. The OLED shows captures landing as small waveforms that dissolve into the next.

The final burst in each category lasts longer in the edit, about 300-450ms. The longer duration on the audio combined with a slightly held shot signals that this is the commit capture: the snippet that will be used for previewing in that channel.

The compression is video editing style rather than a depiction of literal workflow pace. The real-world use case is more leisurely: a musician would take ten to fifteen seconds to test inputs naturally, playing a chord, listening, adjusting, playing again, settling on usable captures over the kind of timescale that real input testing actually requires. The video compresses this into staccato montage for kinetic energy and to keep the sequence inside the song's musical time. The cast performs at natural pace during shooting; the editor creates the stuttering compression in post.

After the montage resolves, the user has captured snippets in both channels: a guitar chord and a vocal phrase. The video returns to more naturalistic pace for the preview-while-scrolling phase that follows.

As the user scrolls through effects options, each highlighted effect plays a 100-250ms preview using the captured snippet as the source. The user hears their own captured chord transformed through each guitar effect: a brief snippet of the captured chord clean, then through heavy distortion, then through thick chorus, then through hall reverb, in rapid succession as they scroll. The user might switch to vocal effects briefly, hearing the captured vocal phrase through reverb, compression, pitch shift. The OLED indicates which channel the current effect applies to. The user navigates back and forth between guitar and vocal effects as their selection logic requires, stopping on the right effects in each channel.

The previews are the user's own playing transformed, not generic samples. This is the platform's design philosophy made visible: the effect-preview workflow is built around the user's actual playing rather than around pre-recorded demo material. The multi-channel capability (guitar and mic both active simultaneously, each with its own captured snippet) is shown through the user's natural exploration during the preview phase.

The mic check reveals one thing the user wants to refine. They dive one level deeper into the menu, into the parameters of the effect they just selected. They tweak something specific: the reverb decay, or the chorus depth, or the amp simulation's mid-range EQ. They hit a chord one more time to verify. The sound is now right. They press one more button to save the customized preset under a name they will remember. The screen briefly shows "saved" or a similar confirmation.

The camera zooms out. The user picks up the BX2 and places it on the floor. As they do this, the rhythm pulls back. The full kit recedes; the sixteenth note hihats give way to sparse closed hihats; the busy energy of the studio phase gives way to quiet anticipation. The viewer feels the user gathering themselves before the public performance moment. The same closed hihat that carried the computer-shit tension now carries positive anticipation; the texture is similar but the emotional content is reversed.

At the same moment the rhythm pulls back, the video quality and color grading begin to shift. The bedroom studio's clean lighting and balanced grading give way to something different during the BX2's movement: bright spots beginning to bloom, dark areas gaining visible noise, the dynamic range compressing, the color grading pushing in some direction.

The BX2 lands on the floor. The camera holds on the pedal for about 200ms. The pedal sits there alone on a surface that the viewer now realizes is not the bedroom floor; the visual quality issues are fully visible (bloom, noise, color shift); the cause is not yet clear. The user's foot is visible at the edge of frame. This 200ms hold is the suspended moment of mystery: long enough to register as a deliberate beat, short enough to maintain the staccato energy.

During this 200ms hold, the OLED plays a fun animation: the platform's logo coming to life. The two tin cans separate from each other; the string between them becomes taut; the cans get a cartoonish scale transform applied as if they are rebounding from the sudden tension in the string. The cans expand briefly when the string snaps tight, then contract back, the visual language of classic 2D animation's stretch-and-squash applied to the platform's logo elements. The animation completes just as the stomp triggers the reveal. The small bright OLED in the middle of the otherwise mysterious visual register draws the viewer's eye; the animation gives them something to focus on during the suspended moment.

Then the stomp. The user's foot comes down on the footswitch. A massive TR-909 kick (or equivalent) lands exactly on the impact. The camera pans up. The audience appears in the same motion. Total silence for one or two beats follows the kick. The visual quality shift now makes sense: those are stage lights causing the bloom, this is a dark venue causing the noise, the user has been teleported from the bedroom studio to a stage. The diegetic reality of the stage has taken over from the orchestrated soundtrack.

The silence breaks as crowd noise fills in: cheers, claps, whistles, the ambient sound of an audience in a venue. The user has crossed the threshold from private creator to public performer.

The user says "hello Cleveland." As they say it, a huge fill begins building from the silence: drums entering softly, gaining intensity, building toward a peak that would launch into the song's main body. The fill is conventional rock or pop in character (different from the descending tom fill at the laptop drop), rolling around the kit with rising energy.

At the moment the fill reaches its peak, just before the song's main body would land, the audio cuts to silence. Sharply. The song the viewer has been waiting for throughout the entire video is now happening, but the video does not stay to hear it. The viewer's imagination fills in what the song sounds like; the platform's marketing trusts the viewer to complete the experience.

Fast fade to black. Marketing text appears in the silence: the platform's logo, a tagline, the URL or call to action. The brand acknowledgment lands when the viewer is most receptive because the silence has made the brand the only thing happening.

The dog has been present throughout the studio sequence, eventually following the user to the door and settling in the doorway as the user heads to the stage.

The dog has been present throughout the studio sequence, snoozing on the floor, occasionally raising its head at noises, eventually following the user to the door and settling in the doorway as the user heads to the stage.

## Video Two: Band In A Can

The video opens one month after the first. The same user from the first video is shown at home again, this time with additions to their rig. An accelerometer is mounted on the headstock of their guitar; a breath sensor sits near their microphone. As each accessory is shown, a cash register sound plays with the price displayed: a few dollars for the accelerometer, a few more for the breath sensor.

The user then tests each accessory in turn and the audio results are kickass. They play a sustained chord and then physically move the guitar's neck the way guitarists naturally do when they want a note to feel more alive; the audio responds with vibrato or a filter sweep or pitch wobble that follows the neck motion. The user smiles or nods slightly at how good it sounds.

Then they take a breath in, sing a phrase, and the audio responds to the breath itself. The reverb blooms on the breath, or a synth pad swells, or the compressor responds to breath dynamics. The user's expression registers the genuine musical surprise of hearing their own breath shape the sound.

The camera then cuts to a very low angle and follows a USB-C cable along the floor. The cable becomes a character in the shot, leading the viewer's eye from the first user's BX2 to somewhere else. The cable runs along the floor for several seconds, building anticipation about what is at the other end.

The cable terminates at a second pedal. This pedal is visibly different from the BX2: a different color, with stickers on it that indicate personal history. It is an A module. The cash register sound plays again, this time with a $50 price tag.

The camera pans up to reveal a second musician on stage with the A module at their feet. They say "Hi Toronto, we're Band In A Can. Enjoy the show." Then they shred into the opening of a song with energy and skill that signal real musical seriousness.

The shot zooms out. The visible stage rig is minimal: two pedals total, one power cord serving both, TRRS cables running to each musician's stage monitors, the BX2's combo jack outputs feeding the venue's PA system.

The video ends with the band playing the song.

## Video Three: The Hardware Hacker

### The Setting

The setting is a makerspace or a hardware hacker's bedroom workshop. The space is recognizably a place where hardware work happens: soldering iron on the bench, oscilloscope on a shelf, junk drawer of components visible, maybe other projects in various states of completion.

### The Script

The hacker shows the consumer object they are starting from. Held briefly up to camera, maybe with a slight smirk. The object is recognizably cheap and consumer-grade: a Theremin toy from a science kit, an old game controller, whatever they have decided to repurpose.

The disassembly montage. Quick cuts of the hacker opening the object, identifying useful components, salvaging the parts they need. Screws being removed, plastic shells being pried open, circuit boards being inspected.

The integration with Metapedal. The salvaged components get wired to an M8 connector through a small protoboard. The hacker is comfortable with the soldering iron; the work is competent and quick.

The hacker finishes the last solder joint. They flip their oscilloscope on and do a brief one or two second test, probing the new peripheral's output to verify the signal is what they expected. Brief shot of the oscilloscope trace showing a clean signal varying as the hacker waves their hand near the Theremin's antenna.

Then immediately to the Metapedal. The hacker plugs the M8 connector into the Metapedal module. A second or two of menu diving on the OLED to configure the Theremin's two antennas in the classical way: vertical hand position controls pitch, horizontal hand position controls volume. Then they wave their hands in the space around the antennas and the Metapedal module produces the eerie, expressive, slightly unsettling sound that everyone recognizes as classical Theremin music.

The hacker plays for a moment, hands gliding through space, the audio responding with the smooth pitch glissandos that only a Theremin can produce. The hacker smiles. The build works musically, not just technically.

And then the social moment. Someone else in the space hears what is going on. They walk over to see what the noise is. They have a baby in a sling strapped to their chest, plus a small Eurorack modular synthesizer setup. They watch the Theremin hacker play for a moment, then notice the TRS jack on the Metapedal module.

"Can I plug in my MIDI?"

The Theremin hacker shrugs slightly. "I don't know. Probably. Give it a shot."

The Eurorack person plugs a TRS-MIDI cable from their modular into the Metapedal's TRRS jack. Within seconds, the platform recognizes the new MIDI source. The Eurorack person triggers a sequence on their modular; structured rhythmic patterns and harmonic content begin flowing into the makerspace.

Now the Theremin hacker reconfigures the routing graph mid-session, taking maybe ten seconds in the OLED menu. The Theremin's two antennas no longer drive pitch and volume of a synth voice; they now drive parameters on the audio coming from the Eurorack. Vertical hand position controls a filter cutoff applied to the modular's output; horizontal hand position controls the filter's resonance. The Theremin has transformed from a sound source into an expression controller shaping the modular synth's sound.

The musical effect is striking. The Eurorack person provides sequenced notes and harmonic structure; the Theremin hacker shapes the resulting sound dynamically with hand motion. The combination of vertical and horizontal motion produces complex tonal evolution.

The video ends with the two of them playing together, locked into a groove neither of them planned. The baby is inches from the music being made, present at the spontaneous collaboration.

## Video Four: The Thrift Store Discovery

The video opens in a thrift shop. Browsing montage: the user walking through aisles past various electronics, used CDs, old appliances. The shot lingers briefly on items that almost catch the user's eye before they move on.

The user finds a USB MIDI controller. A cheap 37-key mini-key keyboard, plastic build. The user picks it up, blows the dust off, peels off a piece of masking tape that has a $10 price tag handwritten on it.

They look around. Down on the next shelf there is a pair of cheap powered speakers with a $5 masking tape price tag. The user picks them up and inspects them. The speakers are powered but there is no power adapter included.

The user turns around. They find the bin of orphaned power adapters at $1 each and start digging. The over-the-shoulder POV captures hands sorting through the tangle: pulling out one adapter, checking the voltage and polarity and connector size against the speaker's requirements, putting it aside, pulling out another.

After some seconds of digging, the user finds the right one. The connector matches; the voltage matches; the polarity matches. They hold up the adapter briefly with a small sound of satisfaction.

Rather than leaving the store to set up at home, the user does the demonstration right there. They pull a gas-station-grade USB-C power bank out of their bag. They plug the power bank into a Metapedal module they also have in their bag. The module powers up. They plug the thrift store controller into the module via a USB-A-to-USB-C adapter. They plug the cheap powered speakers into the module's combo jack output, with the just-found $1 power adapter providing the speakers' juice.

The user is still in the thrift store aisle, surrounded by other shoppers continuing their browsing.

The Metapedal module detects the new USB MIDI controller automatically. Brief shot of the OLED showing the controller as a new MIDI input source.

The user does a few seconds of menu diving. They navigate to the synthesizer menu and select the DX7 emulator. They play a chord. The familiar DX7 electric piano sound fills the immediate area of the thrift store. A couple of other shoppers glance over, slightly confused.

The user explores the DX7's voice bank: the iconic electric piano, FM bass, BRASS, BELL. They mutter exploratory things. The thrift store cat that lives at the store walks over, curious about the activity. At some point the cat strolls across the keyboard; random keys depress under the cat's paws; the platform produces sounds the user did not intend. The user gently moves the cat aside and continues.

Back to the menus. They navigate to the Hammond emulator. They hit a chord. The Hammond's warm rotating tone fills the thrift store space.

"Fuck yeah."

The video cuts to a new shot: a wider angle showing the original user with their assembled rig on one side of a thrift store shelf, and another character on the other side of the same shelf, both visible in the same frame. The cameo character is someone viewers will recognize from a previous video.

The two characters spot each other across the shelf. The cameo character's eyes land on the assembled rig with the platform module at its center.

"Oh, Metapedal. Sweet."

The original user gives a small nod. Both characters' faces are visible in this shot.

Cut to the next shot: the two characters are now side by side at the original user's rig. The shelf is no longer between them. The cameo character pulls their own Metapedal module from their bag, plus a dollar-store-grade USB-C cable. They plug the cable into the original user's Metapedal and into their own.

The two strangers jam together briefly in the thrift store. Other shoppers stop to listen. The thrift store cat watches from a nearby shelf.

The video ends with them playing, surrounded by junk shelves and used appliances.

## Video Five: The Album

### The Setting

The video opens in a basement. Not a professional recording studio and not a stage. The lived-in quality of the basement matches the platform's broader video aesthetic: real gear assembled from various sources, the casual mess of actual creative work.

### The Cast

The cast comes from the previous four videos:

The laptop-banishing musician from video one as one of the lead vocalists or guitarists.

The Band In A Can musicians from video two as additional members of the expanded ensemble, with their characteristic sticker-covered A module visible.

The hardware hacker from video three with their modified Theremin or expression controller.

The Eurorack person from video three contributing modular synth content. The toddler from video three's baby (a year older now) is in a playpen in the corner.

The thrift store musician from video four with their cheap controller now feeding DX7 or Hammond voices into the recording.

The cameo character from video four returning here in fuller form.

### The Script: Basement Session

The session captures six to eight separate audio tracks simultaneously, each one recorded with the appropriate processing for that instrument or voice. The platform handles this through multiple modules chained together; each performer has their own module appropriate to their role.

The session is simultaneously being livestreamed. The video might show brief moments of chat reacting to the music: a comment or two scrolling by on a phone or laptop monitoring the stream.

Faces appear during the session as the cast members react to each other's playing, share glances about musical decisions, communicate the energy of collaborative work. The basement session is fundamentally about human connection.

The keyboardist member of the band has their dog snoozing on a couch beside their keyboard rig. A dog lies on the floor in a quiet spot. The toddler in the playpen is occasionally looked after by whoever is not currently playing.

### The Script: Next Morning

The video transitions to the next morning. The laptop-banishing musician from video one is at home, sitting at the desk where it all started. They reach down and open the same desk drawer the laptop was banished into. They reach in carefully and lift the laptop out, setting it gently on the desk surface where the BX2 had been during the first video.

Before opening the laptop, the user reaches for a small Metapedal sticker and presses it onto the laptop's lid with a quick thumb press. The laptop now bears the platform's mark.

They open the laptop and the screen shows the same kind of computer-shit the first video established: DAWg Walk update prompts, login requirements, the usual friction. The video fast-forwards through it. A cup of coffee in the corner of the shot empties as the fast-forward continues.

The user clicks the button to allow the computer to reboot. They go offscreen for a few seconds, still in fast-forward. The audio fills in the gap: the sound of an espresso grinder, the hiss of a steam wand, milk being frothed.

The user returns to frame just as the computer finishes rebooting. They are carrying a beautiful latte in a real mug, possibly with latte art. The latte sits next to the laptop as both objects come ready for the work that is about to begin.

The user's dog is at their feet again (callback to video one's home studio), the same dog that walked with them during the inspiration moment in video one.

They plug a Metapedal module into the laptop via USB-C. They pull the multi-track stems off the module's microSD card. The transfer happens through the platform's USB mass storage capability.

They open DAWg Walk on the laptop. They import the stems. They start working on the album, latte in hand, dog at their feet.

The video might briefly show the producer making mixing decisions, adjusting levels, applying mastering processing, listening to the result.

The video ends with the producer hitting render on the finished song, or hearing the mastered track for the first time. The album exists.
