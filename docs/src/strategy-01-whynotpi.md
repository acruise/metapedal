## The Raspberry Pi Comparison

A natural objection to the H7 choice is "why not just use a Raspberry Pi?" The Pi 4 has roughly six to twelve times the CPU compute of an STM32H7, plus one to eight gigabytes of RAM versus the H7's roughly one megabyte of SRAM. The compute headroom is genuinely vast, and the price of a base Pi 4 is similar to the H7-based motherboard target. So it is worth being explicit about what a Pi-based platform can and cannot do, since this comparison is the obvious one critics will make.

On audio latency, the Pi 4 can achieve sub-4-millisecond round-trip latency with a good USB audio device on a stock PREEMPT kernel, according to the PiPedal project that has put real work into measuring this. Aggressive tuning with JACK and careful configuration can push this down to around 2.7 milliseconds for the audio period, comparable to what the H7 achieves at a 32-sample block size. So the Pi's absolute latency numbers can match the H7's numbers in best-case configurations. The Pi can run guitar effects with playable latency.

But the Pi's numbers are best-case with potential variability from filesystem activity, network traffic, power management state transitions, and other system events. The PiPedal documentation explicitly notes that filesystem activity affects USB audio even with an RT_PREEMPT kernel, and that there are outstanding issues with the Broadcom 2711 PCI Express bus drivers on Raspberry Pi OS kernels that "may require building a realtime kernel with all power-saving options disabled" to work around. The Pi can be tuned for stable low-latency audio, but the tuning is real engineering work that has to be done and maintained, and the result is still less deterministic than what the H7 delivers out of the box. The H7's numbers are simply what the hardware does, with no kernel patches, no JACK configuration, no isolated USB buses, no power-saving workarounds.

On audio quality from the built-in jack, the Pi is genuinely weak. The Pi's 3.5mm headphone output is a PWM-based output with only 11 bits of effective resolution at 48 kilohertz, giving approximately 66 decibels of theoretical signal-to-noise ratio. Pi engineers have described it as "vaguely equivalent to FM radio" quality, which is well below CD quality (16 bits, 96 dB SNR). This is fine for system sounds and casual listening but not adequate for any serious audio work. For real audio quality, a Pi-based product needs either a USB audio interface, a HAT-based audio board with proper DAC and ADC chips, or I2S output to an external codec. All of these add hardware that is similar in cost and complexity to what Metapedal already includes natively on the B mezzanine.

The HDMI output is technically another option for high-quality digital audio from a Pi. 48 kilohertz 24-bit audio is well within what HDMI supports, with the digital samples embedded in the HDMI stream alongside video data. But HDMI audio has catastrophic latency for the guitar effects use case. The HDMI receiver buffers significant amounts of audio and video to handle its video processing pipeline, with TVs typically adding 50 to 200 milliseconds of audio latency and even monitors or AV receivers adding 20 to 50 milliseconds. For a player who wants to hit a string and hear the result with sub-10-millisecond latency, adding 20 to 200 milliseconds on the output side alone makes the system unusable. HDMI audio is a real option for high-quality output from a Pi, but the latency rules it out for live audio processing. This is actually a nice illustration of the Pi's broader audio dilemma: the built-in 3.5mm jack has acceptable latency but unacceptable quality, the HDMI output has acceptable quality but unacceptable latency, and getting both requires adding external audio hardware that brings the cost up to where Metapedal already sits.

The complete cost comparison is therefore not Pi versus Metapedal motherboard, but Pi-plus-audio-hardware versus Metapedal-motherboard-plus-mezzanine. A Pi 4 plus a competent audio HAT runs perhaps sixty to ninety dollars before any enclosure, footswitch, display, controls, or inter-module connectivity. Adding all the rest of what makes a guitar pedal actually usable, the total cost ends up similar to or higher than the equivalent Metapedal module, with less determinism and substantially more software maintenance burden.

The product experience is the larger issue, even if cost were equal. A Pi-based product is a small computer running Linux that has been configured to behave like a pedal. It boots in seconds rather than milliseconds. It has a filesystem that can become corrupted. It needs kernel updates, distribution updates, library version compatibility management. It runs background processes that can interfere with audio. It needs careful shutdown rather than just being unplugged. The whole user experience is fundamentally a computer pretending to be an appliance, with all the maintenance burdens that implies. The H7-based architecture is a true appliance: power on, instant start, work indefinitely, power off by unplugging, no maintenance ever.

The Pi audio market does exist and includes successful products. Critter and Guitari's Organelle is a Pi-based instrument with years of market presence. PiPedal is a popular open-source guitar effects platform for the Pi. Pisound and similar HAT-based products serve specific use cases. These products are real and useful. But they are mostly aimed at experimental musicians and DIY hobbyists who tolerate the Linux complexity in exchange for platform flexibility and the compute headroom. They are not aimed at mainstream guitarists who want a pedal that just works. Metapedal is trying to serve a broader audience that includes both the experimental fringe and the mainstream, and the H7-based architecture is what makes the mainstream serving possible.

The conclusion is that the Pi is a more capable computer than the H7 by a wide margin, but it is the wrong computer for the job. The job is deterministic real-time audio processing in an appliance-grade product that boots instantly, runs forever without maintenance, and survives being stomped on. The H7 does this job naturally. The Pi can be coerced into doing parts of this job with substantial engineering effort and ongoing limitations, but the result is always going to feel more like a small computer than a pedal, which is exactly the user experience Metapedal is trying to avoid.

The right class of part is something like the STM32H7 family. These are Cortex-M7 microcontrollers running at up to four or five hundred megahertz, with substantial on-chip static RAM, double-precision floating point hardware, and serious digital signal processing instruction support. They boot in milliseconds. They run a single firmware image with no operating system to speak of, or at most a small real-time kernel like FreeRTOS or Zephyr providing scheduling and synchronization primitives. They have well-documented peripherals. They have audio-relevant features built in, like Serial Audio Interface blocks that can talk to external audio codecs at the appropriate sample rates without significant CPU involvement. And they are reasonably priced in production quantities, in the five to ten dollar range depending on the specific part and the order size.

The audio path benefits from a consolidated approach using a single audio codec chip that integrates both audio ADC and audio DAC into one package. The professional audio industry has many such parts, from Cirrus Logic, Texas Instruments, AKM, and others, all in the twenty-four bit, ninety-six kilohertz, stereo, three to five dollar range, talking I-squared-S or time-division-multiplexed audio to the microcontroller's serial audio peripheral. Using a single codec chip rather than separate ADC and DAC parts simplifies the bill of materials, simplifies the board layout, simplifies the firmware, and gives matched performance between the input and output paths.

Once the audio path is consolidated into a single codec chip, the question of variant motherboards becomes interesting. The traditional approach used by products like the Raspberry Pi is one PCB design with optional components populated differently for different variants, with the variant difference captured in the bill of materials. This works but has real manufacturing costs because the assembly process has to handle multiple component configurations on the same board, and inventory has to track which configuration each board is in.

A cleaner approach for Metapedal is to have one truly identical motherboard for all variants, with the B-specific audio circuitry living on a hard-soldered mezzanine board that attaches to the motherboard at manufacturing time. The motherboard is one product with one bill of materials and one assembly process. The mezzanine is a separate product. The variant is determined by whether a mezzanine is attached and which mezzanine is attached.

The mezzanine attaches through a board-to-board connector pair rather than through castellated edge connections or a flex cable. A fine-pitch board-to-board connector like the Hirose DF12 family or the Samtec ERM/ERF series, at 0.5 mm pitch with 50 pins, provides a snap-fit mechanical attachment plus the electrical signal path between motherboard and mezzanine. The 50-pin connector at 0.5 mm pitch spans about 25 mm, which fits comfortably on a small pedal-sized mezzanine. The cost per matched connector pair is about $1.80-3 in production quantities, which is real money on the BOM but well worth the benefits the connector approach provides over alternatives.

The board-to-board connector pattern is well-established in modern electronics. Smartphone and tablet manufacturers use fine-pitch board-to-board connectors throughout their products because they enable modular construction where individual subassemblies can be tested, replaced, and upgraded independently. The connectors are designed for high-density signal routing with controlled impedance, repeated mating cycles (typically 50-100 cycles rated), and substantial mechanical retention force that holds the boards together without requiring additional fasteners.

The board-to-board approach is the right choice for several reasons beyond the cost trade-off. Cable-based daughter boards have all the problems of any high-impedance signal path: the cable acts as an antenna for noise pickup, the connector at each end adds reliability concerns, and the impedance discontinuities at cable transitions degrade signal integrity for higher-speed signals like the I-squared-S audio interface. Castellated edge connections (the alternative we initially considered) are essentially zero-cost in connector terms but require permanent solder attachment, which makes the mezzanine and motherboard a single unrepairable assembly. The board-to-board connector approach gives signal integrity comparable to castellated edges through controlled-impedance fine-pitch contacts, while keeping the assembly separable for repair, upgrade, and prototyping.

The separability is genuinely valuable for the platform. If a mezzanine fails, the user (or a repair shop) can unmate the connector, replace just the mezzanine, and remate. With castellated construction the entire motherboard-plus-mezzanine assembly would have to be reworked or replaced. A motherboard manufacturer can produce motherboards once and ship them to a different facility for mezzanine attachment, decoupling the manufacturing supply chain. A hobbyist with a soldering iron can solder the connector to a custom mezzanine they designed, mate it with a standard motherboard, and have a working module without aligning castellations precisely during soldering (which is genuinely difficult for hand assembly). A designer iterating on a mezzanine can swap prototypes against the same motherboard during development.

The mezzanine concept applies specifically to the B variants and not to the A variant. The A variant's audio circuitry is cheap and routine: a basic DAC for the headphone output, a basic microphone preamp into the H7's built-in ADC, the TRRS jack, and the supporting passives. All of this lives directly on the motherboard with no mezzanine needed. The A variant is a complete product as a bare motherboard with these components populated, with no additional board to manufacture, attach, or test.

The B variant adds a mezzanine that brings the audio codec, the quarter-inch or combo jacks, and the more careful analog circuitry that the B use cases need. The mezzanine plugs into the board-to-board connector on the motherboard. On B variant builds, the A-specific audio components are not populated, because the codec on the mezzanine takes over the audio I/O role and the dedicated DAC would conflict with it. On A variant builds, no mezzanine is attached and the A-specific components are populated in that same physical area. The two configurations are mutually exclusive on a per-board basis, with the same PCB area serving one or the other.

This is the classic Raspberry Pi A/B model, with a snap-fit mezzanine that the manufacturer (or in some cases the end user) can install or remove. The motherboard is one PCB design with optional components and an optional mezzanine attachment. The A variant skips the mezzanine and populates the simple analog components. The B variant skips the simple analog components and attaches the mezzanine instead. Manufacturing tracks which configuration each board is built in, and the resulting product is either an A or a B depending on the choices made at assembly.

The mezzanines each carry their own analog circuitry with their own analog ground plane on the mezzanine PCB. The motherboard's digital ground plane and the mezzanine's analog ground plane meet at the board-to-board connector, with multiple connector pins dedicated to ground for the grounding topology described later in this document. The connection inductance and physical separation at the connector provide inherent isolation between the digital noise sources on the motherboard and the more sensitive analog circuitry on the mezzanine. The mezzanine can be optimized for its analog needs, and the motherboard does not need to compromise its digital layout for analog concerns it does not have.

The TRRS jack lives on the motherboard for both variants, since it is wired to either the A-specific DAC and microphone preamp directly (A variant) or to the codec on the mezzanine through the motherboard-to-mezzanine signal interface (B variant). This means the back panel of the enclosure has a TRRS cutout in the same location regardless of variant, simplifying enclosure design.

The signals crossing the motherboard-to-mezzanine boundary include the I-squared-S audio interface between the codec and the H7's SAI peripheral, the codec's control interface (I-squared-C or SPI), the routing to the TRRS jack on the motherboard, power rails for the analog circuitry (3.3V analog, 5V for headphone amplifier supply and phantom power generation), ground returns for both digital reference and analog reference, and various control signals for codec configuration and phantom power enable. Total pin count plus generalization headroom comes to 50 pins, which fits the 50-pin board-to-board connector specification described later.

The mezzanine size depends on what is on it. The codec itself is a small surface-mount package. The analog circuitry around it adds some space. The quarter-inch jacks and combo jacks are mechanically significant components that need to extend through the enclosure's back panel. A B1 mezzanine with one combo jack might be around 40 by 60 millimeters. A B2 mezzanine with two combo jacks is somewhat larger. The mezzanine size sets the area of motherboard real estate dedicated to the B variant audio circuitry.

The mezzanine concept generalizes nicely beyond just the B family. Different mezzanines can extend the platform in different directions. A mezzanine with onboard DSP acceleration provides external DSP capability. A mezzanine with CV/gate interface adds modular synthesizer compatibility. A mezzanine with specialized I/O extends the module's interface set for specific applications. Each mezzanine is a separate product that plugs into the standard motherboard connector, with the resulting module being a specific configuration in the platform's product family. The motherboard remains stable across all of these variants, with the mezzanines being the locus of product differentiation for any variant beyond the base A.

The board-to-board connector approach makes field upgrades genuinely practical. A user who buys an A variant and later decides they want B variant capability can perform the upgrade themselves with modest soldering skill: desolder the A-specific audio components (the DAC, the headphone amp, the microphone preamp) from the motherboard, and plug in a B mezzanine to the connector. The desoldering step is the harder part because it involves removing small surface-mount components, but it is within what a hobbyist with hot air rework or careful iron technique can accomplish. The mezzanine attachment is just plugging in the connector. The total upgrade cost is the mezzanine price plus perhaps $15-30 in labor at a repair shop, or self-installation time for users with the equipment.

This changes the product proposition in some useful ways. A user unsure whether they need B variant capability can start with an A variant and upgrade later if their needs change. The cost of being wrong about the initial purchase is lower because the wrong choice is recoverable. Used A variants become more attractive on the secondhand market because the upgrade path exists. And the platform's longevity improves because users can refresh specific parts of their gear rather than replacing whole modules.

The connector specification also opens up a category of "mezzanine kits" that the platform or third parties can sell. A user with an A variant motherboard can buy a B mezzanine kit (the mezzanine itself plus instructions for removing the A-specific components and installing the mezzanine), perform the upgrade themselves over a weekend, and effectively have a B variant. This is the kind of user-empowering capability that distinguishes open-ecosystem platforms from closed commercial products, where comparable upgrades typically require replacing the whole product.

The hand-solderable mezzanine interface also opens up space for third-party and DIY mezzanine designs. The motherboard's open specification includes the mezzanine attachment pad layout and signal definitions, so anyone can develop conforming mezzanines. A user with electronics skill can design and build a mezzanine for a specific application: a custom CV/gate interface for modular synth integration, a specialized I/O panel for an unusual instrument, a high-end audio variant with premium codec and analog components beyond what the official mezzanines offer. The same way the pin header inside the enclosure provides an extension point for hardcore hacker modifications, the mezzanine interface becomes a higher-level extension point for users who want to alter the module's core audio capabilities.

A manufacturing flexibility angle also emerges. A small manufacturer wanting to offer Metapedal B variant modules can buy motherboards from a primary supplier and then attach their own specialty mezzanines to create differentiated products. The barrier to entry for being a Metapedal B variant manufacturer drops significantly because the engineering-intensive motherboard work is done by someone else, and the differentiation work happens at a complexity level that small operations can handle. This is the open hardware ecosystem story working as it should: a stable shared foundation with a vibrant market of derivative products built on top.

The mezzanine interface itself becomes a long-term architectural commitment that needs careful design. The pad layout, the signal definitions, the power rails available to the mezzanine, the mechanical envelope, and any handshake or identification protocols all need to be specified in a way that supports both current and future mezzanine designs without breaking compatibility. This is the same consideration that applies to the pin header pinout, which we already flagged as an open question worth getting right the first time. The mezzanine interface deserves the same treatment.

So the standard motherboard is something like this. A Cortex-M7 microcontroller, probably an STM32H7 family part, provides the processing. Its built-in twelve-bit DAC handles control voltage outputs. Its built-in low-resolution ADC handles knob positions and other slow control inputs. A small OLED display interface, the inter-module communication interface, the pin header, the microSD card slot, the standard power regulation circuitry, and the TRRS jack are all populated on every motherboard. The A-specific audio components (basic DAC for headphone output, basic microphone preamp for the TRRS microphone input) are populated only on A variant builds; on B variant builds, those components are skipped and a B mezzanine is attached instead.
