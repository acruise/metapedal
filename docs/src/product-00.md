# Metapedal: Vision and User Experience

This document describes what Metapedal does for musicians: what kind of system it is, what users can do with it, what the user experience looks like, and how it fits into musical workflows. The product strategy and architectural rationale (why microcontroller rather than small computer, why open-ecosystem rather than proprietary, market positioning, open questions) lives in a separate strategy document. The detailed engineering specifications live in the technical document.

## What Metapedal Is

Metapedal is a working name for an open hardware and protocol specification for guitar effects pedals and related audio processing devices, organized around a single core idea: the control surface that a musician uses to shape a sound need not be part of the same physical device that actually processes the audio signal. This is a decoupling, in the same intellectual tradition as separating policy from mechanism, or separating control planes from data planes in distributed systems. The name reflects this. It is a pedal about pedals, a meta-pedal, a framework within which conventional pedals are just one possible configuration.

The motivating example is to imagine a theremin used as a control surface. A theremin is a beautiful instrument because the player's hand position in the air becomes the entire musical interface, with no physical contact required. But the theremin is also a complete instrument, with its own oscillator and its own characteristic sound. What if you wanted the gestural interface of a theremin to drive a distortion pedal instead? Or an octave shifter? Or a tremolo, where the rate of tremolo follows the position of your hand? In the conventional pedal world, this is awkward to impossible, because the control surface and the audio processor are welded together inside a single metal box, and that weld has no fundamental reason to exist. It exists because that is how pedals have always been built.

Once you break that weld, an entire space of musical instruments opens up. A breath controller can drive a wah. An accelerometer mounted on a guitar headstock can drive a phaser. A pressure-sensitive pad under your foot can crossfade between two different distortion settings. The same physical control surface, once built and learned, can be retargeted to any parameter on any processor in your signal chain, and that retargeting can happen in software, in seconds, without any rewiring of physical cables.

The decoupling principle, once you take it seriously, ends up applying recursively throughout the architecture. Control surface is decoupled from audio processor. Audio path is decoupled from inter-module communication. Per-module concerns are decoupled from chain-wide services. Specialized capabilities are decoupled from the standard module's basic feature set. The same intellectual move shows up at every layer, and the consistency is what makes the platform coherent rather than just being a collection of clever individual ideas.

## You Do Not Have to Adopt the Whole Thing

A point that matters more than the architecture diagrams might suggest: Metapedal's capabilities are orthogonal. Each thing the platform does can be adopted independently of the others, alongside whatever gear you already own and love. The platform does not ask you to switch from an analog workflow to a digital workflow, or to throw out the pedals you have spent years collecting. It asks you to integrate Metapedal where it provides value to you specifically, and to leave the rest of your gear doing exactly what it has been doing.

A guitarist with a hand-wired tube amp and a pedalboard full of analog effects she has carefully tuned over a decade does not need to replace any of that to use Metapedal. She might use one Metapedal module at the end of her chain, taking the analog output and converting it to digital so that she can send it to a recording rig in another room, or stream it to a board operator at FOH, or capture it as multi-channel tracks for later mixing. The rest of her gear stays untouched. Metapedal becomes the bridge between her analog rig and digital domains that her rig cannot reach on its own.

A bassist who loves his vintage compressor and his specific overdrive pedal does not need to give them up to add modern flexibility. He might use a Metapedal controller module to send MIDI to a different piece of gear that does accept MIDI, while his analog pedals stay in his signal chain unchanged. Or he might use a small robotic peripheral that physically turns a knob on his analog overdrive in response to control signals, in the spirit of the Useless Machine that flips its own switch off. The analog pedal's character is preserved; the control surface is what becomes flexible.

A producer running a small home studio with existing audio interfaces, studio monitors, and an outboard preamp she has owned for years can use Metapedal modules for routing and per-source effects without replacing any of that. The platform becomes the routing matrix and the optional DSP layer; the rest of the studio infrastructure stays where it is.

A particularly nice variant of this for analog enthusiasts: a B variant module can act as a software-controllable send and return loop for analog gear. The module's DAC sends audio out to one or more analog pedals (a treasured overdrive, a specific compressor, a hand-built filter), and the module's ADC brings the analog-processed signal back into the routing graph. The onboard DSP is not involved at all; the module's role is converting between digital and analog at the boundaries of the analog chain. This means the analog pedal effectively lives inside the user's digital routing graph at any point the routing specifies. The same overdrive pedal can appear before reverb in one preset and after reverb in another, controlled by software rather than by recabling. Combined with a robot finger peripheral on the analog pedal's knob, the analog gear's parameters become software-controllable as well, giving the user analog character with fully digital flexibility. This kind of hybrid analog-digital configuration has been a long-running pain point for producers; Metapedal makes it natural.

A band with a working PA and effects setup can add Metapedal modules to provide per-performer monitor mixes without changing the existing FOH workflow. The Metapedal modules contribute one specific capability the existing rig does not have; the existing rig continues to do everything else.

The orthogonal capabilities Metapedal offers, each independently adoptable: transmission (carrying audio between physical locations as digital signals over standard cables), recording (functioning as a USB-class audio interface for capture), routing (sending signals between sources and destinations under software configuration), control (translating gestures into parameter changes for either Metapedal or external gear), effects processing (running DSP algorithms on audio), synthesis (generating audio from MIDI or other input), and monitor mixing (sending different audio mixes to different listeners).

A user adopting Metapedal for just transmission keeps their entire existing rig and adds a single module at the end of the chain. A user adopting Metapedal for just control uses a Metapedal controller alongside their existing audio gear. A user adopting Metapedal for just effects processing uses Metapedal modules as effects pedals in an otherwise unchanged signal chain. The platform's value compounds as users adopt more capabilities, but the entry point is genuinely low: one module, one specific purpose, alongside whatever gear the user already loves.

This decomposable adoption is itself meaningful. Most platform-style products ask users to commit to the platform's worldview wholesale. Eurorack expects you to build a modular system. Neural amp modelers ask you to replace your amps and pedals. Recording systems expect you to record everything through their interface. Metapedal does not require this kind of commitment, which makes it accessible to musicians who would not consider a wholesale switch but might consider a small targeted addition to a rig they already trust.

## A Concrete Vision: The Hardcore Punk Guitarist

It is useful at this point to ground the abstract architecture in a specific person doing specific things, because the value of the system only becomes clear when you can see what it changes about an actual musician's working life. So consider a sixteen-year-old guitarist playing in a hardcore punk band. He plays shows in basements, in VFW halls, in DIY venues with questionable electrical systems, occasionally in proper small clubs. His band plays sets of twenty to thirty minutes, with eight to twelve songs, no breaks between songs, often with no soundcheck. His amp is whatever he can carry on the train. His pedalboard is small because anything larger gets in the way during a chaotic set where he is jumping off the kick drum and singing into a shared microphone. The music demands enormous dynamic shifts within a single song, from clean palm-muted intros to wide-open distorted choruses to feedback-drenched bridges, and the transitions need to happen on the right beat or the song falls apart.

In the current pedal world, several things about this situation are painful. The dynamic shifts within a song typically require stomping on multiple pedals in rapid succession at the moment of transition. Switching from a clean tone to a heavy chorus tone might mean simultaneously kicking on the distortion pedal, the boost pedal, and the delay pedal, in the half-second between the last note of the verse and the first note of the chorus. This is a tap dance, and at this kind of show, with adrenaline and beer and people slamming into him, he misses the transition sometimes. When he misses, the song's emotional structure breaks for several seconds while he gets the pedalboard back into the right state. Audience members do not always notice but bandmates do.

Worse, the band plays multiple songs that need different baseline tones. Their fast hardcore songs want a thinner, more aggressive distortion. Their slower mosh-oriented songs want a fatter, lower-tuned distortion. A song they wrote with clean verses and screaming choruses wants the transition setup described above. Between songs, in the few seconds the drummer takes to count off, he has to remember which pedals should be on for the start of the next song, kick the right combination of footswitches, and be ready by the time the count starts. He often gets this wrong, and the first eight bars of a song get played with the wrong tone before he can recover.

The recording situation has its own problems. When the band records, the producer wants to capture the dry guitar signal as well as the processed signal, so that decisions about effects can be made later in mixing. But his pedalboard is designed around feeding the processed signal directly into his amp. Capturing the dry signal requires adding a splitter or a direct-out box that he does not own and does not really know how to integrate. So they record the processed signal only, and they have to commit to specific effects choices during tracking that they would rather keep open until mixing.

Now consider what Metapedal changes about all of this.

The first thing it changes is the transition problem. Instead of stomping on three pedals at once, he configures a single footswitch, anywhere on his pedalboard, to be the chorus-mode trigger. That footswitch is set up in advance through the phone app, with each song's settings stored as a named preset. The preset for song one says that hitting the chorus-mode switch engages distortion at this level, boost at this level, delay at this rate and mix, and disengages the clean compressor. When he stomps that one switch at the right moment, all five changes happen simultaneously and atomically. The transition is clean every time, regardless of how chaotic the stage is. He has freed up four feet of footswitch tap-dance and replaced it with a single stomp.

Between songs, the same mechanism scales up. A second footswitch cycles through song presets. At the end of song one, he stomps that switch, and his entire pedalboard reconfigures for song two. New baseline distortion character, different delay timing, different reverb settings, and the chorus-mode switch is now bound to song two's appropriate transition. He does not have to remember which pedals to engage for the next song. He just hits the song-advance switch and the pedalboard knows.

The second thing it changes is the bandwidth problem of his hands. Hardcore guitar playing is physically demanding. Both hands are constantly busy with the instrument, with pick attack and string bending and feedback control. There is no spare bandwidth for him to reach down and adjust a knob during a song. In the current world, this means anything that needs to change during a song has to be a binary footswitch action. Continuous parameter changes during a song are essentially impossible. With Metapedal, he can mount a small sensor on his guitar headstock, an accelerometer or a tilt sensor, and configure it to control any parameter of any effect. When he tilts the guitar neck up dramatically, which he does anyway as a stage gesture, the feedback module's resonance increases or the delay feedback ramps up, creating swells that he could not produce with his hands alone. The physical gestures he is already making for performance reasons become musical control inputs without any additional effort from him.

A breath sensor, like the one used in wind controller instruments, can be clipped to his microphone stand. When he screams into the mic, the breath pressure controls something. Maybe it opens up a low-pass filter on the guitar signal, so the guitar gets brighter as he screams louder. Maybe it modulates the rate of a tremolo. The physical intensity of his vocal performance becomes a continuous control input for his guitar tone, in a way that current pedalboards cannot accomplish.

These are not fancy studio gimmicks. They are exactly the kind of expressive moves that make a hardcore performance feel intense and physical and present. The current pedalboard architecture forces a separation between physical performance and tonal control, with the feet handling tone changes and everything else doing performance. Metapedal collapses that separation, letting any physical gesture potentially control any tonal parameter. The performer's body becomes a richer musical instrument than the guitar alone.

The third thing it changes is the configuration burden. Right now, every time he wants to add a new song to the set, he has to figure out which pedals to engage at which moments, practice the tap dance until he can do it reliably, and remember the routine under stage pressure. With Metapedal, he configures the song's preset on his phone, probably in his bedroom the day before the show, with the comfort of a real touchscreen and visual feedback about what each control does. He tests the preset by tapping through the song's sections in the app. When the band rehearses the song, the preset is already loaded and working. The configuration work happens in advance, in a comfortable environment, with good tools, rather than under the time pressure of a soundcheck or the chaos of a show.

The fourth thing it changes is the recording situation. When the band records, the Metapedal modules in his signal chain can simultaneously send the dry guitar signal and the processed guitar signal to the recording computer over USB, on separate audio channels. The producer gets the processed sound as a reference for what the band heard during tracking, plus the clean dry signal that can be reprocessed later. Multiple takes can be recorded with different preset chains, all without changing any physical setup, just by switching presets between takes. If the band decides during mixing that song three needs a different distortion character, the producer can run the dry signal back through a Metapedal Processor module with a different preset and get the new sound, without anyone having to come back to the studio. The flexibility of the recording process expands enormously, because the decisions are no longer baked in at tracking time.

The fifth thing it changes is the breakage problem. Hardcore shows are hard on equipment. Pedals get stomped, beer gets spilled, cables get yanked. In a traditional pedalboard, a broken pedal in the middle of the chain takes down the whole signal path, and the show stops while the guitarist figures out which pedal failed and how to bypass it. With Metapedal, the modules are connected over the inter-module bus, and the routing is software-controlled. If a module fails, the system can detect the failure and route around it, with the user notified through the OLED on the next-nearest module. He keeps playing through a degraded but functional signal chain, and figures out the failure between songs rather than mid-song. The same software flexibility that enables preset switching also enables graceful degradation under hardware failure.

There is also a social and economic dimension worth naming. Hardcore is a scene where people share gear, trade pedals, and build pedalboards on tight budgets. A traditional pedal is a fixed-function device, useful only for the one thing it does. A Metapedal Processor module is reconfigurable, so the same physical hardware that runs distortion for one player can run delay for another, with no firmware swap or anything technical, just a preset change. Bands sharing a backline can each have their own preset bank for the shared modules, switching between them at set changeover. A used Metapedal module on the secondhand market is more valuable than a used traditional pedal of equivalent quality, because the buyer is buying potential rather than a specific function. Over time this changes the economics of building a pedalboard, making it cheaper for new players to get started with capable gear because the gear is more flexible per dollar.

The vision, summarized, is this. A hardcore guitarist using Metapedal spends less time fighting his pedalboard during performance and more time playing. His tonal palette during a show is wider, because he can use continuous physical gestures to control effects rather than just binary footswitches. His configuration work happens in advance on a comfortable interface rather than under stage pressure. His recordings preserve more flexibility for mixing decisions. His gear is more robust against failure and more flexible across uses. The pedalboard stops being a thing he has to manage during the show and becomes a thing that responds to how he is already playing. That is the vision worth building toward, and the architectural decisions throughout the rest of the platform exist in service of making that vision real for this specific kind of musician among many others.

## A Concrete Vision: The Four-Piece Band Replacing the Mixing Console

The hardcore guitarist vision shows the platform serving a solo performer with relatively simple needs. The other end of the spectrum is the small band that uses the platform to replace a substantial slice of traditional live-sound gear. This vision shows what a full rig replacement looks like and why the platform's architectural decisions add up to something useful at this scale.

Consider a four-piece band: one vocalist with a microphone, an electric guitarist, a bass player, and a drummer with a kick mic, an overhead, and a snare mic. That is six simultaneous audio inputs flowing into the live sound system. The traditional approach uses a dedicated mixing console (a small Behringer or Mackie unit at the low end, a larger Allen and Heath or Yamaha at the high end), plus outboard effects processors for compression and reverb, plus a multitrack recorder if the band wants to capture the performance. The cost is somewhere between $2,000 and $5,000 for a competent rig, plus the cabling, stands, and racks to integrate everything, plus the setup time before each show.

The Metapedal version uses three B2 Processor modules and one A module connected through the inter-module bus. Each B2 has two combo jacks (XLR or quarter-inch with phantom power), giving six combo jacks across the three B2 modules. The vocal mic plugs into the first combo jack of the first B2. The bass plugs into the second jack of the first B2. The guitar plugs into the first jack of the second B2. The three drum mics fill the second jack of the second B2 and both jacks of the third B2. The A module sits in the chain as the touch UI host and headphone monitor coordinator.

Each module captures its inputs dry to its own microSD card while running live DSP effects in parallel. The dry recordings preserve every input exactly as it arrived, with no processing artifacts, for later mixing or release. The DSP effects run on each module's own H7 microcontroller, with each module handling the processing for its own two channels: compression and de-essing on the vocal, compression and amp simulation on the bass, amp simulation and cabinet emulation on the guitar, gating on the kick, EQ on the overheads. Each module is doing its local work in parallel with the others, with the H7 having plenty of compute headroom for two channels of processing.

The processed audio from each module flows through the inter-module bus to the designated chain leader, which mixes all six channels down to a stereo output with appropriate gains, pans, and mix-bus effects (a master compressor, the room reverb, a final EQ). The stereo output goes to the venue's PA system through the leader's quarter-inch jacks, looking like a normal stereo source from any pedalboard. The PA does not need to know anything about Metapedal; it just sees stereo audio at line level.

Each performer can plug in-ear monitors into the TRRS jack of their respective module for a per-performer monitor mix, with the module providing emphasis on that performer's own signal and the rest of the band at lower levels. This is genuinely useful because each performer gets their own mix without needing a dedicated monitor engineer.

After the performance, the band collects the microSD cards from each module and combines the six dry tracks into a multitrack session in their DAW. The dry tracks have no live-processing artifacts because they were captured before any DSP. The band can produce a polished studio mix later from the same audio that drove the live performance, with full flexibility for mixing decisions.

The cost comparison is dramatic. The Metapedal rig is three B2 modules plus an A module, totaling perhaps $250 to $400 in retail equipment at the launch price point, plus a standard pedalboard power supply and cables. That is replacing perhaps $2,000 to $5,000 of dedicated mixing console plus effects plus multitrack recorder gear. The cost reduction is meaningful for small bands, school music programs, community organizations, and other budget-constrained users who could not afford the traditional rig. The Metapedal rig is also more portable, sets up faster, and travels more easily than the traditional gear it replaces.

The architectural elements that make this work are all elements the platform already has for other reasons. The inter-module bus carries audio between modules. The routing graph configures the per-channel processing and the mix-down. The microSD storage handles the dry-track recording. The TRRS jacks provide per-performer headphone monitoring. The high-speed USB ensures bandwidth headroom for the six-channel chain. The standard 9V pedalboard power lets the rig use existing power infrastructure. The dual-purpose USB Audio Class implementation lets the same rig become a six-input USB audio interface when connected to a computer for studio recording sessions. None of this requires features that the platform does not already have for other use cases; the four-piece-band scenario just exercises the features in combination.

The sound mixer position can use an external touch device as the user interface for the rig, turning the platform from "a bunch of pedals doing live sound" into something that feels much more like a proper mixing console. The architectural approach that works best is data-channel rather than rendered-video: the rig sends real-time level data, parameter values, and chain state as a stream of small numeric messages over a USB data channel, and the touch device runs a Metapedal companion application that renders the mixer UI from this data. The touch device sends user input (fader positions, mute toggles, scene selections) back to the rig as commands. The display and rendering happen entirely on the touch device, with the rig providing only data.

This separation has several genuinely valuable properties. The rendering can be much richer than the rig could feasibly produce on its own: smooth 60 Hz or higher meter animations with arbitrary visualizations (VU-style needles with realistic ballistic behavior, spectrum analyzers with custom frequency scaling, waterfall displays of recent level history, color-coded warnings with smooth gradients), all rendered by whatever GPU or graphics capability the user's device has. The bandwidth is tiny: six channels at 100 samples per second each is well under 3 kilobytes per second, compared to gigabits per second for a rendered video stream. The responsiveness is excellent because the touch-device rendering does not depend on the rig's video output capability. And the data is composable: the same level stream can flow to multiple consumers (the front-of-house touch device, a phone-app secondary display, a logging system for post-show analysis) without the rig having to do anything different.

The implementation works across a range of touch devices. A USB-C touchscreen monitor with a built-in small computer can plug directly into the rig and run the companion application on its embedded compute. An iPad or Android tablet running the companion app connects to the rig over USB-C and gets the same experience with a more familiar device. A laptop with a touchscreen runs the desktop version of the companion app. For wireless operation (allowing the sound person to walk around the venue), a Bluetooth peripheral on the chain provides the data stream over BLE to a tablet, with slightly higher latency but freedom of movement. The user picks the touch device that fits their needs and budget; the rig provides the data regardless.

The mixer UI presents a real-time view of the rig: per-channel faders, mute and solo buttons, sends to effects and monitor mixes, level meters with peak holds, spectrum analyzers per channel if desired, and scene recall for switching configurations between songs. Touch a fader, drag it to the new position, and the corresponding module's gain coefficient updates over the inter-module bus. Tap a scene name to recall an entire mix configuration with one action. The cabling from the rig to the touch device is one USB-C run, comparable to what a traditional console requires for its mix position but vastly simpler than the multi-pair snake cables traditional consoles need. Wireless operation drops even this cable.

The touchscreen has real advantages over physical consoles for the small-band use case. Multiple pages can show different aspects of the mix without requiring dedicated physical hardware: a main mix page, a monitor mix page, an effects page, a recording status page. Visual feedback is richer than meters and indicator LEDs allow: spectrum analyzers, color-coded clip warnings, smooth meter animations. Scene management is smoother than physical console snapshot systems: tap a name, the whole rig reconfigures instantly. And critically for the recording workflow, the touchscreen can display recording status alongside the mixing controls, showing the sound person which microSD cards are healthy, how much recording time remains, and whether any module has had a dropout that needs attention. This is integration that traditional consoles cannot easily provide because the recording is typically separate equipment.

The touchscreen has some disadvantages worth being honest about. Physical faders give tactile feedback that touchscreens cannot match; the sound person can find the right fader by feel during dim shows, while touchscreens require visual attention. Multiple faders moving simultaneously is awkward on a touchscreen because finger count is limited. The lack of physical controls also makes the system more vulnerable to accidental parameter changes from finger slips. The right balance for serious work is probably touchscreen for the bulk of the mixing with physical assist from specific modules' controls for the most critical parameters: the lead vocalist's microphone level always has a physical knob assignment, the chain leader's footswitch serves as a "mute master" emergency control, and so on.

A particularly nice property of this configuration is that the touchscreen does not interfere with the recording workflow. The sound person sees the live mix on the touchscreen and adjusts it for the room's needs, but the dry tracks are recording to the microSD cards regardless of what the mix looks like. The sound person can be aggressive with live processing during the show without worrying about destroying the recording quality. The dry vocals are pristine even if the live mix has heavy compression and de-essing. The band gets a polished live mix for the audience and a flexible multitrack for post-production from the same rig.

The companion application work is real software development effort and the version-one and version-two scoping of it matters. Version one ships with at least a basic touch-device companion application that runs on at least one platform, probably a desktop laptop application initially because that has the simplest development path and broadest compatibility. Version two expands to mobile platforms (iOS app, Android app) for the tablet experience that the sound person actually wants, plus the Bluetooth peripheral that enables wireless operation. The version-two scope makes sense because mobile app development is meaningful effort and because building it after the platform has stabilized is lower risk than building it in parallel with the platform.

The product proposition here is genuinely interesting. The Metapedal platform is not just a flexible guitar effects system but is also a viable live-sound replacement for small ensembles, with a touchscreen mixer interface that approaches the user experience of a proper console. The same hardware that serves the hardcore guitarist can serve a four-piece band with their sound person, with the difference being the configuration and the connected touchscreen rather than the underlying capabilities. This is the kind of multi-purpose breadth that the platform's decoupled, modular architecture enables and that traditional fixed-function gear cannot match.

## A Concrete Vision: The Streaming Basement Band

Consider a four-piece band that practices in a basement: drummer, bassist, guitarist, vocalist. They have been playing together for a couple of years, they have written enough material for a set, and they want to start livestreaming their practices to Twitch or YouTube to build an audience while they work toward booking proper gigs. They own gear that works for local shows: amps, microphones, a small PA, the instruments themselves. They have a laptop with reasonable specs.

What they do not have is the audio infrastructure for a good livestream. A camera pointed at the band and the laptop's built-in microphone produces audio that sounds terrible: drums boom, vocals get buried, the room reverb dominates everything. Viewers click away within seconds. Pricing out a "real" streaming setup hits a financial wall fast. An audio interface with enough inputs to capture the band properly runs $400-800. A mixer for monitor mixes and routing runs $300-1,500. Effects processing for vocals and drums adds $200-500. In-ear monitor systems or headphone amps add $200-500. The streaming computer needs to be capable enough to handle audio plugins alongside OBS, adding $1,000-1,500 over a basic laptop. Total before microphones and instruments the band already owns: $3,000-5,000, easily.

This is the wall that stops most basement bands from streaming at quality high enough to build an audience. The music is there; the streaming infrastructure is not.

Metapedal collapses most of that cost into a small number of multi-purpose modules. The configuration: four B2 Processor modules, one per musician, with their microphones plugged into the modules' combo jacks. The drummer's module captures kick microphone and an overhead microphone (or alternatively, a single B16 module captures the full drum kit through eight inputs); the bassist's module captures a DI plus an optional second microphone; the guitarist's module captures the amp microphone plus optionally a DI; the vocalist's module captures the vocal microphone plus optionally a backing vocal. Each module is simultaneously doing the audio capture and the per-channel processing (vocal compression and EQ, guitar amp simulation, bass DI processing, drum gating) that traditional setups need outboard hardware for.

A fifth module sits as the mix bus, taking all the captured signals and producing a clean stereo output for the stream. This is probably a BX variant if the band wants extra DSP headroom for bus processing (master compression, glue reverb, broadcast limiter), but a B1 with enough cycles to spare works fine for simpler bus chains. A sixth module, an A variant with a cheap dynamic microphone plugged into its TRRS jack, serves as the talkback channel: the producer running the stream can press a footswitch on a passive footswitch peripheral and talk to the band through their monitors.

Total Metapedal cost: four B2 modules at $80-95 each ($320-380), one BX at $140-170, one A variant at $50, plus a cheap microphone at $30. Roughly $540-630 for the entire audio infrastructure. The microphones and instruments the band already owns. The streaming laptop they already have. The reduction from $3,000-5,000 to $540-630 is the difference between "streaming is impossible for us" and "we can do this tonight."

The routing graph configuration handles all the audio plumbing. Each musician's audio capture flows into their module's per-channel processing, then into the mix bus module, then out to the laptop as a stereo USB audio stream that OBS sees as a standard audio source. Each musician's monitor mix flows out to their headphones through the TRRS jack on their own module, with the mix configured per-musician (the drummer hears the click and balanced band; the vocalist hears more reverb on their voice and reduced drum bleed; the guitarist hears their own amp prominently). The talkback module's signal routes into all four monitor mixes when the footswitch is pressed.

The platform's audio analysis capability adds polish that would require manual mixing intervention in a traditional setup. The vocal microphone's envelope follower automatically ducks the band slightly when the vocalist starts singing, helping the vocal sit better in the stream mix. The kick drum's transient detector drives subtle sidechain compression on the bass channel for the classic ducking effect, without anyone having to wire sidechain connections. The drum kit's beat tracker syncs the guitarist's delay to the band's actual tempo, so the delay locks to the song without anyone tapping a tempo. These automatic responses to performance dynamics happen because the routing graph treats audio analysis output as control signals, and control signals can drive any parameter.

The reliability story is also better than a computer-based streaming setup. The Metapedal modules run dedicated real-time audio firmware. They do not run general-purpose operating systems with unpredictable behavior. If the streaming laptop crashes mid-stream, the audio capture on the modules continues, the multi-track recording to microSD continues, and only the live stream goes down until the laptop comes back. The band keeps playing; the recording survives; the stream resumes when the laptop recovers.

Each B variant module simultaneously records its captured audio to microSD at full quality alongside sending it down the chain. The band gets a multi-track recording of every stream automatically, giving them raw material for later mixing if they want to release the recording as an album or video. The microSD cards hold hours of multi-track audio at 48 kHz/24-bit, easily enough for a full practice session or several streams between card swaps.

The configuration the band sets up once and refines over time. The web interface running on someone's phone or laptop lets them tweak per-channel processing between songs, save the configuration as a preset, recall the preset for the next stream, and gradually develop a stream-ready configuration that becomes second nature. The whole audio infrastructure becomes a thing they set up once and use forever, rather than a thing they have to fight with every time they stream.

This use case captures something important about what Metapedal enables. The technology required for high-quality basement-band streaming has existed for years, but the cost wall has kept it out of reach for the bands who would benefit most. Metapedal's architectural decoupling (audio capture, processing, routing, monitoring, recording, all as software-configurable capabilities of the same general-purpose modules) lets a band achieve professional-quality streaming infrastructure at consumer pedal prices. The basement band stops being a band that practices in a basement and starts being a band that produces content from a basement. That is the kind of capability shift that grows audiences, builds careers, and turns basement practices into the foundation of working musical lives.

## The Standard Module at a User Level

The physical form factor of a Metapedal module is conventional in the most important ways. It is a metal box, roughly the size of a small guitar pedal, designed to be stepped on without damage. What distinguishes the Metapedal physical design from conventional pedals is that the module is intended to be used in two distinct physical contexts, on the floor and in the hand, and the design assigns different roles to different surfaces of the enclosure to serve those contexts well.

The top surface is the performance surface. Its dominant feature is a primary footswitch, large and rugged, suitable for being stomped on with a boot. Toward the back of the top surface sits a small OLED display, tilted up slightly so that it is readable both from standing height when the module is on the floor and from the natural viewing angle when the module is held in the hand. Alongside the footswitch and OLED, the top surface holds a status indicator whose color and pattern are driven by the routing graph, communicating just enough about the module's current state for the performer to recognize what is engaged without breaking concentration on the music.

The side surfaces are the configuration surfaces. On each side of the module sits a recessed navigation control. The intended grip is to hold the module in one hand with the back panel facing the user, two fingers resting on the up and down buttons on one side, and the thumb on the select button on the other side. The user can navigate menus and confirm selections without ever shifting their grip. This is essentially how people interact with a remote control or a small camera, well-established ergonomic patterns that work well. The side controls are recessed so they cannot be triggered by an accidental brush from a passing foot during performance.

The module knows whether it is currently being held or sitting on the floor through a small accelerometer on its motherboard. The OLED adapts its rendering based on context: large at-a-glance text when the module is in performance position, richer menu-style content when the module is held for configuration. The side controls activate only when the module is held, preventing accidental presses during use. The transitions happen automatically based on the user's physical actions rather than requiring any explicit mode switch.

The back panel is the connection surface. It holds the inter-module bus ports, the peripheral ports, and the audio jacks. The bottom surface is the floor-contact surface with non-slip feet. Each surface has a coherent visual identity that matches its functional role.

A critical design principle is that every module must be configurable using only its own on-board controls. There must never be a situation where a musician on a dark stage cannot adjust a module because the configuration tool is on a laptop or phone that is not present. External configuration tools are a convenience that enriches the experience, but not a requirement that the platform depends on.

## Module Identification: Colour and Nicknames

The platform's flexibility creates a usability problem that traditional pedals do not have. A traditional pedal is visually identifiable from its top panel: the user sees "Tube Screamer" or "Digital Delay" printed on the metal and knows what the pedal does. A Metapedal module's function is determined by what preset is loaded and how the routing graph is configured, so the module has no inherent visual identity that maps to its current behavior. Two identical-looking modules might be running entirely different effects, and the user has no way to tell them apart at a glance. This is a real problem during performance, when the user needs to know which module to stomp on for a particular effect change.

The platform addresses this with two complementary identification mechanisms: enclosure colour and on-demand nickname display.

Modules are sold in a range of enclosure colours, allowing users to build a visual vocabulary that maps colours to functions. The red module is always the distortion. The blue module is always the delay. The green module is always the modulation effects. The user looks at the pedalboard, sees the colour layout, and immediately knows which module does what. This works regardless of what is printed on the modules because the colour is the identifier, not the text. The colour assignment is the user's choice, made when they buy and set up their modules, and different users will make different choices according to their own conventions. Standard offerings include black as the default plus several distinct colours covering the main visual categories users want, with manufacturing through standard anodizing processes that keep the cost difference between colours small.

The nickname feature complements the colour identification by giving users an on-demand way to read the precise identity of each module. Each module has a user-assigned nickname stored locally, something like "Lead Boost" or "Verse Delay" or whatever name makes sense to the user. A specific control action, mapped through the routing graph like any other action, triggers all modules in the chain to display their nicknames on their OLEDs simultaneously. The user sees the names of all the modules at once, mapped to their physical positions, and quickly identifies which module is which. After a timeout the displays return to their normal content. The trigger can be a long-press of a footswitch, a button on the phone app, a voice command, or whatever else the user prefers.

The colour and the nickname work together. The colour is the always-on visual hierarchy that the user sees at a glance during normal playing. The nickname is the on-demand detailed identification that the user triggers when they need to confirm exactly which module they are looking at. Both work without requiring the user to remember anything beyond their own conventions.

There is a real friction point worth flagging. The platform's flexibility means a module's function can change over time as the user reassigns it. A module that started as the distortion might later become the delay if the user reorganizes. The colour, carefully chosen to mean distortion, no longer matches the new function. Users have to either accept the mismatch, swap modules around to maintain colour consistency, or buy additional modules in the colours they need for the new layout. Users who reorganize frequently might prefer to lean on the nickname feature more heavily and treat colour as decorative rather than functional. Users with stable pedalboard arrangements can lean on colour identification more. Both approaches work.

The RGB status LED on each module's top surface adds a third identification mechanism that operates per-module rather than per-chain. The LED's colour is driven by the routing graph and can indicate the module's current mode or active state. A module configured as a distortion might glow red when active. A module configured as a delay might glow blue. This is complementary to the enclosure colour rather than a replacement: the enclosure colour persists across configurations while the LED colour can change with the active preset. The combination of enclosure colour, LED colour, and on-demand nickname display gives users multiple overlapping ways to identify modules in different contexts.

## The Routing Layer

The architectural heart of Metapedal is the routing layer. This is the system that connects control sources to control destinations and audio sources to audio destinations, with transformations applied in between. The routing layer is what makes the platform's central decoupling concrete.

Every module exposes typed inputs and typed outputs to the routing graph. This is a software and protocol concept, not a hardware one. The physical interfaces (USB-C connectors, M8 connectors, quarter-inch jacks, the TRRS jack) carry whatever bits or analog signals the protocol says they carry, with no hardware-level enforcement of what kind of data flows over which connector. The routing graph keeps track of which logical ports produce and consume which types of data, and the inter-module bus protocol carries typed messages that the software at each end interprets. The hardware just moves bits and analog signals; the type system lives in the software.

The basic types in the routing graph are audio, continuous control, and discrete control events. The routing graph connects outputs of one type to inputs of the same type. This means a knob on one module can be configured to drive a parameter on a different module. A footswitch can trigger a chain-wide preset change. A microphone signal can be analyzed in real time to produce trigger events that modulate effects elsewhere in the chain. The same architectural machinery handles all of these cases uniformly, because they all decompose into typed signals flowing through the routing graph.

The hardware does have a small amount of physical type-encoding through the M8 peripheral pinout system. Analog Single, Analog Multi, and Smart Digital pinouts are physically distinct in how the contacts are wired, so a peripheral cannot accidentally plug into the wrong port type. But this is a coarse-grained distinction at the level of peripheral classes, not the fine-grained type system of the routing graph. The dual-purpose TRRS jack is another small case where hardware behavior depends on configuration (audio versus MIDI), but the underlying type system is still a software construct.

Buttons and knobs and sensors on a module are not intrinsically connected to that module's local functions. They are just inputs whose meanings are assigned by the routing graph. The default meaning is the locally intuitive one, because that is the most useful default, but the binding is just configuration. The user can break that binding at any time and rebind the controls to anything else.

The audio-to-control extraction capability is worth highlighting because it is genuinely novel. The platform can analyze an audio signal in real time to produce control signals. A peak detector running on a bass drum mic produces a discrete event each time the drum is struck. An envelope follower running on a guitar signal produces a continuous control that follows the amplitude envelope. A pitch tracker on a monophonic instrument produces a continuous pitch signal that can drive a synthesizer. Each of these analyses lives in a dedicated peripheral module optimized for its specific extraction.

A preset captures a complete snapshot of the routing graph and all parameter values in the chain at a moment in time. Activating a preset replaces the current routing-plus-parameters with the saved one, atomically across all modules. The audio processing functions themselves do not change, because the modules are still the same modules. What changes is which signals flow to which parameters and what values those parameters hold. This data model is small and clean and supports the full range of use cases the platform serves, from simple effect switching to elaborate cross-instrument coordination.

Mappings in the routing graph can include transformations: scaling, curve shaping, smoothing, deadzones. These are necessary because raw sensor outputs almost never map cleanly to useful parameter values. A theremin signal needs scaling and deadzones. A breath controller needs a logarithmic curve. An accelerometer might need gesture recognition. The transformations are configurable per mapping, with reasonable defaults that work for common cases.

The technical details of the routing layer, including the bus protocol that carries control signals between modules, the lease-based leader election that coordinates chain-wide state, and the streaming router that handles passthrough traffic in hardware without CPU involvement, are covered in the technical specification.

## DSP Capacity: What You Can Actually Run

A reasonable question for musicians evaluating the platform is what kinds of effects and processing each module can actually run. The answer matters for users planning their rigs because it determines whether they can fit their desired effect chain into one module or whether they need to distribute work across multiple modules.

A single B1 or B2 module's STM32H7 microcontroller running at 480 MHz has substantial DSP capability for embedded audio. The chip handles single-precision floating point in hardware, has well-optimized SIMD instructions for audio work, and includes substantial RAM for delay lines and convolution buffers. In practical terms, a single H7 can comfortably run the kind of effect chain you would build with traditional pedals, with room to spare.

A useful way to think about effect complexity is to group algorithms into tiers based on how much of the H7's compute budget they consume. The tiers are approximate, since specific implementations vary widely in cost, but they give a useful sense of what fits where.

**Trivial effects** cost essentially nothing: gain stages, panning, simple filters, hard clipping, A/B switching, tuners, level meters. These are arithmetic operations on samples without significant state. A single H7 can run hundreds of these without noticing.

**Lightweight effects** include the bread-and-butter pedal effects: noise gates, compressors (including LA-2A-style optical compressor emulations), parametric EQs, graphic EQs, simple drive and fuzz pedals (Tube Screamer, Big Muff style waveshaping), tremolos, simple choruses, ring modulators. Each costs maybe 1-5% of the H7's budget. A typical guitar chain of "noise gate, compressor, drive, EQ, tremolo, simple chorus, delay" is all lightweight effects and fits easily in one H7 with substantial headroom for more.

**Medium effects** include the more substantial bread-and-butter: digital delays with modulation and feedback (Strymon DIG-style), spring reverb simulations, multi-voice choruses, phasers, flangers, pitch shifters using PSOLA at musical ratios, octavers, envelope-following wahs, plate reverbs, basic algorithmic reverbs. Each costs maybe 5-20% of the H7's budget. A single H7 can run several medium effects simultaneously, with the budget filling up at perhaps 4-6 medium effects.

**Heavy effects** include the computationally substantial work: realistic amp simulations with full tube preamp, power amp, tone stack, and cabinet/IR simulation (Axe-FX-style amp modeling); convolution reverbs with 1-2 second impulse responses (typical halls and rooms); spectral pitch shifters that preserve formants; granular synthesis with many simultaneous grains; complex modular-synth oscillator banks. Each costs 30-80% of the H7's budget. A single H7 can run one or maybe two heavy effects, possibly combined with lighter effects depending on the specific algorithms.

**Beyond a single H7** are algorithms that do not fit within one H7's budget at full quality: neural-network-based audio processing (Neural DSP-style "AI amp models" at their full quality); real-time pitch correction at studio quality (Antares Auto-Tune at maximum settings); large convolution reverbs with multi-second impulse responses (cathedral simulations, large concert hall IRs); high-quality time-stretching at extreme ratios; high-channel-count operations (16+ channels with full DSP per channel). These typically run on desktop processors or specialized DSP chips with substantially more compute than embedded microcontrollers provide.

For practical user planning, this means a single B1 module can comfortably run a complete typical guitar effects chain: noise gate, compressor, drive pedal, amp simulation with cabinet modeling, EQ, modulation effect (chorus or phaser), delay with modulation, and reverb. This is the kind of chain you would build with traditional pedals, except all of it runs on one module that costs $50-95 retail rather than $500-1500 of individual pedals.

A B2 module running USB audio interface mode for two channels of recording, with light processing on each channel (compressor, EQ, light reverb), runs well within the H7's budget. This is the kind of setup a singer-songwriter would use for tracking guitar and vocals into a laptop.

The platform has a quiet advantage over traditional pedal DSP for RAM-intensive effects. The STM32H7 chip has 1 MB of on-chip SRAM, with about 700-800 KB realistically available for user DSP after firmware and system overhead. By comparison, typical commercial pedal DSP chips like the Spin FV-1 have around 32 KB of working memory; classical reverb-on-a-chip parts have 16-64 KB. The H7 has 10-25 times more usable RAM than these traditional pedal DSP options, which matters because RAM-intensive effects (long delays, convolution reverbs, granular synthesis, sample-based effects) are inherently more tractable on the platform than on traditional pedal hardware. A user who cares about long reverbs, multi-tap delays, or other memory-hungry algorithms gets meaningfully more capability per module than competitive products at similar price points provide.

Heavy DSP loads that would tax a single module include high-end convolution reverbs with very long impulse responses, modern realistic amp simulations with detailed tube modeling, and spectral effects like high-quality pitch shifting at extreme ratios. These can still run on the platform, but the user might dedicate a whole module to the heavy effect, or distribute the work across multiple modules in the chain. A 3-module chain has roughly 3x the DSP budget of one module plus 3x the RAM, which makes heavy combinations feasible.

The platform deliberately uses Faust as its DSP language because Faust's standard library includes well-documented implementations of most of these effects, with measured computational costs available. Users planning their chains can look at specific Faust implementations of the effects they want and see the actual cycle costs, making the planning concrete rather than guessing.

Some categories of processing are deliberately not supported at version one quality levels: neural amp modeling (probably waits for v2 with more compute), studio-quality real-time pitch correction (similar story), large convolution reverbs (the H7 has enough memory and compute for medium IRs but not for the very large ones that distinguish high-end commercial products). Users who specifically need these capabilities should know they are outside the platform's launch sweet spot. The platform serves most pedal-effect needs comfortably; the niches it cannot serve at launch are precisely the niches that require desktop-class compute.

## Listening: Audio as a Source of Control

One capability that falls naturally out of the platform's architecture but reads as a major feature to most musicians is the platform's ability to analyze audio and use the analysis to drive control signals. In traditional gear, audio and control are completely separate worlds: audio flows from instruments to amplifiers and PAs, control flows from knobs and footswitches to parameters, and the two never meet. Metapedal makes them meet, because the routing graph treats audio signals and control signals as first-class citizens with the same lifecycle and addressability. Adding "audio analyzed to produce control signals" is just another type of node in the graph.

What this looks like in practice: a user plugs a cheap microphone into the TRRS jack of an A or B variant module, or into the combo jack of a B variant, or attaches a contact microphone through the contact mic breakout peripheral. The microphone picks up some part of the performance: the drum kit, the room sound, the singer, the bass amp, whatever. The Metapedal module runs an analysis algorithm on the captured audio and produces control signals that flow into the routing graph. The control signals then drive parameters anywhere else in the chain.

Several specific analyses become possible with modest DSP cost:

**Tempo and beat tracking.** A microphone listening to a drum kit or to the band as a whole can produce a continuous tempo estimate plus phase information about where the beat falls. Tempo-dependent effects (delay times that lock to the beat, tremolo rates that match the song, arpeggiator speed) synchronize automatically to whatever the band is playing. The drummer does not have to play to a click track; the delay pedal learns the tempo from listening.

**Transient detection on specific sources.** A microphone or contact mic on the kick drum produces a trigger event each time the kick hits. The trigger drives any parameter on any module: a duck on the bass synth that creates space for each kick hit, a gate that opens the lead synth on every downbeat, a step in an arpeggiator that advances with the kick.

**Envelope following on the vocalist.** A microphone near the singer produces a continuous envelope signal that tracks how loud they are singing. The envelope drives parameters that respond to vocal dynamics: reverb that gets wetter when the singer goes quiet, EQ that opens up on louder vocals, compressors that respond to the actual performance.

**Pitch detection on melodic sources.** A microphone on a vocalist or melodic instrument produces a continuous pitch estimate. The pitch drives effects like a filter that tracks the singer's melody, a harmonizer that produces fixed intervals from the detected pitch, or a synth voice that doubles the melodic line in real time.

**Spectral analysis of the room.** A microphone listening to the overall band produces spectral information: low/mid/high band energy, brightness, density. These drive parameters on individual instruments that respond to what the rest of the band is doing. An EQ that subtly responds to the band's spectral content; a reverb whose decay shortens when the band gets dense and lengthens when the band gets sparse.

**Ambient awareness.** A microphone listening to the room can detect applause (the band knows when to stop), audience volume (the band knows whether they need to play louder), and other room context. The control signals drive parameters that respond to the actual performance situation rather than to predetermined automation.

The DSP cost of these analyses is generally modest. Envelope following and transient detection are essentially free. Pitch detection is moderate but manageable. Beat tracking runs at a low rate (a few times per second rather than at audio rate) so the analysis cost is amortized across many samples. All of these fit comfortably within a B variant module's DSP budget alongside normal effects processing.

The architectural elegance is that the user does not need to think about whether a control signal came from a footswitch, a sensor, a MIDI controller, or audio analysis. From the routing graph's perspective, control is control. A delay's tempo parameter can be driven from a tap-tempo footswitch in one preset and from beat-tracking analysis of the drummer in another preset, with no other change to the configuration. The signal source is interchangeable; the parameter mapping stays the same.

This capability also enables a use case for users who only want the analysis without the effects: a single Metapedal module configured as "the ears of the rig," with a microphone input, audio analysis algorithms producing control signals, and those control signals flowing out to drive parameters on other gear (whether other Metapedal modules or external gear via MIDI). The module's audio output is unused in this configuration; the value is purely the analysis. This is yet another instance of the platform's partial-adoption story: a user can buy one Metapedal module just to add audio-driven control to a rig that is otherwise unchanged.

## Portable Operation

The basic Metapedal use case assumes a pedalboard with wired power, but the architecture also supports a different use case: portable operation with battery power and a worn or carried form factor. This opens the platform to musicians who do not have access to power outlets or stages, including buskers, street performers, protest singers, campfire performers, songwriters who want to walk and write, music teachers traveling between locations, and entry-level musicians who do not yet own pedalboards. The portable use case meaningfully expands the platform's addressable market.

The basic portable rig is a single Metapedal module with a guitar plugged into the quarter-inch input, a microphone plugged into the TRRS input, and the processed output going to whatever speaker is available. The module runs from battery power provided by an external accessory containing replaceable 18650 cells. The accessory is essentially a small USB-C power bank with mounting hardware that clips onto a belt or guitar strap. The user wears the battery pack and the module while playing, with short cables connecting them to the instruments and to the speaker.

The Bluetooth peripheral is particularly valuable in portable operation. With Bluetooth available, the audio output can go to ubiquitous Bluetooth speakers that the user or the venue already owns. The performance rig is a module, a Bluetooth peripheral, a battery pack, and a Bluetooth speaker. Total cost is in the range of one hundred and fifty to two hundred dollars depending on the speaker, which is dramatically cheaper than any traditional portable performance system.

The 18650 cells are user-replaceable, which is the right design choice for this kind of accessory. Built-in pouch cells eventually degrade and turn the whole accessory into trash. Replaceable cells mean the accessory lasts indefinitely, and users can carry spare cells for extended runtime if needed. The cells are a standard part with consistent dimensions, available from any battery vendor at competitive prices, and the same accessory works with cells of varying capacities depending on what the user prefers.

The user interface during portable performance happens through external peripherals rather than the module's onboard controls, because the module is not reachable when worn. An expression pedal at the user's feet, a footswitch peripheral, a wireless controller on the guitar, voice commands through the microphone, or gesture detection through the module's accelerometer. The module recedes into the background while the user interacts through whatever peripherals fit their performance context. This is the platform's central decoupling premise becoming visible because the physical context demands it.

## The MIDI Controller and Synthesizer Use Case

Another use case that falls out of the architecture naturally is plugging an inexpensive USB MIDI controller into a Metapedal module and running a software-defined synthesizer locally. The user plugs a cheap MIDI keyboard into one of the module's USB-C ports, the module enumerates it as a USB MIDI device, and the keyboard's MIDI events flow into the routing graph. A synthesizer algorithm running on the module's processor produces audio in response to those MIDI events. The audio comes out through the standard quarter-inch jack, the headphone output, or the inter-module bus to other modules for further processing.

The synthesizer itself is just another DSP algorithm running on the module. In the typed-port model it has a MIDI input port and an audio output port. The routing graph handles the wiring the same way it handles everything else.

The specific case of DX7 emulation is feasible on the platform's processor and worth highlighting because it is a compelling product proposition. The Yamaha DX7 is one of the most iconic synthesizers ever made, with sounds that defined an entire era of pop music. The Dexed project provides an open-source emulation that has been successfully ported to embedded microcontrollers similar to what Metapedal uses, running full sixteen-voice polyphony with effects at roughly half CPU utilization on a Cortex-M7. A Metapedal B variant with a cheap USB MIDI controller plugged in becomes a complete DX7 in a small box, with the user supplying only the keyboard.

The economics are favorable. A cheap entry-level MIDI controller runs perhaps fifty to a hundred dollars new, and the user already has access to the full ecosystem of decades of accumulated DX7 patches through the standard SysEx voice dump format. This is substantially cheaper than a used original DX7, with better physical form factor and full Metapedal integration including effects, routing, and presets.

The DX7 is one specific example. Any synthesis algorithm that takes MIDI input and produces audio output runs on the platform, from subtractive synthesis through wavetable, physical modeling, granular, and additive synthesis. The user picks the synth that fits their needs and the platform runs it. This makes Metapedal a viable replacement for a variety of standalone synthesizers, with the bonus of integration into the user's broader signal chain.

A particularly nice illustration is Hammond B3 emulation with Leslie speaker simulation. The Hammond B3 is fundamentally an additive synthesizer with nine sine-wave oscillators per note, mixed at levels controlled by the famous nine drawbars. On a real B3 the drawbars are physical sliders on the console, taking up significant panel space. The combination of drawbar settings determines the timbre, and players manipulate drawbars in real time as part of performance technique. Metapedal can run the Hammond synthesis on its processor, but the drawbar interface decouples from the synthesis itself. The nine drawbar values are just nine continuous control inputs that can come from anywhere: a touchscreen connected through USB-C, the assignable knobs on a MIDI keyboard, a dedicated drawbar controller peripheral with nine physical sliders that plugs into the chain, or any other source. The user picks whatever drawbar interface fits their performance style, and the routing graph wires it to the synthesizer.

The Leslie speaker emulation is similarly decoupled. The speed control that toggles between slow chorale and fast tremolo modes is just another control input in the routing graph. A footswitch can drive it for the traditional binary switching. An expression pedal can drive it for continuous control. More creative mappings work too, like having the speed automatically follow the keyboard's volume envelope. The user is not constrained to any specific physical control style for this; whatever they prefer works.

The complete classic organ rig is a MIDI keyboard, a Metapedal module running Hammond plus Leslie emulation, and whatever drawbar interface the user prefers. Total cost is well under two hundred dollars including the keyboard. Real B3-with-Leslie rigs cost thousands of dollars and weigh hundreds of pounds. The convenience advantage of the platform-based approach is substantial for any performance context where transporting a real organ is impractical.

This also opens the platform to keyboardists as a user community alongside guitarists. The two groups have different gear conventions and aesthetic preferences, but the architectural decisions serve both well enough. The platform broadens its addressable market by being useful across instrument families rather than serving only one.

## The B Variant Family and USB Audio Interface Use

The B variant we have been discussing is actually a family of related variants distinguished by the number of audio input channels each provides. A B1 has one combo jack that accepts either quarter-inch instrument signals or XLR microphone signals. A B2 has two combo jacks, allowing simultaneous capture of two independent audio sources. Higher channel counts are possible at the cost of additional back-panel space and higher-channel-count codec chips. The B family naming communicates capability clearly: users see "B1" or "B2" and know exactly what they are getting without consulting feature matrices.

Each step up in channel count adds the cost of an additional combo jack, an additional microphone preamp (when populated), additional codec ADC capacity, and additional back-panel space. The B1 fits in a standard pedal form factor easily. The B2 fits with somewhat tighter back-panel layout. Higher channel counts probably need a larger form factor, perhaps half-rack or full-rack, which makes them a separate product category rather than continuations of the pedal-sized line.

A meaningful capability that the B family enables, and that deserves explicit framing, is the dual use of these modules as USB audio interfaces. When connected to a host computer, a B variant module presents itself as a class-compliant USB audio device, recognized natively by macOS, Windows, Linux, iOS, and Android without custom drivers. A B1 with microphone support is functionally an entry-level USB audio interface comparable to a Focusrite Scarlett Solo or PreSonus AudioBox iOne. A B2 with two combo jacks is functionally a dual-input USB audio interface comparable to a Focusrite Scarlett 2i2. Both modules can capture audio to the host computer's DAW for recording, using the same audio interfaces that audio engineers expect from dedicated products.

The differentiator from dedicated USB audio interfaces is that Metapedal modules are also useful when not connected to a computer. A traditional USB audio interface is dead weight without a host; a Metapedal module is a standalone effect processor running its own DSP. The same hardware serves both use cases without modification, which means the user gets two products in one. A home studio musician who wants both recording capability and standalone practice gear can buy a single B1 instead of buying separate products for each use. A solo performer who wants live processing and recording from the same gig can use a single B2 to both process audio for the PA and capture it to a laptop for later mixing.

This dual-use capability also enables some interesting hybrid scenarios. A musician playing live can run audio through the module's DSP for live sound while simultaneously sending the dry signals to a laptop for recording. The processed signal goes to the PA; the dry signals go to the DAW for later remixing. This is the dry-plus-processed workflow that high-end audio interfaces support and that recording musicians value, available from a single Metapedal module without requiring any special configuration beyond preset selection.

The USB audio interface capability uses the same class-compliant USB Audio Class 2.0 specification that all major commercial audio interfaces use, so DAW compatibility is universal. The same module also presents a USB MIDI interface for its control surfaces and a USB HID interface for higher-resolution control where applications support it. From the host computer's perspective, a Metapedal B variant looks like any other audio interface plus controller combination, just one that happens to also do its own DSP when the computer is not involved.

The B family combined with the platform's USB host capability also creates an interesting product proposition for the entry-level recording market. A user who wants to start recording at home can buy a single B2 module, which gives them a competent dual-input audio interface for around the same price as a basic Scarlett 2i2 but with the added value of standalone effect processing capability. As they expand into more serious recording work, they can add additional modules to their setup, with each new module integrating cleanly into the Metapedal chain while also being independently useful through its own USB connection if needed.

Every B variant module also includes a microSD card slot, which enables local audio recording without requiring a connected computer. The user inserts a card, assigns a footswitch or other control to start and stop recording, and captures audio directly to the card as standard WAV files. This adds several distinct use cases the platform did not previously support. A practicing musician captures their sessions for later review. A street performer documents their gigs without lugging a laptop. A songwriter records musical ideas as they occur. A loop pedal use case becomes richer because loops can be saved between sessions and combined with new ones to build larger compositions. A sampling use case becomes possible because sample libraries can live on the card, with the module loading and triggering samples through MIDI events or footswitches. The user pulls the card out and reads the recordings on any computer because the filesystem and file formats are universal, with no proprietary software required to access the user's own recordings.

## Modular Control Surfaces: The Tile System

A user who wants more control surface than their modules provide on their own has several options on the Metapedal platform: passive breakouts that add specific kinds of inputs (footswitches, knobs, expression-pedal jacks), additional Controller modules, peripheral hubs that expand the M8 Smart Digital port count. But the most flexible option, and the one that most embodies the platform's open-ecosystem philosophy, is the tile system: a modular control surface specification that lets users build the physical pedalboard interface they actually want.

The pattern is borrowed from Gridfinity, the modular storage system that Zack Freedman designed in 2022 around a 42 millimeter grid pattern. Gridfinity defines a tiny standard (a specific grid spacing, a specific way of attaching storage bins to baseplates) and a community has built thousands of compatible designs around it. The standard is dimensions and a connection pattern; the ecosystem of compatible accessories is essentially limitless because anyone with a 3D printer can produce conforming parts.

Metapedal applies the same pattern to control surfaces. A baseplate, which is a flat metal surface with a grid of mounting points and an embedded set of M8 Smart Digital ports along one edge, accepts modular control tiles. Each tile is a small board with one or a few controls (a single footswitch, a knob, an LED indicator, a small OLED, a quarter-inch expression-pedal jack, a capacitive proximity sensor) plus a standardized physical mounting and electrical connection to the baseplate. The baseplate connects to the parent module's M8 port through a single cable, carrying power and the I2C bus that addresses each tile.

The user picks the baseplate size that fits their pedalboard space (a 2 by 4 baseplate has 8 tile positions; 2 by 8 has 16, and so on), then picks the tiles that match their playing needs. Four footswitches plus two knobs plus an OLED plus an expression-pedal jack might be one common configuration. Eight footswitches with no knobs might be another. The user snaps tiles into the baseplate in whatever arrangement suits their playing style, connects the baseplate to their parent module, and configures the routing graph through the parent module's web UI to map each tile to the parameters they want to control. The control surface fits their specific needs rather than being a fixed layout the manufacturer chose.

A user with an evolving rig can rearrange tiles as their needs change. They start with simple bypass switches, then add expression knobs as they get more comfortable with the platform, then add an OLED for visual feedback. The physical surface evolves with the user. A user with multiple performance contexts can swap entire baseplate populations: a studio-configured baseplate has many knobs and an OLED for detailed editing; the same user's stage-configured baseplate has more footswitches for quick performance control. They swap baseplates when the context changes.

The tile specification is open from day one, which enables third-party manufacturers to produce specialty tiles that the platform itself does not offer. A jazz musician's tile catalog might include a small pressure pad for swell control or a tile that captures a specific gestural input. A metal guitarist's catalog might include a kill switch tile or a tile with a specific footswitch that has a particular tactile feel. The community produces specialty tiles for specialty needs. Hobbyists with 3D printers and basic electronics skills produce one-off tiles for personal use, with the open specification meaning their custom tiles work alongside official ones.

The cost picture is good for users. Individual tiles are cheap because each is a small simple board: a footswitch tile is maybe $5 to $10 retail, a knob tile $4 to $8, an OLED tile $8 to $15. The baseplate itself is $30 to $60 depending on size. A complete 8-tile baseplate configured for typical use might be $60 to $120 retail, which is competitive with dedicated commercial foot controllers in that price range while being vastly more flexible because the user can reconfigure it themselves.

The architectural elegance is that the tile system is just a physical organization for the kinds of passive peripherals the platform already supports. A footswitch tile is functionally identical to one quarter of a passive four-switch breakout. A knob tile is one channel of a knob breakout. The smart-digital protocol on the M8 port handles the signaling; the routing graph in the parent module handles the parameter mapping. The tile system just packages these capabilities in a modular physical form factor that users can rearrange.

The Gridfinity analogy is exactly the right one because it captures the essential insight: define a small standard, let the ecosystem produce the variations. The Metapedal tile specification defines a grid dimension, a connector, an electrical interface, and a catalog of standardized tile types. Everything else is up to the ecosystem.

This is the kind of capability that distinguishes Metapedal from fixed-function commercial products. Commercial foot controllers ship with a particular layout the manufacturer chose, and users either accept it or buy a different product. Metapedal tiles let the user design the layout themselves, with the platform providing the building blocks. The same user can also buy fixed-layout breakouts (the passive four-switch breakout, for instance) for cases where a standardized form factor is preferable, but for users who want to design their own surface, the tile system makes it straightforward.

## Multi-Performer Chains and Cue Audio

While version one focuses on a single performer's pedalboard, the architecture supports multi-performer chains where the gear of multiple musicians is interconnected. This is version two material rather than version one, but it is worth describing the use cases because they motivate some of the architectural decisions even in the version one design.

Multi-performer chains enable cross-instrument control. The drummer's kick triggers the bassist's filter. The bassist's pitch tracker controls the synth player's parameter. The guitarist's volume envelope modulates the drummer's reverb. These kinds of musical interactions are very hard to achieve with traditional pedalboards but become natural with a routing-graph-based architecture.

The bandwidth analysis confirms this is feasible. The inter-module bus has more than enough capacity for the audio and control traffic that multi-performer setups generate, even at high channel counts. The reason multi-performer chains are deferred to version two is architectural complexity rather than bandwidth: routing, addressing, synchronization across multiple instrument sources, and the user interface for configuring a band-wide system are non-trivial design problems best handled after the single-performer case is well-validated.

Cue audio is a related capability that fits the multi-performer architecture. The idea is that audio can be used for communication between performers and between performer-and-system, separately from the music audio the audience hears. A click track plays into every performer's monitor. Voice cues play when the bandleader hits a button signaling a section change. Performance feedback gives subtle information about timing or pitch or set position. Drone references help performers find pitches in unfamiliar keys.

Cue audio uses the same routing infrastructure as music audio but is tagged with a different type, so modules can subscribe to cue streams independently. Each performer's monitor mix combines music audio with cue audio at user-chosen levels. The cue audio never enters the music audio path, so the audience hears only the music while performers hear music plus cues.

The cue system also has a recording application. In a studio context, the producer's talkback microphone is a cue audio source. Each musician's headphone mix is a cue audio sink. The elaborate headphone amp systems that commercial studios use, which can cost thousands of dollars, become an emergent capability of the same modules the band uses for live performance. The same hardware handles both contexts by being configured differently. This extends the platform's reach significantly.

## What Version One Looks Like

A useful exercise is to imagine what the minimum viable version one of Metapedal looks like, both as a specification and as a set of physical modules a user could buy.

Version one covers the core platform: the standard module in the A variant for controllers and the B variant family for audio-capable modules (with B1 and B2 differing in channel count), the inter-module bus over USB-C, the typed-port routing model with single-performer scope, the M8 peripheral ports, the dual-purpose TRRS jack supporting both audio and MIDI, the on-device interface with footswitch and OLED and side controls, the format and latency configuration system, the lease-based leader election, the chain-wide identify command, the microSD card slot on B variants for local audio recording and sample storage, and the USB host-mode support for connecting to laptops and external displays. The dual use of B variant modules as USB audio interfaces is a baseline capability rather than a premium feature, since the architecture supports it without additional cost. The technical details of all of these are in the technical specification.

Version one explicitly does not cover multi-performer chains, cue audio beyond what falls out naturally from the single-performer routing, the routing-module product category for complex topologies, audio-to-control extraction beyond a basic envelope follower, layered or stacked presets, USB 3.2 transport for higher-bandwidth digital audio, or various other ambitious extensions. These are version two material. The discipline of leaving them out of version one is what allows version one to actually ship.

The version one module catalog might include a B1 Processor module with one combo jack input that serves both as a configurable digital effects unit and as an entry-level USB audio interface. A B2 Processor module with two combo jacks for users who need stereo capture or dual-source recording, such as guitar plus vocal. A Controller module that is a simple theremin or capacitive proximity sensor. A Controller module that is a high-quality expression pedal. A rotational foot control that uses angular position rather than rocker-style force, available in spring-loaded (returns to center, good for pitch bends and momentary effects) and free-rotating (stays put, good for volume swells and persistent settings) variants. A peripheral that is a basic envelope follower extractor. A peripheral breakout hub providing multiple M8 ports of each pinout type plus power distribution and additional inter-module bus ports, for users with elaborate peripheral setups. A contact mic breakout peripheral that hosts an array of piezo transducer inputs through TS jacks, with per-channel threshold detection and trigger output, for percussionists who want to make found objects percussive (a cardboard box becomes a kick drum, a metal pipe becomes a tom, a table becomes a snare, with the routing graph treating each contact mic as a typed event source). A passive footswitch breakout that provides four (or eight, in a larger variant) independently mappable footswitches in a small metal enclosure connected through the M8 Smart Digital port, with no firmware or microcontroller of its own, retailing at $40-70 for the four-switch version. A passthrough module that does nothing audio-wise but provides extra control inputs. And a battery pack accessory with replaceable 18650 cells that enables portable operation by providing both 9V pedalboard power and USB-C power output.

With this small catalog and a clear specification, a user can build a real pedalboard that demonstrates the central premise: the theremin and the distortion are separate devices, the theremin controls the distortion, and the same theremin can be remapped to control the delay if the user changes their mind. That is the demo that sells the entire idea, and that is the demo that version one needs to make possible.
