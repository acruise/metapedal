# Metapedal: What It Is and Why You'd Want One

So here is the thing about pedalboards. The pedals you stomp on and the boxes that actually mess with your guitar tone are the same physical objects, and there is no real reason for that to be true. It is just how pedals have always been built. Once you stop and think about it for thirty seconds, you realize the only reason your wah pedal is shaped like a wah pedal is because somebody in 1966 needed to put the rocker and the circuit in the same enclosure because there was no other practical option. Sixty years later, we are still doing it the same way, and it is kind of weird when you actually examine it.

Metapedal is what happens when you stop doing it that way. The control surface, the thing you actually interact with as a musician, gets separated from the audio processor, the thing that does the math on your guitar signal. They become different modules. You can pair them however you want.

That probably sounds abstract, so let me give you a picture. Imagine you have a small box on your pedalboard that does distortion, delay, reverb, all the usual stuff. The amount of distortion is controlled by a sensor on your guitar's headstock, an accelerometer the size of a dime that you stuck on there with double-sided tape. When you tilt your guitar neck up in that classic rock-out move you already do, the distortion gets gnarlier. When you tilt it back down, the distortion settles. You did not have to bend down and twist a knob. You did not have to time a footswitch. The gesture you were already making as a performance move became a tonal control move.

Or imagine you stomp on one footswitch and five things happen at once: distortion goes from on to off, delay changes from a slapback to a long ambient wash, the chorus engages, your boost pedal kicks in, and a noise gate engages so the silence between notes is clean. One stomp, five simultaneous changes, exactly on the beat. The current pedalboard world cannot do this. You would have to tap dance across five footswitches in half a second, and most people miss it most of the time.

That is what Metapedal does. The plumbing between your physical controls and your audio processing becomes flexible. The connections live in software. The hardware is reconfigurable. The gear gets out of your way and lets you actually play.

## The Hardcore Punk Kid

Let me give you a concrete person so you can see how this actually plays out. Picture a sixteen-year-old playing guitar in a hardcore punk band. He plays basements and VFW halls. His band's sets are twenty minutes long, ten songs, no breaks between them, no soundcheck most nights. His pedalboard is small because anything bigger gets stepped on by people slamming around the room. He is jumping off the kick drum and screaming into the singer's mic while still playing guitar. His feet are busy and his hands are busy and there is just not enough bandwidth for the kind of tonal control the music needs.

Here is what is painful about his current setup. His songs have these huge dynamic shifts. A clean palm-muted intro, then it explodes into a wall of distorted noise, then it drops to feedback and silence, then it explodes again. Each of those transitions needs three or four pedals to change state simultaneously. He has to stomp on the distortion, the boost, and the delay all in the half-second between the last clean note and the first heavy note. Most nights, in the chaos, he misses the transition. The song's emotional structure breaks for a few seconds while he gets the board sorted out. Bandmates notice even if the audience does not.

Then there is the song-to-song problem. Different songs want different baseline tones. The fast hardcore songs want a thinner aggressive distortion. The slower mosh-pit songs want fatter and lower-tuned. The drummer counts in the next song while he is trying to remember which pedals to kick on and which to kick off. He gets it wrong sometimes and the first verse plays with the wrong tone.

Metapedal fixes both of these. One footswitch becomes the "chorus transition" trigger. He sets it up at home on his phone the day before. When he stomps that switch, all five tonal changes happen at once and on the beat. A different footswitch cycles through song presets. He stomps it between songs and his whole pedalboard reconfigures for the next song. No more remembering, no more tap dancing, no more missed transitions.

And then there is the gesture stuff. He cannot use his hands for tonal control during a song, because his hands are playing the guitar. But he is already making all kinds of physical gestures: tilting the neck up, leaning into the amp for feedback, kicking the mic stand. Each of those gestures can become a tonal input. The accelerometer on his headstock makes the feedback module's resonance climb when he tilts up. The breath sensor clipped to the mic stand opens up the low-pass on his guitar when he screams. The physical intensity of his performance becomes the tonal intensity of his guitar, automatically, without his having to think about it.

This is not a fancy studio thing. This is exactly the kind of expressive performance move that makes hardcore feel intense and present, and it is impossible with traditional pedals because the controls are bolted to the wrong places.

## The Recording Thing

When his band records, they want to capture both the dry guitar signal and the effected signal. The producer wants flexibility in mixing. But his pedalboard sends only the processed signal to the amp, and capturing the dry signal means adding a splitter or a DI box that he does not own. They end up tracking the processed signal only, committing to effects choices during recording that they would rather keep open.

With Metapedal, the modules can simultaneously send the dry signal and the processed signal to the recording computer over USB on separate channels. The producer gets both. The band can decide later that a particular song wants a different distortion character, run the dry track back through a Metapedal module with a different preset, and get the new sound without anyone having to come back to the studio. The flexibility expands enormously because you are not committing to anything at tracking time.

There is also a microSD card slot on every Metapedal module, which changes the recording game even further. You do not need a laptop or a recording computer at all. Slap a microSD card into the module, configure a footswitch to start and stop recording, and you are capturing audio directly to the card as standard WAV files. The card pulls out, plugs into any computer, and the files are right there. A practicing musician captures every practice session for later review without thinking about it. A street performer documents their gigs without dragging gear they cannot afford to leave unattended. A songwriter records ideas the moment they happen, before they evaporate. You can even use it as a loop pedal that saves your loops between sessions, so a thing you made on Tuesday is still there on Saturday when you want to build on it. The microSD turns every module into its own little recording setup, and the recordings are universal WAV files that work with any computer or any DAW, no proprietary software anywhere in the loop.

## When Stuff Breaks

Hardcore shows are hard on gear. Pedals get stomped, beer gets spilled, cables get yanked. In a traditional pedalboard, one broken pedal in the middle of the chain takes down the whole signal path. The show stops while the guitarist figures out which pedal failed and how to bypass it.

Metapedal modules talk to each other over a software-controlled bus. If a module fails, the system detects the failure and routes around it. The OLED on the next module shows him which one died. He keeps playing through a degraded but functional chain. He figures out the failure between songs. The same flexibility that lets him reconfigure for a different song also lets him reconfigure to skip a broken pedal.

## The Cost Picture

Hardcore is a scene with tight budgets. People share gear, trade pedals, scrape together pedalboards on student wages. A traditional pedal is a fixed-function device. Your distortion pedal does one thing forever. A Metapedal processor module is reconfigurable. The same physical hardware that runs distortion for one player can run delay for another, with no firmware swap or technical anything, just a preset change. A used Metapedal module on the secondhand market is more valuable than a used traditional pedal of equivalent quality because the buyer is buying potential rather than a specific function. Over time this makes building a capable pedalboard cheaper, because each piece of hardware does more.

## A Full Band Story

For a different vision, consider four people in a basement, recording demos. Bass, guitar, vocals, a couple of drum mics. The traditional setup is: a small audio interface that costs four or five hundred dollars, an audio-interface-quality preamp for each input, a laptop running a DAW, plus everyone's individual effects gear, plus separate monitor mixes for each performer if they want to hear themselves through headphones. Easily two or three thousand dollars of gear in a basement that probably should not have that much gear in it.

The Metapedal version: three B-variant modules in the chain, each with two combo jacks for guitar/microphone input. Total cost maybe four hundred bucks. The modules each function as USB audio interfaces, send all the channels to a laptop running a DAW for recording, and simultaneously process the audio in real time for performance monitoring. Each performer plugs their headphones into the TRRS jack on the module nearest them, and that module gives them their own custom monitor mix. The drummer wants their kick mic louder in the monitor; that is just a preset on their module. The singer wants reverb on the monitor but not on the recording; the routing graph in the chain handles that without any complication. The sound engineer, if there is one, sits in the corner with a tablet on the local wifi, looking at a mixer interface that shows all the channels and lets them adjust the mix in real time. The tablet is talking to the chain over the network; the modules are doing the actual audio work.

The total gear cost for the band is comparable to the audio interface alone in the traditional setup, and the band gets multi-channel recording, real-time effects processing, per-performer monitor mixes, and a touch-tablet mixer interface, all from the same hardware. The interesting part is that this is not specialty gear. The same modules that the hardcore kid uses for his solo pedalboard are what the band uses for their multi-channel setup. The difference is just how many modules and how they are configured.

## Wait, It Can Be A Synthesizer Too?

Here is something that might not be obvious from the pedal framing. A Metapedal module does not actually need any audio input to make sounds. The same DSP that processes a guitar signal can synthesize audio from scratch. Feed it MIDI data and it generates the audio. The TRRS jack on every module can carry MIDI in addition to audio, so you can plug a USB MIDI keyboard into a USB hub plugged into a module, or use a small adapter to bring in standard 5-pin MIDI from an older keyboard, or just send MIDI over the inter-module bus from any other module in the chain.

What this means in practice: a Metapedal module is also a synthesizer in a can. A B variant module with a MIDI keyboard plugged in becomes a polyphonic synth that costs the same as the module. Plug headphones into the TRRS jack and you have a complete portable instrument, no laptop required. Plug the output into a PA and you have a stage synth. The DSP capability is plenty for serious synthesis: multiple oscillators, filters, envelopes, LFOs, the whole modular synth toolkit, plus all the effects that the module can run anyway because the same processor handles everything.

The classic vintage instruments are a particularly good fit for the platform. A faithful TR-808 drum machine emulation runs comfortably on one module, with plenty of headroom for additional voices and effects. A TB-303 acid bass synth emulation, complete with the characteristic resonant filter behavior that makes acid house sound like acid house, runs as part of the same module with cycles to spare. The DX7 FM synthesis engine that defined eighties pop runs as an 8-voice polyphonic synth on a single module. A Hammond-organ emulation for gospel and jazz contexts. Various analog synth emulations covering the Minimoog, Pro-One, SH-101, and similar classics. These are all just Faust programs running on the same general-purpose DSP infrastructure.

The interesting thing about electronic music users is that they actually get more capability per module than guitar users do, because the DSP cost of synthesizing drums and bass is genuinely lower than the DSP cost of simulating guitar amplifiers with cabinet impulse responses. A single Metapedal module can run a complete electronic music performance rig: 808 drums, 303 bass, a melodic synth voice for leads, plus vocal processing with effects, all running simultaneously with comfortable cycle budget headroom. The same module that struggles to deliver every aspect of a maximalist guitar rig delivers a complete electronic music rig without strain.

This opens up a bunch of use cases that have nothing to do with guitar. A keyboardist who wants a small reliable synth setup gets one. An electronic music producer who wants 808 and 303 voices for live performance gets them in a portable form factor competitive with the boutique reissue hardware. A hip-hop producer can use the module as a sampler with MIDI control, with a sample library on the microSD card. A modular synth person can use the platform's CV and gate capabilities to integrate with their existing eurorack setup, with each Metapedal module becoming a flexible voice or processing block in the larger system. A guitarist who occasionally needs keyboard or drum parts in their songs can add a small MIDI controller and have those sounds available on demand.

The synthesis is just another DSP program running on the same hardware. Switching a module from "process guitar signal" to "be a synthesizer" is a preset change. The same module on Monday running distortion and reverb for a guitar can on Tuesday be running an 808 drum machine plus a 303 bass voice for the same musician's electronic set. The hardware is general-purpose; the function is software-defined.

## What Makes This Possible

The reason any of this works is that the platform has a few specific architectural commitments that pay dividends throughout. The audio between modules flows over a high-speed USB connection that is fast enough to carry many channels at high quality. The modules know about each other and coordinate their behavior without you having to set up complicated configurations. The software interface for setting things up runs in any web browser on any device you already own, so configuration happens on your phone or laptop with a comfortable touchscreen interface, not on a tiny OLED with three buttons. And the whole thing is open hardware with an open protocol specification, which means a community of musicians and developers can build new modules, new effects, new tile types, new everything, without permission from anyone.

The platform supports four quality tiers depending on what you need. The standard tier is 48 kHz at 24-bit, which is what most professional recording happens at and is plenty for serious music work. There is a higher-quality tier at 96 kHz for audiophile work, and an even higher tier at 192 kHz for mastering and audiophile applications. There is a CD-quality tier at 16-bit for situations where you need lots of channels or long delay buffers and can trade off bit depth for them. You pick the tier that fits your needs.

The modules also come in different flavors. A "controller" module that has no audio processing, just inputs like footswitches and knobs. "Processor" modules with combo jacks for guitar and microphone inputs, doing the actual DSP work. Specialty modules for things like contact microphones, rotational foot controls (which are like expression pedals but use position instead of force, so you actually have fine control over them), and tile-based modular control surfaces where you snap together footswitch tiles and knob tiles and OLED tiles into any layout you want.

## Why Now

Guitar effects have not really changed architecturally in fifty years. There are now neural amp models, fancy convolution reverbs, sophisticated multi-effects units, but the basic shape is still a metal box on the floor with the controls and the audio processing in the same place. The compute is now in the box, but the box is fundamentally the same idea as a Cry Baby from 1968.

Metapedal is what guitar effects look like when you take seriously the idea that the controls and the processing should not have to live in the same box. The compute has gotten so cheap that a tiny microcontroller in a pedal-sized enclosure can outperform a desktop computer from a decade ago at audio work. The connectivity has gotten so cheap that pedals can talk to each other at gigabit speeds over a cable that costs a dollar. The user interface technology has gotten so cheap that every musician already carries a touch-sensitive Linux computer with a high-resolution color display in their pocket. The pieces have been in place for a while; nobody has assembled them into the obvious product because the existing pedal industry has no reason to disrupt itself.

The platform is open hardware and open protocol from day one. Anyone can build a conforming module. Anyone can design new control surfaces, new effects, new everything. The platform survives any particular vendor going out of business because the specifications are public and the software is open source. A musician buying into the platform is buying into something that will be around for the long haul, not into a specific company's product roadmap.

That is the pitch. Pedals that are actually built for how musicians actually play, with the controls separated from the processing so that any control can drive any parameter, with software that handles all the routing so you do not have to memorize tap dances, with multi-channel recording and per-performer monitoring built in, at a cost competitive with traditional pedals, on an open platform that will not lock you into anyone's ecosystem. The pedalboard stops being a thing you fight during a show and becomes a thing that responds to how you are already playing.

If that sounds interesting, the platform exists. Or it will. Or it could.
