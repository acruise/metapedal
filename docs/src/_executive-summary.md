# Metapedal: Executive Summary

## What Metapedal Is

Metapedal is a modular hardware platform for musicians, designed around a single architectural insight: in traditional guitar pedalboards and similar audio gear, the user controls and the audio processing are bolted to the wrong places. Each pedal puts the knobs and footswitches on the same box as the audio circuits, which means the musician's expressive bandwidth is artificially limited to what fits on a pedal-sized control surface. The musician's gestures, breathing, posture, and other natural expression cannot drive audio parameters because the architecture does not let them.

Metapedal separates controls from processing. Each Metapedal module is a pedal-format aluminum enclosure containing a microcontroller, an audio interface, and high-quality DSP, but the user controls are decoupled from the audio circuits through a software-defined routing graph. Any source of control information (a sensor on a guitar's headstock, a foot pedal, a contact microphone on a drum kit, a button on a phone, a knob on a separate controller module) can drive any audio parameter in any module. Modules connect to each other through USB-C cables; the connections carry both audio and control information. The whole system is configured through a web interface that runs on any phone, tablet, or laptop the musician already owns.

The result is gear that costs $50-95 retail per module (competitive with traditional pedals) while delivering capability that traditional gear cannot match at any price: software-defined routing of any control to any parameter, multi-channel recording from any configuration, per-performer monitor mixes for bands, full polyphonic synthesis on a module the size of a stomp box, and an open hardware ecosystem where third parties build mezzanines for specialty use cases the core team would never anticipate.

Metapedal is currently at the architectural design stage. The platform's specification has been developed in substantial detail across a suite of technical documents covering the core architecture, the mezzanine bus contract, the audio analog design, the DSP architecture, the inter-module transport, the peripheral ecosystem, the firmware control plane, and the battery accessory. No commercial operations exist yet; this is the architectural foundation from which a future product could be built.

## How It Works

Each Metapedal module is built on three layers. The motherboard contains the microcontroller (STM32H7 family in v1, delivering 480 MHz Cortex-M7 with substantial DSP capability), the inter-module USB infrastructure, the power management, and the basic peripheral interfaces. The mezzanine board contains the audio codec, analog circuitry, and any specialty silicon (such as the SHARC DSP chip in the BX variant). The enclosure is a standard Hammond 1590B-style aluminum box that pedal builders have used for fifty years.

The motherboard is identical across all variants. The mezzanine determines what the module does: an A variant has no mezzanine and uses simple audio components on the motherboard for controller-only use; B1 and B2 variants have one or two combo jacks for instrument and microphone audio I/O; the BX variant adds a dedicated audio DSP chip for the most demanding algorithms; B16 and B32 variants support multi-channel recording and installation use.

Modules connect through USB-C cables for short runs (typical pedalboard configurations) or ethernet cables for longer runs and multi-performer setups (band on a stage, installation across a venue). The connections carry both audio (multiple channels at professional quality up to 192 kHz, 24-bit) and the control information that lets the platform know which module is which and what audio should flow where.

Configuration happens through a web interface served from a network coprocessor on each module. The musician's phone, tablet, or laptop becomes a routing-graph editor where they can drag connections between sources and destinations, configure each module's specific behavior, save presets, and share configurations with other musicians. The web platform runs on every modern device without requiring downloads or dedicated apps.

The whole architecture is open hardware with an open mezzanine bus specification. The 80-pin connector between motherboard and mezzanine is a documented contract: any conforming mezzanine works with any Metapedal motherboard. The specification covers pin allocation, lease-based I2S resource allocation, identification protocol, mechanical constraints, and compliance testing. Third parties can design and produce mezzanines independently of the platform's vendor team, and those mezzanines work transparently with the rest of the ecosystem.

## What It Enables

Concrete scenarios illustrate the platform's value better than abstract description.

**The hardcore punk kid** plays small venues with a pedalboard that has to keep up with chaotic performances. Songs have dynamic shifts that need three or four pedals to change state simultaneously, and traditional pedalboards make him miss these transitions. With Metapedal, one footswitch becomes the "chorus transition" trigger; all the simultaneous changes happen at once on the beat. An accelerometer on his guitar's headstock makes the feedback respond to how he tilts the neck. The physical gestures he was already making become tonal controls automatically.

**The electronic music busker** runs a complete one-person rig on a single Metapedal module: an 808 drum machine, a 303 bass synth, a melodic synth voice for leads, and vocal processing with effects. The MIDI backing track plays from the module's microSD card. The complete electronic music performance setup that costs $1,500-3,000 in commercial gear runs on hardware that costs less than $100, in a form factor that fits in a backpack.

**The basement band recording demos** uses three or four Metapedal modules instead of a $500 audio interface plus $1,000 of effects gear plus separate monitor mixing. The modules function as USB audio interfaces for recording while simultaneously providing per-performer monitor mixes through their TRRS headphone jacks. The drummer gets a click track; the singer gets reverb on their monitor but not on the recording; the producer gets dry stems plus processed signals for post-production flexibility.

**The same band livestreaming their practices** to Twitch or YouTube to build an audience uses the same Metapedal modules in a different configuration. Five or six modules handle audio capture per musician, per-channel processing, a mix bus to the streaming computer, talkback so the producer can address the band, and per-performer monitor mixes. Total Metapedal infrastructure cost: roughly $540-630, compared to $3,000-5,000 for the equivalent traditional setup with audio interface plus mixer plus outboard processing. The platform's audio-listening capability adds automatic ducking, beat-synced effects, and other production polish that would otherwise require manual mixing during performance. This use case is particularly meaningful because the cost wall has historically kept high-quality streaming out of reach for bands at the basement-practice stage; Metapedal collapses the wall.

**The small theater installation** uses Metapedal modules with ethernet connections to put audio processing in different rooms, with a standard gigabit switch connecting everything. The 100 Mbps ethernet capacity is comfortable for realistic channel counts; the long cable runs (up to 100 meters) handle the geometry that USB cannot. Per-performer monitor mixes, click track distribution, and talkback all work in the same infrastructure as the main signal flow.

**The keyboardist** uses a Metapedal module as a polyphonic synth-in-a-can: plug in a MIDI keyboard, plug in headphones, have a complete portable instrument. The same hardware runs a vintage TR-808 emulation on Monday for an electronic music gig, a guitar amp simulation on Tuesday for a session, and a Hammond organ emulation on Wednesday for a church service. The module is general-purpose; the function is software-defined.

Each of these scenarios is a real use case that traditional gear either cannot serve at all or serves with substantially more cost and complexity. Metapedal's value is that the same platform architecture serves all of them through different configurations of the same underlying modules.

## You Do Not Have to Adopt the Whole Thing

A point worth being explicit about: Metapedal is designed so that its capabilities are orthogonal. You can adopt any individual part of what the platform offers without committing to the rest. Users with beloved existing gear (vintage tube amps, hand-built analog pedals, modular synths, treasured microphones, whatever) keep all of it and add Metapedal capabilities selectively. The platform does not ask you to switch from your current workflow to a Metapedal workflow; it asks you to integrate Metapedal where it provides value to you specifically.

The orthogonal capabilities the platform offers, each independently adoptable:

**Transmission.** A Metapedal module at the end of an existing analog signal chain converts the chain's output to digital and carries it over USB-C or ethernet to wherever it needs to go: the next room, the recording rig, a FOH console at the other end of the venue, a streaming setup. The user's analog signal chain stays entirely intact. Metapedal is the bridge to digital domains that the analog rig cannot reach on its own.

**Recording.** A Metapedal module functions as a USB-class compliant audio interface. A user can plug their existing analog rig into a Metapedal module and record through it, getting multi-channel capture without changing anything upstream. The module replaces a standalone audio interface; the rest of the gear stays the same.

**Routing.** A user with multiple existing rigs (different guitar setups, different mic preamps, different effect chains) can use Metapedal as the routing matrix that connects sources to destinations based on configuration. The existing gear keeps doing what it does; Metapedal handles the "what goes where" question that previously required complex cable patching.

**Control.** A user with existing pedalboards or analog gear can use Metapedal controller modules to drive parameters on whatever gear they already have. Gear with MIDI inputs can be controlled directly. Gear that lacks MIDI can be controlled through specialty peripherals such as small robotic actuators that physically turn knobs or flip switches on analog pedals — taking inspiration from the Useless Machine, where a small mechanism reaches up to flip a switch. This bridges the digital control plane to analog gear that pre-dates digital control.

**Effects processing.** A user can use Metapedal modules as DSP for some or all of their effects, while keeping their amps, preamps, and other gear unchanged. The DSP capability is real and substantial, but the user can also choose to use Metapedal modules with their onboard DSP entirely bypassed, treating them as routing and transmission infrastructure only.

**Analog send and return.** A particularly useful application of the previous point: a B variant module can act as a software-controllable send and return loop for external analog gear. The module's DAC sends audio out to an external analog pedal (or short chain of analog pedals), and the module's ADC receives the analog-processed audio back into the routing graph. The onboard DSP is entirely uninvolved; the module's value in this configuration is the high-quality conversion at each boundary plus the integration with the routing graph. The user's beloved analog pedal effectively lives inside the digital routing graph at any point the user wants, with software-controlled bypass and the option to combine multiple analog chains in parallel or in series with digital effects. This is a particularly powerful combination for users who want analog character with digital flexibility.

**Synthesis.** A user can add a Metapedal module configured as a synth voice to their existing keyboard rig, contributing a new sound source without replacing anything. The synth-in-a-can capability is additive rather than substitutive.

**Monitor mixing.** A band can keep their existing PA and effects gear and use Metapedal modules to add per-performer monitor mixes that their current rig does not support. The mixing happens in the Metapedal modules; the rest of the rig is unchanged.

**Audio-to-control extraction.** A perhaps surprising capability that falls naturally out of the architecture: any Metapedal module with an audio input can analyze the audio it receives and emit control signals into the routing graph based on what it hears. A cheap microphone plugged into a module's TRRS jack or combo jack can drive control signals like detected tempo, beat triggers when a kick drum hits, envelope following on a vocalist, pitch detection on a melodic source, or spectral content from the room. These control signals then drive parameters on other modules in the chain. The traditional separation between audio paths and control paths dissolves, since the platform's architecture treats both as first-class routing-graph citizens. A user could buy a single module just to be the "ears" of their rig, converting what the band is doing acoustically into control signals that shape what the rest of the gear does.

The platform's value compounds as users adopt more of these capabilities, because each capability composes cleanly with the others through the same routing graph and the same module architecture. But the entry point is genuinely low: you can buy one module for one specific purpose, use it alongside your existing gear, and expand only when additional capabilities become useful to you. This decomposable adoption is itself a meaningful differentiator from platforms that ask you to commit to their worldview wholesale.

## What Makes It Different

Several architectural choices distinguish Metapedal from existing audio platforms.

**Software-defined routing as a first-class capability.** Most audio gear has fixed signal paths; the routing graph is determined by which cables you plug into which jacks. Metapedal makes the routing graph software-defined, with the same physical connections supporting whatever topology the musician's current preset specifies. This is the architectural change that enables most of the platform's other capabilities.

**Multi-channel recording integrated with effects processing.** Most pedalboard products do not record; most audio interfaces do not process. Metapedal modules do both simultaneously, with each B variant functioning as a USB-class compliant audio interface while also providing standalone DSP. A band using Metapedal modules for their live rig gets recording capability included; a producer using them for recording gets effects processing included.

**Web-based configuration on existing devices.** Most pedal products require either tiny LCD screens with limited UI, smartphone apps with proprietary protocols, or proprietary editor software on a laptop. Metapedal serves a standard web interface from each module that runs on any phone, tablet, or laptop the musician already owns. No app downloads, no proprietary protocols, no platform lock-in.

**Open hardware with open mezzanine bus.** Most pedal and audio gear is closed: the manufacturer specifies what variants exist, and that is what users can buy. Metapedal publishes the mezzanine bus specification openly so that third parties can build conforming mezzanines for use cases the core team does not address. The ecosystem grows beyond what any single vendor would prioritize, and users benefit from a broader catalog of options than a closed platform could provide.

**The breadth of use cases on shared infrastructure.** Most audio gear is purpose-built for specific use cases (a pedal does effects; an interface does recording; a synth does synthesis). Metapedal modules serve all these use cases through software configuration of the same hardware. A musician buying Metapedal gear can use it as a pedalboard today and a recording rig tomorrow without buying additional equipment.

The combination is the differentiator. Each individual capability exists somewhere in the audio gear market, but no existing platform combines them all on shared hardware at consumer pedal price points with an open ecosystem. Anyone building a competing product has to choose between matching one of these dimensions and matching all of them; the architecture that matches all of them is what Metapedal contributes.

## Current Status and Document Map

Metapedal is currently architectural design, not a shipping product. The platform's specification has been developed in detail across a suite of documents, but no hardware has been built, no firmware has been written for production use, and no commercial operations exist. The founder is a software engineer working on this design as a personal architectural project alongside a day job.

The architectural work is real and substantial; the commercial work has not started. If you are evaluating the platform, the right framing is "well-specified open hardware platform design awaiting execution" rather than "commercial product you can buy today."

The document set that captures the platform's specification:

**User-facing and strategic materials.** The vision document covers what Metapedal does for musicians, with detailed use cases and the architectural principles that motivate the design. The casual pitch is an informal introduction suitable for sharing with musicians who might be interested. The strategy document covers business positioning, voice and branding strategy, and the strategic choices the platform makes. The investor pitch is the version of the platform's case calibrated for institutional investors at the seed stage.

**Technical specification set.** The core technical document covers the standard module architecture, motherboard functional blocks, mezzanine architecture overview, and the platform's electrical and packaging considerations. The mezzanine bus specification is the contract third parties build mezzanines against, including pin allocation, lease-based I2S protocol, and mechanical constraints. The audio analog design document covers the B variant analog circuitry: quarter-inch path, XLR microphone support, phantom power, galvanic isolation. The DSP document covers the platform's DSP architecture, the Faust language choice, the format and latency configuration matrix, and detailed DSP budget analysis. The transport document covers inter-module communication: connectors, cable testing, the ethernet variant, latency analysis, topology choices, bus protocol. The peripherals document covers the peripheral ecosystem including the tile-based control surface system, the rotational foot control, and various breakouts. The web platform document covers the presentation protocol, presets, and the streaming router. The battery accessory document covers the 18650 backpack specification.

**Open questions and future considerations.** The TODO document captures architectural questions still open, design decisions deferred to v1.x, and the platform's "v2 is an open question" framing that explicitly defers thinking about v2 until v1 demonstrates real value.

Anyone approaching Metapedal can use this document set as follows. If you are a musician curious about what the platform does, read the vision document and the casual pitch. If you are an engineer interested in the architecture, read the core technical document and then whichever specialized technical document covers your specific concern. If you are an EE considering building a mezzanine, read the mezzanine bus specification. If you are interested in the platform's business positioning or considering supporting commercialization, read the strategy document and the investor pitch with the understanding that the platform is at architectural design stage rather than commercial operation.

The next concrete steps for the platform depend on what direction the project takes. Continuing as architectural design produces more documents and explores design questions further. Building toward commercialization requires finding hardware cofounders, building physical prototypes, and the social and financial work that the founder is currently constrained against. Some intermediate paths exist: publishing the document set as portfolio work to attract collaborators, engaging with the hackspace community to validate the design with EEs, and small experiments that test specific architectural assumptions with minimal investment.

The architectural foundation is in place. What happens next depends on what the platform's stakeholders decide to do with it.
