# Metapedal Audio Analog Design

This document covers the analog circuit design for Metapedal B variant mezzanines and related mezzanine categories. The audience is engineers designing audio mezzanines (B variants, specialty audio I/O mezzanines, tube preamps, vintage-impedance variants) who need to understand the analog signal path requirements.

For the mezzanine bus contract that this analog work plugs into, see `tech-04-mezzanine-bus.md`.

For the digital signal path and DSP architecture, see `tech-03-dsp.md`.

## The Quarter-Inch Audio Path: Levels, Impedance, and Protection

The analog circuitry around the quarter-inch input and output jacks on the B variant deserves its own discussion because guitar and bass signals have specific characteristics that the input stage must accommodate, and getting these wrong produces audible problems that no amount of clever DSP can fix downstream.

Electric guitar pickups produce a wide range of signal levels. A typical electric guitar outputs between 100 millivolts and 1 volt during normal playing, with an average somewhere around 150 to 300 millivolts for moderate playing on a typical pickup. Low-output single-coil pickups in vintage Stratocasters produce maybe 100 millivolts, while hot humbuckers can reach 600 to 800 millivolts in normal playing and some boutique high-output pickups go higher still. But the average is not the design constraint; the peaks are. The first transient peak of a strongly picked chord can reach about 1.5 volts before settling down to the steady-state level a moment later. Aggressive playing on hot pickups can push transient peaks even higher.

Bass guitar signals are similar in voltage range to electric guitar, but active basses with onboard preamps can produce higher levels, sometimes 2 to 3 volts peak in worst-case transients. The lower fundamental frequencies of bass do not change the voltage requirements, just the frequency response considerations.

So the realistic input voltage range that the Metapedal quarter-inch input must handle cleanly spans from a few millivolts at the quiet end, for sensitive fingerpicking on a low-output pickup, up to about 2 volts peak for worst-case transients on hot pickups or active basses played aggressively. The input stage needs to be linear across this entire range without clipping at the top or being lost in noise at the bottom. This is a dynamic range of around sixty decibels for the signal itself, comfortably within what twenty-four-bit audio handles.

The impedance situation is the other half of the input design. Passive guitar pickups have output impedances in the 5 to 20 kilohm range at audio frequencies, but the impedance is highly frequency-dependent because pickups are inductors with significant inductance, typically 3 to 10 henries. The pickup expects to drive an input impedance much higher than its own output impedance, conventionally 1 megohm or more. If the input impedance is too low, it loads the pickup and rolls off the high end audibly. The classic symptom of a low-impedance input is that the guitar sounds dull and lifeless. Active pickups and active basses have low output impedances of a few hundred ohms, so they do not care about input impedance loading, but the input stage has to support the demanding passive case.

This sets specific requirements for the Metapedal quarter-inch input. The input impedance must be at least 1 megohm and ideally higher, perhaps 2 to 10 megohms, to accommodate any passive pickup without coloring the tone. The input voltage range must handle peaks up to at least 2 volts cleanly, with some margin for the user with hot pickups and aggressive playing style. The noise floor must be low enough that quiet playing at a few millivolts is still clean and audible above the inherent noise of the analog circuit.

The implementation is a buffer amplifier feeding the codec ADC. The buffer presents the high input impedance to the guitar and converts the high-impedance signal to a low-impedance signal that the ADC can sample without loading concerns. A JFET-input op-amp like the OPA1641 from Texas Instruments or similar audio-grade JFET op-amps from Analog Devices or other vendors handles this well, with the JFET providing megohm-range input impedance naturally and good low-noise performance for the high-impedance source. These op-amps cost a few dollars in the relevant package and quality grades, which is reasonable for an audio-grade input.

The gain of the buffer is set so that the maximum expected input voltage maps to slightly below the maximum input of the ADC, with some headroom for occasional peaks that exceed the nominal design maximum. Anything above the design maximum clips digitally at the ADC, which is acceptable for most playing but could be a problem for aggressive players with hot pickups. The mitigation is a software-adjustable input gain that lets the user trim for their specific instrument, set once when they first plug in a particular guitar and remembered per instrument profile. The software gain works with the codec's built-in programmable gain amplifier where available, or by digital scaling after the ADC if the codec does not have a PGA.

The protection circuit on the input handles a few specific abuse cases. Plugging the cable in or out while signal is present causes transient voltages from contact making and breaking. A user might accidentally plug something other than a guitar into the input, like a speaker output or a power supply, which would feed potentially high voltages into the buffer. The protection is typically a pair of clamping diodes between the input and the supply rails, with a series resistor limiting peak current into the diodes. These components cost a few cents and protect the expensive op-amp and codec chips from being damaged by abuse. The protection has to be designed carefully to not affect the audio path under normal operation, which means using fast diodes with low capacitance and a series resistor small enough to not interact with the input impedance.

The output side has different but related requirements. The quarter-inch output needs to drive into whatever the user plugs it into, which could be a guitar amp expecting a high-impedance instrument-level signal, a powered speaker expecting a line-level signal, or a recording interface input. Guitar amp inputs are typically 1 megohm input impedance, the same as a guitar expects. Line-level inputs are typically 10 kilohm or lower, designed for low-impedance signals at higher levels.

The Metapedal output is designed to drive both kinds of destinations cleanly. The output impedance is low, less than 100 ohms, so it can drive line-level destinations without losing significant signal to the impedance divider formed by the output impedance and the load. The output level is software-controllable, with two main modes: instrument level around 200 to 400 millivolts for driving a guitar amp where the amp expects a guitar-like signal, and line level around 1 to 2 volts for driving recording interfaces and powered speakers. The user selects the appropriate mode based on what is plugged into the output.

The output stage is a standard audio op-amp configured as a buffer or low-gain amplifier, driving the quarter-inch jack through a small output coupling capacitor to block any DC component. Output protection is built into modern audio op-amps in the form of current limiting and short-circuit protection, so a user shorting the output to ground through a faulty cable or wrong connection does not damage anything. The op-amp choice for the output is less demanding than for the input because the source impedance is low, but a clean audio op-amp like the OPA1612 or similar is still the right choice for the audio quality story.

DC offset handling matters for both input and output. Some pickups, particularly active pickups with onboard preamps, can produce small DC offsets in addition to the audio signal. The input stage uses AC coupling, either through a capacitor before the buffer or through the codec's built-in high-pass filter, to reject DC and pass only the audio. The output stage similarly AC-couples through a capacitor to block any DC component that the codec might produce. The cutoff frequency of the high-pass filtering is well below the audible range, perhaps 10 to 20 hertz, to preserve the low end of bass signals without coloration. The actual coupling capacitor value depends on the load impedance, with larger capacitors needed for lower-impedance loads to maintain the low cutoff frequency.

Ground loops and electromagnetic interference are perennial concerns in guitar signal chains, and the high-impedance nature of the guitar signal makes the Metapedal input particularly susceptible. The PCB layout uses careful practices: short signal paths in the high-impedance section between the input jack and the buffer, ground plane separation between analog and digital sections of the board, star-grounding of the analog ground references back to a single point, and shielded routing for the most sensitive traces. Standard audio PCB design practice, but worth being explicit about because it is easy to get wrong and the symptoms of getting it wrong are hum, hiss, and noise that no DSP can remove.

Four-layer construction is fine for both the motherboard and the mezzanines. The standard four-layer stackup of signal-ground-power-signal gives a continuous ground plane underneath every trace, clean return paths for differential signals, and adequate power distribution through the power plane. Both the digital signals on the motherboard (USB 2.0 at 480 megabits per second, SDMMC, I-squared-S audio, and various control buses) and the analog audio paths on the mezzanines work fine on four-layer boards with reasonable layout discipline. The existence proof here is the entire consumer audio market: $30 portable digital audio players, cheap USB audio interfaces, and countless other audio products ship on four-layer boards routinely with perfectly acceptable noise performance. Metapedal is not trying to compete with high-end studio gear that genuinely demands six-layer construction for its very low noise floors; the audio quality target is consumer to low-end pro audio, which is comfortably within what four-layer construction delivers.

That said, the engineering effort spent on the analog design should scale with what the variant actually does. The A variant's onboard audio circuitry is genuinely cheap territory: a DAC driving a headphone output, a basic microphone preamp into the H7's built-in ADC, and a TRRS jack, all populated directly on the motherboard rather than on a separate mezzanine. The use cases tolerate modest analog performance because the headphone output drives nearby user-monitor headphones and the microphone input is read for control extraction rather than for recording quality. Cheap parts, basic layout, perhaps under five dollars in components. No special concerns.

The B mezzanines are where the analog engineering effort genuinely matters. The quarter-inch input takes guitar pickup signals across sixty decibels of dynamic range, and any noise the input stage adds shows up in every subsequent stage of the chain. The microphone preamp on the combo jack handles signals down to a few millivolts, where the preamp's own noise floor determines what users actually hear. The output stage drives PA systems and recording interfaces where coloration of the signal matters. The B variants are also the configuration that gets compared to commercial USB audio interfaces like the Scarlett line, so the analog quality target is higher specifically because that comparison will happen. The B1 and B2 mezzanines justify more careful component selection (the codec choice matters more, the analog op-amps in the input stage matter more, the layout near the preamp matters more) and more design iteration. They cost two or three times what the A variant's onboard audio circuitry costs in BOM and deserve correspondingly more engineering attention.

This is a clean way to think about where the analog engineering effort gets spent. The motherboard is digital and routine, plus the A variant's basic onboard audio components on A builds. The B mezzanines are where the substantial analog engineering effort lives, and they deserve that effort because they are where the platform's audio quality story actually gets evaluated by users.

The motherboard-as-digital-only architecture with the heavy analog work isolated to the B mezzanines is still nice to have for architectural cleanliness and for separation between digital noise sources and serious analog circuitry. The Bluetooth audio module precedent demonstrates this pattern works well for audio products. The A variant's onboard audio components are simple enough that mixing them onto the motherboard does not compromise the broader pattern; they tolerate the modest digital noise environment because their use cases tolerate modest analog performance.

Each mezzanine handles its own analog signal integrity on its own PCB with its own ground plane. The two ground planes meet at the board-to-board connector, with the connection inductance and physical separation providing inherent isolation between the digital and analog domains. The mezzanine is a small four-layer board with the analog components and the analog connectors.

The headphone output considerations are similarly modest. Headphones have a wide range of impedances, from 16 ohms for cheap consumer earbuds to 600 ohms for studio reference headphones. The headphone amplifier on the mezzanine handles most of this range natively. For driving high-impedance studio headphones at full level, the mezzanine might need a higher supply rail than the standard 3.3 volts, perhaps 5 volts. The mezzanine includes whatever local power conversion it needs.

## XLR Microphone Support

A natural extension of the quarter-inch audio path is support for performance microphones through XLR connectors. The standard mechanism in modern audio interfaces is the combo jack, which combines a female XLR connector with a female quarter-inch TRS jack in a single physical housing. The Neutrik NCJ series is the most common implementation. The XLR contacts and the quarter-inch contacts are independent inside the housing, going to separate sets of solder pins on the back. When the user plugs in an XLR cable, the XLR contacts engage and the quarter-inch contacts are unused. When the user plugs in a quarter-inch plug, the quarter-inch contacts engage through the center of the XLR opening and the XLR contacts are unused.

The combo jack costs more than a plain quarter-inch jack, perhaps three to five dollars in production quantities compared to under a dollar for the plain jack. The cost premium is meaningful for budget-conscious products but defensible if the capability adds real value. The mechanical convergence is mostly a packaging convenience; the signals going to the rest of the circuitry still need to be handled differently because microphone signals and instrument signals are quite different electrically.

The signal-level requirements for microphone support are dramatically different from those for instruments. A typical dynamic microphone outputs perhaps 1 to 5 millivolts at conversational speaking levels, with louder sources reaching maybe 50 millivolts on a strongly miked snare drum or a screamed vocal. Condenser microphones produce more, perhaps 10 to 50 millivolts at typical levels, but still much less than a guitar. The microphone input path needs significantly more gain than the instrument input path, somewhere around 40 to 60 decibels of gain to bring microphone signals up to the level the codec ADC expects.

The gain is typically provided by a dedicated microphone preamplifier, a specialized op-amp circuit designed for high gain with very low noise. Integrated microphone preamp chips like the Texas Instruments INA163, the That Corporation 1510, or similar parts cost a few dollars and provide programmable gain across the wide range microphones need. The codec might include a built-in microphone preamp, but those internal preamps are typically sized for headset microphones rather than studio-grade signals. A dedicated external preamp for the combo jack gives better noise performance and a more flexible gain range.

The impedance situation is different from guitars. Microphones expect to drive low input impedances, typically 1 to 2 kilohms, which is the opposite of the megohm input that guitar pickups need. The conventional microphone input impedance of around 1500 to 2400 ohms balanced is well-understood and gives good results across the range of microphones users might plug in.

The balanced signaling is the third major difference. XLR microphone cables carry balanced signals, with the audio appearing as the difference between two conductors and a third conductor for shield ground. The balanced format provides excellent noise rejection over long cable runs, which matters because microphones are often placed far from the audio equipment they connect to. The microphone preamp chips natively handle the differential-to-single-ended conversion that the codec needs.

Phantom power is where this gets genuinely complicated. Condenser microphones, including most modern studio microphones, need power to operate. They use a specific scheme called phantom power, where forty-eight volts of DC is applied between the two signal conductors and the shield conductor of the XLR cable, biased so that no DC appears across the signal conductors themselves. The microphone takes the DC power off the cable to run its internal electronics, while the audio signal passes through normally as the AC difference between the conductors. Dynamic microphones do not need phantom power and ignore it when present; ribbon microphones can actually be damaged by phantom power applied to them.

Providing phantom power means generating forty-eight volts of clean DC and applying it through resistors to the XLR signal lines. The voltage is high enough that it cannot come directly from any normal logic-level supply rail; the module needs a small boost converter to step up from the available rails to forty-eight volts. The total cost is perhaps two to five dollars in production quantities for the boost converter chip, inductor, capacitors, and application resistors.

The power budget for phantom power is real but manageable. Forty-eight volts at the typical microphone current of 5 to 10 milliamps is 240 to 480 milliwatts of power that the module has to deliver to the microphone. Accounting for the boost converter efficiency of around eighty-five percent, the power drawn from the underlying five-volt rail is somewhere around 550 to 600 milliwatts when phantom is active, or about 110 to 120 milliamps at five volts. This is a meaningful chunk of the legacy USB 2.0 budget of 500 milliamps, but it fits comfortably alongside the rest of a module's power consumption.

The proof of this practicality is in the commercial market. Audio interfaces like the Focusrite Scarlett 2i2, the PreSonus AudioBox, the Behringer UMC22, and countless others run from USB 2.0 power and provide forty-eight volts of phantom power to one or two microphone inputs simultaneously, while also running their codec, their controller, their LEDs, and their analog circuits. If they can do all of this within the legacy USB 2.0 budget, then a Metapedal B variant with microphone support has no fundamental power obstacle either. The USB Power Delivery negotiation that USB-C provides is a nice-to-have for scenarios that need more power, like running multiple modules in a chain or driving heavy DSP loads, but the basic case of one module with phantom-powered microphone fits within the legacy budget. Users plugging into older laptops or USB hubs without PD support still get full functionality.

The battery backpack situation looks favorable as well. A single 18650 cell at 3000 milliamp-hours of nominal capacity provides enough energy to run a B variant module with active microphone for several tens of hours of continuous use, which is plenty for any single performance and most multi-performance days. The phantom power load is small enough that it does not dramatically change the runtime calculation compared to instrument-only use. The portable performance use case with phantom-powered microphone support is fully practical, not a marginal capability.

Phantom power must be user-controllable. The default state is off, because not all microphones need it and some can be damaged by it. The user enables phantom power through the module's configuration when they connect a microphone that needs it. The module should also detect the presence of an XLR cable and only enable phantom power when something is actually plugged in, to avoid wasting power and potentially damaging things by running phantom power into a disconnected jack.

There is a safety concern worth being explicit about. Plugging or unplugging an XLR cable with phantom power active can create a transient that briefly puts the forty-eight-volt rail directly across the signal lines, which can damage the input circuitry or the microphone. The protection is to require the user to disable phantom power before connecting or disconnecting microphones, or to use a sequencing circuit that mutes the input during cable changes. Most professional audio equipment handles this by requiring user discipline with a warning in the documentation. A more polished implementation uses detection circuits to mute the input automatically when cable insertion is detected.

The total cost impact of adding microphone support to a B variant is meaningful but bounded. The combo jack adds three to five dollars. The microphone preamp adds two to three dollars. The phantom power boost converter and its application components add two to three dollars, with the heat dissipation being modest enough that no special thermal management is needed. The protection and detection circuitry adds a dollar or so. The bill of materials goes up perhaps eight to twelve dollars on top of the existing quarter-inch input path, which is meaningful enough to warrant a real product decision about whether microphone support is mandatory on every B variant or whether it is a separately variant or peripheral, but not so dramatic that the question is obvious.

The all-in-one-B-variant approach includes microphone support in every B variant module. The user gets a versatile audio interface that handles both instrument and microphone inputs through the combo jack. The cost is added to every B variant, which raises the price floor by ten or fifteen dollars. Users who never use microphones pay for the capability they do not use.

The separate-variant approach creates a C variant that adds microphone support on top of the B variant capabilities. The user buys the B variant for instrument-only use and the C variant for use cases that include microphones. The variant proliferation is real product complexity but it lets price-conscious users avoid the microphone-related costs.

The board-option approach has the standard B variant PCB designed with the combo jack hardware and the microphone path components present, but with the microphone preamp and phantom power supply as components that some manufacturers populate and others do not. The same PCB serves both markets, with the cost difference captured in the bill of materials at manufacturing time. This is consistent with how the A and B variants already work, just adding another dimension to the variant matrix.

The peripheral approach puts microphone support in a separate accessory that connects through the Smart Digital M8 port. The peripheral contains its own preamp, its own phantom power supply, and its own ADC, digitizing the microphone signal locally and sending it to the module as a digital stream. This fits the platform's pattern of pushing specialized capabilities into peripherals, but the cost ends up roughly the same as including the capability in the module itself; the savings come from optionality rather than from sharing components.

The right answer probably depends on use case research that has not yet been done. For solo performers in portable mode, microphone support is genuinely valuable. For guitarists processing only instrument signals, it is dead weight. For the version one specification, the most defensible position is probably to define the combo jack capability as part of the B variant specification, with the microphone preamp and phantom power supply being explicitly optional components that conforming manufacturers may or may not populate. This gives the ecosystem flexibility to ship both microphone-capable and instrument-only B variant modules, with the user picking the right one for their needs.

For users who already have a Metapedal module without microphone support and want to add it later, third-party XLR-to-quarter-inch adapter cables exist on the market, with built-in preamplifiers that step microphone signals up to instrument levels. These adapters typically have their own battery for the preamp power, and they do not provide phantom power, so they work only with dynamic microphones. This is a workable path for occasional microphone use without requiring the module itself to have full XLR support, though it is not as clean as integrated support.

The platform's USB host capability also offers an alternative path for microphone input. A USB microphone plugged into the module's USB-C port works directly if the module has driver support for the USB Audio Class. This works for any modern USB microphone, of which many are available in the consumer market at reasonable prices. The user gets microphone input without needing XLR support in the module at all. The downside is that USB microphones tend to be designed for podcasting and casual use rather than for professional audio capture, so the quality at the high end of the microphone market is not as good as what XLR connectivity provides.

A further question worth pinning down is whether the specification names a specific microcontroller part number or just specifies the capabilities a conforming part must have. For version one of the specification, the right move is probably to name a specific part, in the spirit of how the Raspberry Pi specifies its Broadcom system on chip. This guarantees firmware portability across manufacturers and tightens the developer experience. The constraint can be relaxed in later versions once the ecosystem has stabilized.

## Galvanic Isolation and Signal Coupling

Long cables can act as antennas and pick up stray voltages that may show up as noise on connected electronics. Different industries handle this through different isolation techniques depending on the signal characteristics. Worth being explicit about how Metapedal handles isolation across its various interfaces.

Ethernet uses pulse transformers, commonly called magnetics in industry shorthand, that pass the AC signal content while blocking DC and providing common-mode rejection. The transformer's electromagnetic coupling between primary and secondary windings means there is no electrical connection between them, so DC voltages and low-frequency ground potential differences cannot cross. The transformers are sized for the megahertz-range signals of Ethernet, with bandwidths optimized for that signal spectrum. The pulse transformer approach works well for Ethernet because the signal is differential and the data encoding tolerates the high-pass nature of transformer coupling.

For audio, transformers can also work but have real tradeoffs. Audio transformers exist, ranging from cheap PA-gear parts to expensive Jensen and Lundahl transformers used in high-end recording equipment. They provide excellent galvanic isolation, common-mode rejection, and signal balancing. But they have limitations: frequency response is limited especially at the low end where the transformer's inductance must be large to pass low frequencies; they add cost and weight; they can saturate at high signal levels; and they introduce some distortion and frequency response coloration that audio engineers either love or hate depending on context. Modern professional audio has largely replaced transformers with active electronically-balanced circuits using line driver and receiver chips like the That Corporation 1200 series, which give excellent performance at much lower cost than transformers, with no frequency response limitations and no saturation concerns. The tradeoff is that active balancing does not provide galvanic isolation, so DC and low-frequency ground loop currents can still flow between connected equipment.

Optoisolators couple signals across a galvanic boundary using an LED on one side and a photodetector on the other, with light carrying the signal across the gap. The galvanic isolation is excellent but the LED-photodetector pair has nonlinearity that makes optoisolators tricky for analog audio. The main audio use case for optoisolators is digital signals like MIDI, where the binary nature of the signal makes the nonlinearity irrelevant. The MIDI specification explicitly requires optical isolation in the receiver, and the Metapedal MIDI interface uses an optocoupler following this standard pattern. Optoisolators also appear in some specialized analog audio applications like vintage LA-2A-style compressors where the LED-photoresistor characteristic is part of the desired sound, but for general analog signal coupling they are not the right choice.

For analog audio isolation when it is genuinely needed, modern equipment typically uses isolated power supplies feeding the analog section with signals crossing the isolation boundary through transformers or specialized isolation amplifiers like the TI ISO124 that use internal modulation and demodulation to cross the isolation barrier without the limitations of direct optical coupling. These parts are more expensive than typical opamps but they handle the cases where galvanic isolation of analog audio is genuinely required.

For Metapedal, the question of which interfaces need galvanic isolation has clear answers for the most part. The quarter-inch and combo jacks connect to instruments and microphones that are typically nearby and not subject to ground loop issues; no isolation needed. The TRRS jack connects to headphones which are by definition floating from any ground reference; no isolation needed. The MIDI interface uses optical isolation as required by the standard. The USB-C inter-module bus relies on USB Power Delivery's existing isolation mechanisms for power negotiation; no additional isolation is needed for short module-to-module connections. The microSD card has no isolation concerns because it is a local component.

The interface where galvanic isolation might become important is the inter-module bus when modules are connected over longer distances, particularly the version-two multi-performer scenario where modules from different performers' rigs are connected across a stage. Ground loops between rigs at different stage positions are a real concern that USB isolators address well. USB hubs and isolators exist as commercial products that provide galvanic isolation between USB devices and hosts, and the Metapedal architecture can rely on these external accessories when needed rather than building isolation into every standard module. This fits the platform's pattern of pushing specialized capabilities into peripherals rather than building them into every module. For version-one single-performer use, the inter-module connections are short enough that isolation is not needed and the user has no equipment to buy beyond the modules and cables themselves.

So the bottom line on isolation for Metapedal is that most interfaces do not need galvanic isolation in version one, the one that genuinely requires it (MIDI) uses optical isolation following the standard, and the case where it might become important (long-distance inter-module connections in multi-performer rigs) can be handled by external USB isolators when needed. The platform does not need built-in audio transformers or isolation amplifiers for the standard use cases, which keeps the bill of materials clean.
